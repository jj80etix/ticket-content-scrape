# BookMyShow: Emerging touring models making India an ‘anchor market’

## Summary

BookMyShow CEO Ashish Hemrajani says India is becoming a touring "anchor market" as international artists increasingly book three-to-five city routes rather than one-off shows, citing recent sellouts for Fred Again and Guns N' Roses (including a new stop in Guwahati). He says data analytics are revealing under-served fan demand in genres like hip hop and K-pop, though logistics — a road network unsuited to equipment haulage and reliance on air freight — remain structural constraints.

### Key Points
- India now has "at least five markets" viable for multi-city tour routing, per BookMyShow's CEO.
- Guns N' Roses sold out shows in Bengaluru and the newly-emerging Guwahati market (~55M-person catchment).
- Artists are using platform analytics to identify underserved demand (hip hop, K-pop cited).
- Structural constraints: road infrastructure ill-suited for equipment, heavy reliance on air freight.

## BookMyShow: Emerging touring models making India an ‘anchor market’

The company's CEO Ashish Hemrajani said recent tour announcements show the country is rapidly developing a profitable model of three-to-five city touring routes

![](https://www.iqmagazine.com/wp-content/smush-webp/2026/07/Ed-Sheeran_Mathematics-Tour_credit_-BookMyshow-scaled.jpg.webp)

Ed Sheeran in India © BookMyShow

Recent Indian tours by international acts will have a “ripple effect” through the country’s touring market, BookMyShow CEO Ashish Hemrajani has said, while noting the country is rapidly developing a profitable model of three-to-five city routes.

“India is becoming an anchor market, not just a one stop,” the CEO said on a recent appearance on the Bob Lefsetz podcast, adding that the country now has “at least five markets where you can do a tour”.

“When bands are coming to India, they’re doing least three to five cities within India and that’s when they start making money, and if you come as a one off and one and done, then you will not,” he added.

This follows a flurry of recent tour announcements by international artists, including [Fred Again](https://www.iqmagazine.com/2026/06/fred-again-announces-debut-india-tour/) and [Gun N’ Roses](https://www.iqmagazine.com/2026/06/india-eyes-diversified-touring-circuit-with-guwahati-guns-n-roses-show/), with the latter set to play a milestone show in Guwahati, an as-yet less established touring stop within the national market.

Of the band’s Guwahati concert and the growing emergence of the city as a market, Hemrajani said: “Guns N’ Roses have come back and we’ve sold massive sellout shows in Bengaluru and Guwahati. Guwahati is a market in the northeast, about a two hour flight from Kolkata and which caters to 55 million people in the northeast of the country.

“Guns N’Roses have sold out there. You will see that ripple effect where there’s a huge demand for acts that come back and then they can do multiple cities and multiple tours.”

> “India is becoming an anchor market”

He said, however, that while infrastructure is “catching up”, the touring market in the country is still grappling with “two or three structural issues,” including a road network ill-suited to equipment haulage and the resulting reliance on air freight for tours.

Explaining this momentum behind international acts in the country, the CEO said data analytics is revealing a level of fan demand in the country across a range of genres that is not yet fully served by a corresponding amount of international tours, naming hip hop and K-Pop as particular hotspots for the country.

“Artists are smart; they’re seeing that data,” Hemrajani said. “They have analytics available to them on global platforms and they’re seeing where the demand is coming from. A lot of them, their fans or their listeners are from India \[…\] and they want to come and play the market.”

He also pointed to gradual progress in touring infrastructure, which he said has been what has held the Indian market back from harnessing the fan demand in the country, predicting that “three to four years” may be needed to close this gap.

“I think what’s limiting us today is that we don’t have the infrastructure to be able to cater to a 365 days, 12 months of the year touring market,” he said, adding:”If they are touring in other parts of the world and they want to come, we can’t give them a stadium, we can’t give them an arena, we cannot play in certain weather patterns or weather zones in the country. And once we address that over the next three to four years, we should be in a better place.”

Despite this, Hemrajani said improvements in public infrastructure, if perhaps not industry-specific touring infrastructure, bodes well for the sector in terms of growing logistical efficiency and fan mobility.

“In the last again five or six years, there’s a huge catch up that’s taking place. As we speak today, Bombay has close to eleven metro lines that are being built. Three have been launched. Every six months a new metro line is being launched,” he said, noting that “the same is happening in Delhi, the same is happening in Bangalore and Pune.”
