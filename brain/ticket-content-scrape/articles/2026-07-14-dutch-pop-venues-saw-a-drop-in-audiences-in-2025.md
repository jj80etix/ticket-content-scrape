# Dutch pop venues saw a drop in audiences in 2025

## Summary

A report from the Association of Dutch Pop and Festivals (VNPF) found that its 55 affiliated Dutch music venues saw total audiences drop 5% in 2025 to 6.3 million guests, driven mainly by a 14% decline in club-night attendance, even as standalone concerts grew. Revenue rose 2% to €247m but expenditure rose faster (2.3%, to €251m), with more than half of venues ending the year in the red. VNPF said rising fixed costs and insufficient municipal subsidy increases are squeezing venues' ability to program diverse and emerging talent.

### Key Points
- Affiliated venues programmed 29,523 performances for 6.3 million guests in 2025, down 5% year-on-year (still 3% above 2023).
- Club-night attendance fell 14%; 60% of venues reported declining club nights while concerts drew bigger audiences.
- Revenue grew 2% to €247m but costs grew 2.3% to €251m, and more than half of venues posted a negative financial result.
- New four-year municipal subsidy cycle left some venues with less funding in 2025 than 2024, despite rising fixed costs.

### Highlights
- 5% audience decline (6.3 million guests) across 55 Dutch venues, with club nights down 14% even as concert attendance grew.
- More than half of surveyed venues posted a negative financial result for 2025 despite 2% revenue growth, because costs rose faster (2.3%).
- VNPF flagged that municipal subsidies failed to offset rising fixed costs, with some venues receiving less funding in 2025 than 2024.

### Industry Problem
Venue clients face a structural squeeze between rising fixed costs (staff, property) and stagnant or declining subsidy/ticket revenue, threatening their ability to program diverse and emerging talent — a concrete data point on the financial pain point facing the venue segment of Etix's client base.

### Proposed Solution
Monitor whether similar cost/revenue squeezes appear in other markets Etix serves; the trigger for Etix to act would be clients citing fixed-cost pressure as a reason to seek lower ticketing fees, more flexible settlement terms, or tools that improve programming ROI (e.g., dynamic pricing, better demand forecasting for club nights vs. concerts).

**Dutch pop venues saw their audiences drop by 5% in 2025, with more than half ending the year with a negative financial result.**

The figures were featured by the Association of Dutch Pop and Festivals ([VNPF](https://vnpf.nl/nieuws/stijgende-kosten-minder-publiek-en-achterblijvende-subsidies-zetten-financiele-en-artistieke-positie-van-veel-poppodia-onder-druk/)) in its latest  *Pop Venues and Festivals*  i *n Figures* report, with analysis based on data from the trade body’s 55 associated music venues.

Last year, VNPF’s affiliated venues programmed 29,523 performances, attracting more than 6.3 million guests, marking a 5% decline on 2024. However, it is still 3% more than in 2023. VNPF said the drop was mostly due to fewer attendees at club nights, which dropped by 14% compared to the year prior.

The trade body noted that the image is mixed, as concerts actually attracted bigger audiences in 2025. Additionally, 60% of venues reported a decline in club night attendance while 40% reported growth.

VNPF reported a total revenue increase of 2% year-on-year to reach €247m (£211m/$283m), but total expenditure also increased 2.3% to total €251m.

Fixed costs for aspects like staff and property increased, while ticket sales and food and beverage intake were under pressure with club nights attracting fewer guests. VNPF also noted that municipal subsidies did not offer sufficient compensation for these rising fixed costs.

In 2025, a new four-year subsidy period began in a number of Dutch municipalities, with many venues assigned a new subsidy amount for 2025 – however, VNPF said some received less in 2025 than in 2024.

The trade body added that not only did financial pressure affect venues and the industry, but also the development of emerging artists and new audiences, as well as the diversity of entertainment on offer.

“Pop stages are much more than concert halls,” said VNPF director Berend Schans.

“They curate supply, develop talent, build up audiences and form an indispensable link in the Dutch music ecosystem. But that role requires financial preconditions that fit today’s reality. If the fixed costs continue to rise and the space for programming becomes smaller, the diversity, innovation and accessibility of the offer will eventually also be under pressure.”
