# Echoes of Earth at 10: Inside the Indian event’s sustainable rise

## Summary

A profile of Bangalore's Echoes of Earth festival, marking its 10th anniversary, describing how organizer Swordfish Events & Entertainment built sustainability into the event as it scaled from 5,000 to 25,000 capacity. The festival now diverts all waste from landfill, has grown its solar footprint from 5kW to 100kW, and eliminated over a million single-use plastic bottles via a reusable-tumbler scheme, while organizers Adnan Ahmed and Piyushi Taunk discuss India's growing viability as a touring and festival market.

### Key Points
- Echoes of Earth launched in 2016 and has grown to a four-stage, 25,000-capacity festival; its 10th-anniversary edition runs December 5–6 in Bangalore with roughly 40 acts.
- The festival achieves zero-kilogram landfill contribution and bans single-use plastics for vendors and attendees.
- Solar power capacity grew from 5 kilowatts (2016) to 100 kilowatts, now powering a stage, food court, box office, and general lighting.
- A reusable metal tumbler scheme (10,000 tumblers introduced in 2022) has prevented over 1 million single-use plastic bottles from use.
- Organizers cite India's improving technical infrastructure and cite Lollapalooza and Rolling Loud's recent India debuts as evidence of the market's rise.
- Organizer Adnan Ahmed flags the need for a "single-ticket window" to streamline government event-permission processes as a remaining hurdle for the Indian market.
- Echoes of Earth won the AGF Community Action Award (2026) and the Circular Festival Award (2025).

## Transcript

## Echoes of Earth at 10: Inside the Indian event’s sustainable rise

The team behind the green Bangalore festival tell IQ how they are future-proofing the event as competition ramps up in the domestic scene

![Adnan Ahmed & Piyushi Taunk](https://www.iqmagazine.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/07/Adnan-Ahmed-Piyushi-Taunk.png)

Adnan Ahmed & Piyushi Taunk

As India’s festival market expands, organisers behind Bangalore’s Echoes of Earth tell *IQ* how baking sustainability into its DNA has helped future-proof the festival as the domestic scene picks up speed.

Branded as India’s greenest circular music festival, Echoes of Earth launched in 2016 and has scaled from a 5,000-person gathering to a four-stage, 25,000-capacity festival over the past decade. Having also staged editions in Goa, the festival will return to Bangalore from 5-6 December for its 10th anniversary edition, with roughly 40 international and domestic acts in store for this year’s event.

From the outset, the team behind Echoes of Earth – Swordfish Events & Entertainment – set out to create a music festival with purpose.

“We started off in 2016 as a very simple festival that had the idea of using music as a platform to spread a larger message,” Adnan Ahmed, Swordfish’s head of IPs and Echoes’ music curator, tells *IQ*. “We just started off with no sort of experience or no reference point for something like this, and ever since, I think we’ve been trying to innovate and increase our efforts into sustainability.”

Today, sustainability is embedded across every aspect of the event, from reusing structural materials to banning single-use plastics for vendors and audiences alike. After a decade of refining its operations, the festival now diverts all waste from landfill, recording a zero-kilogram landfill contribution from the two-day event.

Organisers have created a space for music discovery, with the event known for its diverse, genre-spanning lineups that include talent from both global and local domains. Previously, acts including Mount Kimbie, French 79, Acid Pauli, Sid Sriram, Peter Cat Recording Co, and Thaikkudam Bridge have all graced the Echoes stage.

Despite its rapid growth, organisers say their sustainability achievements are due in part to an increasingly engaged audience.

“We’re a festival that’s seen an increase in our audience year on year, but what we also notice is that the waste that is generated has reduced,” Ahmed explains. “Over the years, we’ve seen people become more conscious about not generating more waste. As a growing festival, our waste should be increasing, but we’ve noticed that it’s slowly gone down – it’s a big achievement.”

![](https://www.iqmagazine.com/wp-content/smush-webp/2026/07/Echoes-of-Earth-©-Rithvik-AR-430x269.png.webp)

The rise of India as a touring and festival destination is an increasingly hot topic for the global live music industry. In recent years, [Lollapalooza](https://www.iqmagazine.com/2026/02/india-isnt-an-emerging-market-anymore-its-arrived/)  and [Rolling Loud](https://www.iqmagazine.com/2025/12/rolling-loud-debuts-in-india-we-made-history/) have made their debut in the market, while acts like Ed Sheeran, Coldplay, Post Malone, and Maroon 5 have all come through the touring circuit.

As the country’s live entertainment sector continues to expand, Ahmed says he’s seen the country’s technical capabilities improve dramatically, furthering its capabilities to host world-class productions.

“India may have previously not have had the most cutting-edge technology on what the global standard is used to, but we are rapidly growing in that sector,” Ahmed says. “A technical rider from say five years ago, maybe half of it would not have been available in India. But now, we’re able to speak to global agencies and work with them, and we’re able to co-curate a rider with them and explain what is alternatively available in India.

“The infrastructure that was available in India before and now has drastically developed, and people will be looking at India differently because of that,” he says.

Beyond its own events, the Swordfish team has also shared its expertise in both sustainability and hosting live events in the market, reflecting the power of local expertise in the burgeoning market.

“It is an important market for most of the artists right now, right?” Ahmed says. “What you can get by touring Europe today, on a large scale, you can do maybe a three-city tour in India and achieve a lot more listenership by the end of it.”

> “Artists are definitely going to put India into the touring map”

However, he says that government [support](https://www.iqmagazine.com/2026/01/india-sets-up-live-events-development-cell/) and [facilitation](https://www.iqmagazine.com/2026/04/indian-state-officials-move-to-streamline-event-permissions/) will be key to unlocking the market’s full potential in the coming years, highlighting the need for a single-ticket window to streamline approval processes for large-scale events.

“Artists are definitely going to put India into the touring map, but we also need to be really sure of the infrastructure and the permissions that are available in India,” he says. “Once that happens, artists are going to make sure it’s part of their touring schedule.”

The reality of India’s infrastructure is one that’s changed drastically over the past decade, the Swordfish team shares. Yet one of the biggest hurdles for organisers and the market at large remains energy, with offsetting consumption a key challenge today.

“India is an ecosystem where it’s not easy to get on the power grid systems from the government, so we all have to depend on diesel generators,” Piyushi Taunk, Echoes’ head of department IP & special projects, tells *IQ*.

“Over the years, we started integrating solar power into the equation, starting really small with five kilowatts of power in 2016. Our solar footprint is also now increased to 100 kilowatts of power, and we’re able to power a stage, a food court, the box office, and general lighting of the festival,” she says.

> “We must consider the kind of support and the resources that are available for us to put in those efforts”

Alongside expanding its use of solar energy, Echoes of Earth has steadily reduced waste through a series of practical initiatives.

Since the beginning, the festival has offered free drinking water, expanding efforts in 2022 with a reusable bottle scheme that introduced 10,000 metal tumblers into circulation. Organisers estimate that the effort has prevented more than 1 million single-use plastic bottles from being used, in line with their wider ban on one-off plastics.

“At other festivals, in India and across the world, selling water is a business opportunity, right?” Ahmed says. “They sell water more than they sell beer sometimes, but for us, it was the simple question of, why does water have to be inaccessible, or why do I have to pay for something like this? We were also very strong believers of the concept of the RO \[reverse osmosis\] purification that we do at the festival, so we were strong believers that this could be a good opportunity.”

The festival has also introduced electric vehicles for artist transfers and crew transport, while the food vendors all utilise biofuels to reduce petroleum – all while “keeping in mind India as a region and the kind of support and the resources that are available for us to put in those efforts,” Taunk says.

Those efforts have earned Echoes of Earth international recognition, including the [AGF](https://www.agreenerfuture.com/international-agf-awards) Community Action Award in 2026 and the Circular Festival Award in 2025, placing the event at the forefront of change for Asian festivals.
