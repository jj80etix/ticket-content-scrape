# Washington D.C. Passes Industry-Backed Price Cap Bill, Stripping Primary Oversight in Last-Minute Amendment

## Summary

The Washington, D.C. Council unanimously passed the RESALE Act, one of the most restrictive US ticket resale laws, capping resale markups, banning speculative listings, and imposing licensing/bonding on high-volume resellers. In a last-minute amendment, Councilmember Brooke Pinto stripped out authority the mayor would have had to regulate primary-market ticketing fees and dynamic pricing, leaving primary sellers' pricing practices untouched while resale marketplaces absorb the new restrictions.

### Key Points
- Bill (B26-0224) heads to Mayor Muriel Bowser and faces congressional review; most provisions take effect Jan. 1, 2027.
- Original committee draft would have let the mayor regulate primary-market fees and dynamic pricing too; that authority was removed at final reading via Councilmember Brooke Pinto's amendment.
- Anyone posting 50+ resale listings/year must obtain a D.C. license and hold a $25,000 surety bond; resale exchanges must verify licensing and report unlicensed sellers.
- Speculative ticketing is broadly banned — resellers can't list tickets not in actual/constructive possession; a contractual right to future transfer doesn't count.
- Bill bans "surveillance pricing" (using personal data to set individualized prices) but does not restrict ordinary demand-based dynamic pricing by primary sellers.
- Live Nation publicly praised the final legislation; the bill's structure tracks the NIVA/Fix the Tix coalition policy platform (Fix the Tix members include NIVA, Eventbrite, Recording Academy, Music Artists Coalition).
- Coalition for Ticket Fairness spokesperson Geoff Vetter warned the law disadvantages small/medium resellers competing against Ticketmaster.

### Highlights
- Effective date Jan. 1, 2027; unanimous D.C. Council vote; primary-fee oversight authority explicitly removed by amendment before passage.
- $25,000 surety bond and D.C. licensing required for resellers with 50+ annual listings.
- Speculative-ticket ban blocks resale of tickets not yet in the seller's possession — directly affects delayed-delivery ticketing models.
- "Surveillance pricing" ban (personal-data-based individualized pricing) explicitly carves out ordinary demand-based dynamic pricing as still permitted.
- Live Nation and Fix the Tix coalition (NIVA, Eventbrite, Recording Academy, Music Artists Coalition) delivered the legislative outcome; primary market untouched.

### Industry Problem
Lawmakers regulated only the resale side of the ticket market while leaving primary sellers' inventory control, dynamic pricing, delivery timing, and transfer restrictions unregulated — a structural asymmetry that independent resellers argue entrenches dominant primary platforms (namely Ticketmaster) and narrows competitive alternatives for consumers seeking lower-priced resale.

### Proposed Solution
The bill itself offers no primary-side pricing fix — that provision was deliberately stripped. For an independent primary platform like Etix, this is a state-patchwork regulatory pattern to track (similar price-cap/speculative-ticketing bills are likely to spread state-by-state per the Fix the Tix coalition's stated strategy): monitor for parallel bills, and treat visible "all-in"/non-surveillance pricing and honest delivery-timing practices as a differentiation opportunity against platforms facing this kind of scrutiny.

**The RESALE Act adds transparency and anti-fraud protections, but its final form imposes direct price controls largely on secondary marketplaces while leaving primary ticket fees and dynamic pricing untouched.**

The Washington, D.C., Council has unanimously approved one of the nation’s most restrictive ticket resale laws, capping seller markups, banning speculative listings and imposing licensing and bonding requirements on higher-volume resellers.

But the final version of the [Restricting Egregious Scalping Against Live Entertainment Act](https://lims.dccouncil.gov/Legislation/B26-0224) also represents a retreat from a broader framework that would have allowed the District to address pricing and fees across both primary and secondary ticket markets.

Instead, lawmakers restored a fixed resale price cap and removed the remaining authority that could have been used to regulate fees charged by primary sellers.

The Council’s Public Works and Operations Committee subsequently . That version could have supported rules addressing primary ticket fees, dynamic pricing and resale markups.

The committee report recognized a central problem with regulating resale alone: Primary sellers could continue increasing initial prices through dynamic pricing and other practices, leaving regulators to play what the report described as “price-gouging whack-a-mole.”

That approach did not survive.

At first reading, Councilmembers Charles Allen and Christina Henderson restored the fixed resale cap. The engrossed bill retained limited authority for the mayor to regulate primary-market ticketing fees while allowing future adjustments to resale price and marketplace-fee limits beginning in 2028.

At final reading, Councilmember Brooke Pinto successfully moved to eliminate the primary-market fee provision.

Pinto argued that ticketing fees are part of the financial model used by artists, promoters and venues, including arrangements under which venues receive advances against anticipated ticketing revenue. Giving the mayor open-ended authority over those fees, she said, could create uncertainty and make D.C. less attractive to touring performers.

“Touring artists, venues, and promoters rely on stable fee structures when planning shows, negotiating deals, and setting ticket prices,” the amendment’s supporting memorandum said.

The Council accepted that argument and removed the primary-fee authority while leaving the resale restrictions intact.

Live Nation, which owns Ticketmaster and operates a substantial D.C.-area concert business, publicly praised the final legislation, including its speculative-ticket ban, all-in pricing rules and resale cap.

While the final language of the bill prohibits “surveillance pricing,” that restriction is not a general prohibition on dynamic pricing, as it typically exists today in ticketing.

Under the new language, ticket issuers, resellers and exchanges may not use personally identifiable information collected through surveillance technology to determine an individual consumer’s price, fee or discount.

A final amendment permits publicly disclosed discounts for students, military personnel, senior citizens, loyalty-program members, mailing-list subscribers, group purchasers and participants in need-based programs. Location information may also be used to calculate taxes and meet local requirements.

So, while the provision restricts personalized prices based on a consumer’s identity, behavior or personal characteristics, this bill has no prohibition against a primary seller changing prices based on inventory, aggregate demand, timing or event popularity. The committee language that could have enabled broader regulation of dynamic pricing was removed before passage.

## Licensing, Speculative Sales and Other Measures

While the price regulations are the headline item and the one being broadly celebrated by industry insiders on social media this week, there are other regulations imposed in the new regulatory scheme.

Anyone posting 50 or more ticket resale listings during a year must obtain a D.C. license and maintain a $25,000 surety bond. Resale exchanges must verify that affected sellers are licensed and bonded, retain transaction records and report apparently unlicensed sellers.

The bill also broadly prohibits speculative ticketing. Resellers may not advertise or sell tickets that are not in their actual or constructive possession. A contractual expectation that a ticket will later be transferred is specifically insufficient. This is almost certain to complicate independent resale for events where primary sellers delay ticket delivery until shortly before showtime, as has become standard in the industry.

Issuers therefore retain substantial control over whether tickets can be transferred, when transfers become available and which resale channels can operate effectively.

## Live Nation and “Independent” Allies Delivered Price Cap to Council

The final structure closely tracks the policy platform promoted by the National Independent Venue Association and its Fix the Tix coalition.

[Washingtonian reported](https://washingtonian.com/2026/07/15/dc-just-passed-a-bill-to-protect-you-from-ticket-scalpers) that Audrey Fix Schaefer, communications director for D.C. promoter I.M.P., originally approached Allen about the legislation. I.M.P. operates the 9:30 Club, The Anthem, Lincoln Theatre and The Atlantis.

Schaefer is also president of NIVA’s board, where her affiliation includes I.M.P., the 9:30 Club and The Anthem.

NIVA and Fix the Tix have promoted resale price caps and speculative-ticket restrictions nationally, urging both Congress and state lawmakers to adopt similar policies. Fix the Tix members include NIVA, Eventbrite, the Recording Academy and the Music Artists Coalition, [regularly working alongside Live Nation’s lobbying apparatus with the same end-goal](https://www.ticketnews.com/2023/06/fix-the-tix-has-priorities-similar-to-live-nations-fair-proposals/) s.

The Music Artists Coalition supports legislation allowing artists to control resale terms and impose resale caps. The organization was formed with substantial involvement from Irving Azoff, the former Ticketmaster chief executive and Live Nation executive whose businesses remain deeply involved in artist management and live entertainment.

Given that Live Nation Entertainment and its Fix The Tix allies have been lobbying for similar price cap and anti-resale legislation at both the federal level and on a state-by-state basis for multiple years, it is expected that this victory in Washington D.C. will spur a fresh round of efforts to grow such burdens against competetive independent resale wherever they can find sympathetic lawmakers.

Price cap or no, demand for sold-out events will not disappear. Transactions could migrate to social media, private groups or other channels with fewer refund guarantees and weaker seller accountability. Demand may also be redirected toward premium primary inventory and issuer-controlled exchanges, where prices can be surged to meet demand without legal oversight, and then dropped to move unsold inventory.

The Coalition for Ticket Fairness said the legislation could reduce consumer choice and make it harder for independent businesses to compete with Ticketmaster.

“Small- and medium-sized resellers provide fans with more choice, personalized service, and an important competitive alternative in a marketplace dominated by Ticketmaster,” coalition spokesperson Geoff Vetter said.

The Council briefly advanced a framework capable of examining high prices wherever they emerged in the ticketing ecosystem. The final bill abandons that approach.

Primary sellers will continue controlling initial prices, inventory releases, dynamic pricing, delivery timing and transfer restrictions. Independent resale marketplaces will operate under a fixed markup cap, licensing rules and reporting requirements tied to the original price set by the primary seller.

D.C. consumers may encounter some lower-priced resale listings and stronger protections against fake or undelivered tickets. They may also find less inventory on regulated independent marketplaces and become more dependent on the primary-ticketing systems whose pricing and competitive practices have generated federal and state antitrust scrutiny.

The RESALE Act addresses genuine abuses. But after lawmakers stripped out primary-market oversight, it regulates one side of the ticket market while leaving the other – and the ticketing giant at the core of it – untouched.
