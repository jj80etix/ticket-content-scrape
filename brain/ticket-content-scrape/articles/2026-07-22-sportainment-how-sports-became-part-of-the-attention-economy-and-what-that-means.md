# Sportainment: how sports became part of the attention economy — and what that means for the business

## Summary

A Fever exec's op-ed argues sports and live entertainment have merged into one attention-economy market — citing Bad Bunny's 17-night Atlético Madrid stadium residency and $55B+ of private capital flowing into sports since 2019 — and that the strategic frontier is turning venues into 365-day entertainment platforms rather than single-purpose sports venues.

### Key Points
- Bad Bunny's 10-night Atlético Madrid residency drew 600,000 fans and an estimated €200M economic impact.
- Sports IP trades at 2–5x the revenue multiple of generic PE assets (NBA 11.9x, NFL 10.1x, MLS 9.4x revenue).
- Only 4 of 20 Premier League clubs were profitable in 2024–25 per Sportico; European clubs are closing the valuation gap via capital appreciation, not operating profit.
- Thesis: rights holders that operate as media/live-entertainment platforms (not just sport) will lead the next decade.

### Industry Problem
Venue and rights-holder revenue is increasingly capped by single-purpose programming (games/matches only); the operating-profit gap at many European clubs shows the financial pressure to monetize venues and fan affinity year-round.

### Proposed Solution
The article frames the fix as diversifying venue programming (residencies, adjacent live formats) and owning the "what to do tonight" discovery layer. For Etix's client base, this is a long-horizon signal to watch — venues and rights holders that adopt a platform mindset will want ticketing/CRM infrastructure flexible enough to handle non-traditional, high-frequency programming rather than a seasonal sports calendar.

*Article from Mariano Otero, senior vice president, business development at Fever.*

**On 30 May 2026, Bad Bunny walked onstage at the Riyadh Air Metropolitano and opened a ten-night residency that would draw 600,000 fans across seventeen days and generate an estimated €200 million of impact on the Madrid economy.**

He was not the first artist to headline the stadium, and he will not be the last: The Weeknd, Bruno Mars, BTS and, in 2027, an eleven-night Coldplay run are all confirmed. Atlético Madrid, LaLiga’s third-most-valuable club, is now booking the kind of residency calendar that Las Vegas built its economy around.

**The line between sport and entertainment has collapsed**

A Champions League match no longer competes only with other matches. It competes with Bad Bunny, with the next season of \*Drive to Survive\*, with an immersive Mamma Mia! dinner show, with the FIFA tournament happening on Discord. The competitive set is the entire universe of things people could do with three hours of a Saturday night. Clubs and leagues are no longer fighting only for share of wallet. They are fighting for share of attention, and through that, for share of time. Herbert Simon, who would later win the Nobel Prize in economics, made the observation fifty years ago that has aged better than almost any other piece of social theory: in a world where information is abundant, attention becomes the scarce resource. The entire economy has rearranged around that fact.

Sport happens to be structurally built to win in that economy. It is live, which means it resists time-shifting and ad-skipping. It is tribal, which means fandom is identity rather than preference, and identity does not fragment. It is narratively rich, which means a single match generates weeks of content across highlights, debate, documentaries and social. And it is calendrically scarce – a season is a finite event, each match a non-repeatable occasion. Nothing else in the content landscape ticks all four boxes. Streaming fragmented every other category. Sport got stronger.

The reframe is showing up in capital flows. Sports IP now trades at 2–5× the revenue multiple of generic private equity industries. The NBA averages 11.9× revenue, the NFL 10.1×, MLS 9.4×. Even after the European “relegation discount,” the Premier League’s top six trade at roughly 5.7× and LaLiga’s top clubs at 5.6× — still 2–3× above the all-sector PE median of 2.2×. More than $55 billion of private capital has flowed into sport since 2019, and over 74 North American professional teams now have some form of PE involvement. The asset class has been repriced from trophy holding to growth holding.

But the model works better on one side of the Atlantic than the other. American leagues have always been built for capital: closed leagues, salary caps, reverse-order drafts and equal revenue sharing. The average NFL franchise generates roughly $140 million in operating profit. In Europe the numbers are different.

![](https://www.theticketingbusiness.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/07/Mariano-Otero-300x300.jpg)

Mariano Otero, SVP, business development at Fever

According to Sportico’s 2024–25 analysis, only 4 of 20 Premier League clubs were profitable. Most of the highest-valued European clubs, Manchester United, City, Liverpool, Tottenham, Chelsea, operated at a loss. European football is closing the valuation gap on the back of capital appreciation, not operating profitability. The economic case for many of these investments still needs to be proven.

Which is why the interesting work now sits on the revenue side of the P&L. If sport and live entertainment are now one market competing for time, and if European clubs are still working to close their operating gap, the question facing every rights-holder shifts from “how do we win matches” to “how do we monetize the affinity we already have.” That is the gap where companies like Fever are working. By bringing technology to upgrade the fan experience, by layering in adjacent content and live formats, by owning the moment of decision — what to do tonight, this weekend, next month — and by helping rights-holders extend their IP into new formats, the goal is to turn a stadium into a 365-day platform and a fan into a year-round customer.

The Atlético case study is what that looks like in practice. Ten nights of music inside a football stadium generated a meaningful share of the club’s annual revenue, in a matter of weeks, on high margins. Football still drove the brand; the brand monetised the venue; the venue increasingly resembled a year-round live entertainment platform.

The rights-holders that treat themselves as media and live entertainment businesses – not only as sport – will own the next decade. That is true for the top-flight European clubs that are still figuring out how to close their operating gap, for the American leagues that have always been built for capital efficiency, and for the emerging leagues in Asia and the Middle East that are exporting franchise IP for the first time. For the platforms that sit alongside them – including Fever, working with partners like Real Madrid, Atlético de Madrid and Formula 1 – the mission is clear: build the discovery, technology and monetisation layer that turns fan affinity into durable revenue. What the next frontier looks like in 2030 is genuinely open. The direction is not.
