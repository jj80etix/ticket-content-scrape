# Ticketing-focused changes recommended following Celtic-Rangers disorder

## Summary

An independent Scottish FA review into March's Rangers-Celtic Scottish Cup disorder at Ibrox has issued ticketing-specific recommendations, including expanded digital away-ticketing, unique visual differentiation for paper tickets at major fixtures, and exploration of dynamic/rotating QR-code barcode technology. The review followed a post-match pitch invasion and crowd violence that left two people hospitalized and led to 37 arrests; both clubs face disciplinary hearings next month.

### Key Points
- Review authored by UEFA match delegate Mark Blackbourne, with input from former police officials and both clubs' heads of ticketing.
- Recommends increased digital away-ticketing and visually distinct paper tickets for high-risk fixtures.
- Recommends exploring dynamic/rotating QR-code barcode technology industry-wide.
- Digital ticketing cited specifically for improving cancellation capability, real-time operational control, audit visibility, and supporter traceability.
- Both clubs face disciplinary hearings next month over the March disorder.

### Highlights
- Concrete tech recommendation: rotating/dynamic QR barcodes for high-risk matches.
- Digital ticketing framed as a safety tool (real-time cancellation, traceability), not just a fraud tool.
- An independent review directly recommended ticketing-tech changes following a violent, high-profile safety incident — a governance-driven push, not just industry best practice.

### Industry Problem
Paper tickets and static barcodes at high-tension fixtures create safety and security gaps — clubs can't quickly cancel or trace tickets in real time when crowd-control incidents unfold, and static codes are easier to duplicate or misuse for unauthorized entry.

### Proposed Solution
The review's ask — rotating/dynamic barcodes plus visually distinct paper tickets and expanded digital-away-ticketing — is effectively a product spec; Etix's fraud/security roadmap (Q7) should track whether this becomes a governing-body mandate (UK football, then possibly other leagues), since compliance-driven feature requirements often precede wider client demand.

TheTicketingBusiness News

**The Scottish FA has published its independent review into the events surrounding the men’s Scottish Cup quarter-final between Rangers and Celtic at Ibrox Stadium in March, including ticketing operations recommendations.**

The review was undertaken by Mark Blackbourne, sports event consultant, UEFA match delegate and UEFA stadium inspector, with input from specialists including Steve Storey, former chief superintendent of Northumbria Police, and head of safety and security at Premier League club Newcastle United, and ticketing consultant . Heads of ticketing at Hampden Park, Ibrox and Celtic Park also provided information for the report.

Its purpose was to establish the facts around what happened, examine the planning and preparedness, assess all aspects of planning and arrangements for matchday safety and identify any lessons learnt.

**How we got here**

There were clashes on the pitch after Celtic claimed victory through a penalty shootout in the Scottish Cup quarter-final on March 8, with some visiting fans trespassing on the pitch. Home fans then tried to confront the away fans, with flares and bottles thrown back and forth. Some 37 people were arrested, according to *[BBC Sport](https://www.bbc.co.uk/sport/football/articles/c5y6dk79g08o).*

The report detailed how access gates were forced open, stewards and football staff were assaulted – with two hospitalised – and seats and LED boards were damaged. The report read: “The post-match pitch invasion by several hundred Celtic supporters was wholly inexcusable. It provoked an immediate hostile response from sections of the home support, who forced past stewards and police to reach the pitch. Flares and bottles were thrown, a steward and Celtic staff member were accosted, and multiple scuffles broke out before control was re-established.”

The report added that the “initial actions of the disorderly Ultra groups (whose conduct set the tone) were a significant contributory factor” in the chaos.

It was the first time since 2018 the derby match had a full allocation of away fans, with roughly 8,000 Celtic supporters attending.

Both clubs have been cited and will face hearings next month.

**Ticketing**

To try and prevent situations such as this reoccurring, a number of recommendations on ticketing have been made. This includes the increased use of digital away ticketing where appropriate, and unique visual ticket differentiation should be introduced between major fixtures where paper tickets remain in use.

The report noted that wider use of digital ticketing could improve cancellation capability; real-time operational control; audit visibility; supporter traceability; and supporter communication capability.

It was also suggested that the wider operational use of dynamic barcode or rotating QR code technology be explored, and that Scottish football should consider whether existing operational caution regarding digital away ticketing remains proportionate to the technical and operational capability now available.

Additionally, it was recommended that clubs give greater consideration to ticket ownership traceability, customer-level audit visibility and transfer visibility during high-risk fixtures.

It was also recommended there be greater operation integration between ticket offices, safety officers, the match operations teams and police.

Greater consideration should be given to the role of ticketing, with contingency planning for ticketless supporters, forged tickets, tailgating and perimeter breaches.

Consideration should also be given to whether Scottish Professional Football League operational toolkits and unacceptable conduct guidance contain clearer operation expectations and minimum ticketing standards.

In response to the report, Celtic’s interim chairman said: “We welcome the repeated emphasis on the primacy of safety above all other considerations, a message consistent with Celtic’s approach in all it does. Celtic made its strong objections to important elements of matchday planning very clear, well in advance of the match.

“While we remain in dispute regarding certain points within this review, our club does not shrink from criticisms contained in the report or the lessons to be drawn. Nobody should, since the potential implications of disregarding them are obviously serious, both for safety and for football.”
