# New Jersey Signals Focus on ‘Junk Fees’ Regulation and Enforcement

## Summary

New Jersey Governor Mikie Sherrill and Attorney General Jennifer Davenport launched a statewide "junk fees" initiative in June 2026, pairing an AG enforcement statement with a gubernatorial executive order. The move adds New Jersey to the growing list of states targeting hidden/surprise fees as part of a broader affordability push, with enforcement action grounded in the existing New Jersey Consumer Fraud Act.

### Key Points
- Enforcement statement (NJ AG + Division of Consumer Affairs) flags drip pricing, dark patterns, misrepresenting fee purpose, material omissions, unbundled pricing, excessive/unconscionable fees, and consent manipulation (pre-checked boxes, E-SIGN Act violations) as potential violations.
- Legal basis: New Jersey Consumer Fraud Act, penalties up to $10,000 for a first violation and $20,000 per subsequent violation; AG can also ground enforcement in violations of other state or federal law.
- Executive Order No. 19 directs every executive branch agency to review junk fees in industries it regulates and recommend regulatory/legislative action by September 2026.
- Central executive-order goal: make "all-in" pricing (full cost shown up front) the standard in New Jersey.
- Agencies must also review their own contracts to check whether contractors are permitted to impose junk fees.
- Agency reports to the governor are confidential but may seed future legislation or rulemaking.

### Highlights
- Enforcement statement issued June 2026 by NJ AG Jennifer Davenport and Division of Consumer Affairs, alongside Governor Sherrill's Executive Order No. 19.
- Statutory penalties: up to $10,000 (first violation) / $20,000 (subsequent) per violation under the NJ Consumer Fraud Act.
- Agency recommendations on junk-fee regulation due to the governor's office by September 2026 — a concrete near-term deadline to watch for follow-on rulemaking or legislation.
- Drip pricing and dark-pattern checkout flows are explicitly named as enforcement targets, directly implicating ticketing fee disclosure and checkout UX.

### Industry Problem
This evidences the state-by-state expansion of "junk fee"/all-in pricing enforcement risk beyond the FTC's federal rule — New Jersey is now scrutinizing checkout flows, mandatory fees, add-on products, and consent mechanics (pre-checked boxes, drip pricing) under existing consumer-fraud law, meaning ticketing platforms face real per-violation liability in yet another state even without new legislation.

### Proposed Solution
The article's own recommendation is a compliance audit of pricing/disclosure practices, checkout flows, mandatory fees, and add-ons ahead of the September 2026 agency report. For Etix, this reinforces the case for proactively hardening all-in/transparent pricing display and consent flows (no pre-checked add-ons, clear fee itemization) ahead of enforcement, and monitoring the September 2026 NJ agency recommendations for signs of new rulemaking that would require product changes.

In June, New Jersey Governor Mikie Sherrill and Attorney General (AG) Jennifer Davenport announced a new statewide initiative on “junk fees.” With this announcement, New Jersey joins other states that have targeted “junk fees” as part of a broader focus on affordability.

New Jersey’s announcement pairs an [enforcement statement](https://www.njconsumeraffairs.gov/Junk-Fees/Documents/Junk-Fees-Enforcement-Statement.pdf) from the AG and Division of Consumer Affairs with an [executive order](https://nj.gov/infobank/eo/057sherrill/pdf/EO-19.pdf) issued by the governor. While the enforcement statement highlights regulatory risks under existing law, the executive order foreshadows potential new legislative and regulatory requirements.

### Enforcement Guidance

The enforcement statement signals that the AG and Division of Consumer Affairs are prioritizing enforcement efforts relating to “junk fees.” The statement also explains what features of fees may make them “junk” in the eyes of regulators in New Jersey and how those regulators may ground enforcement actions in existing New Jersey law.

The enforcement statement defines junk fees as “hidden, surprise, or excessively overpriced fees,” including those that provide “little or no benefit to the consumer” or are “not transparently disclosed.” Some of the practices flagged for scrutiny include:

- **Drip Pricing**. The statement says that “the practice of hiding mandatory fees by luring the consumer in with a low advertised price and then tacking on fees later or at the end of the purchasing process” may be unlawfully deceptive.
- **Dark Patterns.** The statement describes “dark patterns” as website or app design features that “can hide information through dense fine print and “terms of service,” pop-ups, manipulated font sizing, text placement, and complex click-throughs.” The statement indicates that these features may confuse, frustrate, and deceive consumers.
- **Misrepresenting the Nature of Fees.** The statement notes that a company may violate New Jersey law by misleading consumers about the purpose of a fee, the recipients of the fee, whether the fee is mandatory or optional, or what value the fee provides to the consumer.
- **Material Omissions Regarding Fees.** The statement indicates that a company may face regulatory action for “knowingly failing to disclose the existence of a fee or the qualities of a fee that would be material to the consumer’s decision to accept the fee or make a purchase,” such as “focusing a buyer on a monthly payment amount without disclosing that the monthly payment includes an optional fee.”
- **Unbundled Pricing.** The statement says that “unbundling the true price of a product into an array of fees for ostensibly separate goods and services” may “distort prices” in a manner that is unconscionable and deceptive “when the consumer reasonably believes the unbundled goods or services would be included in the underlying price and the itemization of separate fees is not otherwise required by law.”
- **Excessive Fees.** The statement indicates that New Jersey regulators may target high fees as “unconscionable” if the fees do not adequately align with the corresponding value of the good or service to the consumer.
- **Practices Relating to Consumer Consent.** The statement also flags for scrutiny practices that may deny consumers an opportunity to provide meaningful and informed consent for fees, such as “practices like pre-loading fees onto a contract, pre-checking boxes that opt the consumer in to fees, violating the provisions of the federal E-SIGN Act that specifically set forth how companies can obtain electronic consent, or employing high-pressure sales tactics that effectively force consumers into providing their consent.”

The enforcement statement identifies the New Jersey Consumer Fraud Act as the primary legal authority for the AG and Division of Consumer Affairs to bring enforcement actions regarding “junk fees.” The statute carries penalties of up to $10,000 for a first violation and $20,000 per subsequent violation.

The Consumer Fraud Act prohibits unconscionable or abusive practices; fraud, deception, and misrepresentations; and knowing material omissions in connection with commercial practices. In addition, the Consumer Fraud Act allows the New Jersey AG to pursue investigations and enforcement concerning “any commercial practice that violates State or federal law.” This means, for example, that the AG may seek to rely on federal regulations regarding fees as the basis for enforcement actions under the Consumer Fraud Act.

### Executive Order

The AG announced the enforcement statement on Junk Fees alongside the governor, who also issued an executive order on the topic.

Executive Order No. 19 directs every executive branch agency to review the industries it regulates, assess the prevalence and consumer impact of junk fees, and recommend regulatory or legislative action by September 2026. A central goal is to make “all-in” pricing — where consumers see the full cost up front — the standard in New Jersey.

The agencies’ reports and recommendations are to be addressed to the governor and kept confidential, but may inform future efforts by the administration to regulate the practices identified, either through legislation or regulatory action.

In addition, agencies are directed to evaluate their existing contracts to determine whether they permit contractors to impose “junk fees.”

### Takeaways

The regulatory focus on “junk fees” is not new. Many companies will already have audited their pricing and disclosure practices in response to this widespread regulatory scrutiny. But for companies operating in New Jersey or interacting with New Jersey consumers, the announcement of the state’s “junk fees” initiative provides an opportunity to reassess those practices — particularly checkout flows, mandatory fees, and add-on products — to ensure alignment with the state’s enforcement guidance. Additionally, companies should be prepared to engage with regulators who may be exploring industry practices in connection with the reports and recommendations due to the governor’s office in September.
