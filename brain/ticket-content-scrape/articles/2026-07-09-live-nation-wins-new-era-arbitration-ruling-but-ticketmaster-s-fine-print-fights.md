# Live Nation Wins New Era Arbitration Ruling, But Ticketmaster’s Fine Print Fights Continue

## Summary

A New York federal judge ordered two consumer antitrust lawsuits against Live Nation/Ticketmaster (Jacobson and Leifer) into arbitration under New Era ADR's revised 2024 rules, finding the updated procedures meaningfully different from — and not controlled by — the Ninth Circuit's 2024 Heckman ruling that rejected Ticketmaster's earlier arbitration setup. The ruling is narrow: it applies to specific secondary-market claims and stipulated rule changes, doesn't disturb the ongoing Popp (formerly Heckman) class action or the separate government/state antitrust case where a jury already found Live Nation/Ticketmaster liable.

### Highlights
- Judge Arun Subramanian (SDNY) compelled arbitration in Jacobson v. Live Nation and Leifer v. Live Nation, June 29 opinion.
- Distinguished New Era ADR's revised 2024 rules (added discovery rights, removed mass-arbitration and asymmetric-appeal provisions) from the rules the Ninth Circuit found unconscionable in Heckman.
- Does not affect the Popp v. Live Nation nationwide class action (trial set July 6, 2027) or the government/state antitrust case, where an April jury verdict already found Live Nation/Ticketmaster liable.
- A related arbitration dispute (Paxson v. Live Nation, tied to Front Gate Ticketing) remains pending before the Ninth Circuit.

### Industry Problem
Mandatory arbitration clauses in consumer ticketing terms remain a major, unsettled legal battleground shaping whether patrons can bring class claims over fees, resale conduct, and other primary-platform disputes — a structural risk factor for any platform relying on click-through terms of use.

### Proposed Solution
No product solution is proposed in the article; for Etix, the relevant posture is Monitor — track how courts evaluate arbitration-provider rule revisions (discovery rights, appeal limits, provider independence) as a template for what terms-of-use provisions would or wouldn't hold up if challenged.

### Key Points
- SDNY compelled arbitration in two Ticketmaster secondary-market consumer antitrust suits under revised New Era ADR rules.
- Ruling explicitly does not overturn Heckman/Popp or affect the government antitrust case (jury already found liability in April).
- Broader antitrust remedies/penalties phase and a related Ninth Circuit arbitration appeal (Paxson) remain pending.

## A New York federal judge ordered secondary-market ticket plaintiffs into arbitration under New Era ADR’s revised rules, distinguishing the Ninth Circuit’s earlier rejection of Ticketmaster’s prior arbitration setup.

A federal judge in New York has ordered two consumer antitrust lawsuits against Live Nation Entertainment and Ticketmaster into arbitration, handing New Era ADR a win while leaving unresolved a wider fight over Ticketmaster’s use of mandatory arbitration clauses in disputes over its policies.

In a June 29 opinion (embedded below), U.S. District Judge Arun Subramanian of the Southern District of New York granted Live Nation and Ticketmaster’s motions to compel arbitration in *Jacobson v. Live Nation Entertainment* and *Leifer v. Live Nation Entertainment*, and stayed the litigation while those arbitrations proceed. The ruling covers claims brought by James R. Jacobson, Abraham Leifer and Tamara Stevens, who alleged antitrust violations against the entertainment giant in a lawsuit filed in 2024.

The plaintiffs’ claims differ from the government and state antitrust case against Live Nation and Ticketmaster. Subramanian noted that the plaintiffs in *Jacobson* and *Leifer* did not directly challenge Ticketmaster’s conduct in the primary ticketing market. Instead, their claims focused on alleged effects in the resale market, where tickets are bought and sold after their initial sale. Leifer and Stevens used StubHub to buy tickets originally sold through Ticketmaster, while Jacobson bought and resold a ticket through Ticketmaster and challenged what he described as a supracompetitive handling charge.

At the center of the ruling was Ticketmaster’s mandatory arbitration provision, which sent covered disputes to New Era ADR and included a delegation clause assigning threshold questions of arbitrability to the arbitrator. Subramanian found that the plaintiffs had agreed to Ticketmaster’s terms of use and failed to show that the delegation clause itself was unenforceable.

The decision was welcomed by New Era, [which issued a press release July 8](https://www.prnewswire.com/news-releases/federal-court-upholds-new-era-adrs-updated-procedures-in-live-nation-litigation-302820971.html) describing the opinion as one of the first to evaluate its revised procedures after changes made in response to earlier litigation. New Era CEO Collin Williams said the ruling reinforced that courts will evaluate arbitration programs based on the “procedures that actually exist today.”

Ticketmaster and New Era’s arbitration system previously suffered a major defeat in *Heckman v. Live Nation Entertainment*, where the [Ninth Circuit rejected Live Nation and Ticketmaster’s effort](https://www.ticketnews.com/2024/10/live-nation-arbitration-appeal-rejected/) to force a consumer antitrust class action into arbitration under earlier New Era rules. In that October 2024 opinion, the Ninth Circuit held that the delegation clause and arbitration agreement were unconscionable and unenforceable under California law. The appellate court identified several problems with the old New Era setup, including its mass-arbitration protocol, limited procedural rights such as lack of discovery, a limited right of appeal and arbitrator-selection provisions.

Subramanian did not treat *Heckman* as controlling the New York cases. Instead, he found that New Era’s newer 2024 rules were meaningfully different from the rules reviewed by the Ninth Circuit. The judge also emphasized that Live Nation and Ticketmaster stipulated that two controversial provisions — New Era’s mass-arbitration protocol and asymmetric appeal provision — would not apply in these arbitrations. The defendants also agreed that no neutral with an ownership stake in New Era would be appointed.

The court said the mass-arbitration and appeal provisions could be severed, and later concluded that New Era’s revised rules addressed many of the problems that had driven the Ninth Circuit’s earlier unconscionability analysis. Among the changes, the revised rules added an explicit right to discovery, removed prior limits on evidence, and replaced language that had allowed New Era to replace an arbitrator “at its sole discretion” with a narrower rank-and-strike replacement process.

The ruling also [followed limited discovery into plaintiffs’ allegations](https://www.ticketnews.com/2025/09/live-nation-latham-face-scrutiny-over-arbitration-ties-in-ticket-pricing-lawsuit/) about New Era’s relationship with Live Nation, Ticketmaster and Latham & Watkins, the law firm that has represented the ticketing companies. Plaintiffs had argued that New Era’s rules were tainted by Latham’s involvement and that the arbitration provider was too closely tied to Live Nation and Ticketmaster. Subramanian rejected that theory after discovery, saying the defendants are now “two of many” New Era clients and that the record did not show anything improper.

The court was especially direct on the Latham issue, writing that Jacobson had raised a serious concern that New Era’s rules were tainted by Latham’s role, but that discovery showed the concern was “a phantom.” The opinion said communications between New Era and Latham concerned contract negotiations, not evidence that Latham or the defendants authored New Era’s arbitration procedures. The opinion also characterized a subscription agreement between the defendants and New Era as involving a recurring fee and per-case fee, with nothing indicating an undue financial interest. That underlying agreement, however, is sealed, meaning the public record currently rests on the court’s description rather than the document itself.

The decision was not a sweeping finding that Ticketmaster’s arbitration practices are free from challenge. Subramanian acknowledged that some procedural unconscionability remained, including the adhesive nature of the consumer terms and the ability to change terms, but he found less procedural unconscionability than the Ninth Circuit found in *Heckman*. He also placed less weight on Ticketmaster’s ability to change contract terms retroactively, reasoning that users still had to click or sign in again to accept changes to Ticketmaster’s terms, and that plaintiffs had not shown that Live Nation or Ticketmaster controlled New Era’s rule changes.

The ruling also rejected a duress argument raised by Leifer and Stevens, who said they had effectively been forced to accept Ticketmaster’s terms in order to claim tickets they had already purchased through StubHub. Subramanian found that the plaintiffs had agreed to Ticketmaster’s terms on other occasions and that “losing out on a concert ticket” was not enough to establish duress under California law. The judge noted that StubHub, not Ticketmaster, brokered those resale transactions.

For Live Nation, Ticketmaster and New Era, the opinion provides a useful answer to one of the central criticisms that emerged after *Heckman*: that New Era’s structure and rules were fundamentally designed to protect corporate defendants from large-scale consumer claims. For plaintiffs and consumer advocates, the ruling is more limited. It applies to specific secondary-market claims, revised New Era rules and stipulations that removed some of the most controversial provisions from the arbitrations at issue.

## A wider arbitration fight

The New York decision lands in the middle of a broader legal fight over Ticketmaster’s online terms and arbitration strategy.

The most important prior case remains *Heckman*, now proceeding in district court as *Popp v. Live Nation Entertainment*. In *Heckman*, the Ninth Circuit rejected Ticketmaster’s prior New Era-based arbitration system, finding both the delegation clause and the arbitration agreement unconscionable under California law. The Supreme Court declined to review that decision on Oct. 6, 2025, leaving the Ninth Circuit ruling intact.

The underlying consumer class action in California is still moving forward. A class notice issued this year states that *Popp v. Live Nation Entertainment and Ticketmaster* is pending in the Central District of California and was previously captioned *Heckman*. The notice says the court has allowed legal claims to proceed as a nationwide class action for certain U.S. consumers who bought primary tickets and paid associated fees for concert events at major concert venues since 2010. The class excludes purchasers judicially determined in that case to be subject to an enforceable arbitration agreement governing the claims.

That exclusion is important for understanding the impact of the new SDNY ruling. Subramanian’s order sends the *Jacobson* and *Leifer* plaintiffs to arbitration, but it does not automatically remove anyone from the *Popp* class or undo the Ninth Circuit’s *Heckman* ruling. The *Popp* trial is currently set for July 6, 2027.

Ticketmaster’s current consumer terms raise a separate set of issues. Ticketmaster’s posted Terms of Use now list an effective date of Aug. 12, 2025, and prior TicketNews coverage found that the [company’s update moved disputes into JAMS-administered arbitration](https://www.ticketnews.com/2025/09/new-ticketmaster-tcs-very-close-to-line-of-violating-arbitration-judgement/) while preserving a class-action waiver and broad arbitration language.

The New York ruling does not directly validate those newer JAMS-based terms. It evaluates New Era’s revised 2024 procedures in the context of the *Jacobson* and *Leifer* cases. Still, the decision gives Live Nation and other companies a new argument: that arbitration providers can revise rules after adverse decisions and later win enforcement if courts find the revised procedures fair enough.

Another related dispute is pending in the Ninth Circuit. In *Paxson v. Live Nation Entertainment*, a ticket buyer challenging claims tied to the canceled 2022 Lovers & Friends festival is appealing an order compelling arbitration against Live Nation affiliates, including Front Gate Ticketing Solutions. [The June 23 Ninth Circuit argument](https://www.ticketnews.com/2026/06/ninth-circuit-again-presses-live-nation-over-ticket-buyer-arbitration-terms/) focused on Nevada contract law, Front Gate’s terms and whether the companies’ layered online agreements created an enforceable arbitration obligation. No decision was issued at argument.

The SDNY ruling may be cited in *Paxson*, but it does not control that case. *Paxson* is before the Ninth Circuit, involves different contract terms and appears to turn on different formation and consideration issues. It is another sign, however, that courts continue to scrutinize how Live Nation-controlled ticketing platforms use consumer terms to route disputes away from court.

The government and state antitrust cases against Live Nation and Ticketmaster are separate from the consumer arbitration fight. Government plaintiffs are not bound by Ticketmaster’s consumer Terms of Use, so Subramanian’s arbitration ruling does not affect those claims. The broader antitrust docket has also changed substantially: the DOJ has filed Tunney Act materials and a proposed final judgment with settling plaintiffs, while a coalition led by New York and other states continued to trial and won a jury verdict in April finding Live Nation and Ticketmaster liable for violating federal and state antitrust laws. Remedies and financial penalties are to be addressed separately.

Taken together, the cases show that Ticketmaster’s arbitration policies remain a major legal battleground even as Live Nation and Ticketmaster face broader antitrust scrutiny. The SDNY ruling is a meaningful win for New Era and for Live Nation’s effort to enforce arbitration in the *Jacobson* and *Leifer* cases. But it is not a reversal of *Heckman*, not a ruling on Ticketmaster’s current JAMS-based terms, and not a bar to the government or state antitrust claims.

The immediate takeaway for the ticketing industry is narrower but still significant: after *Heckman*, arbitration clauses tied to ticket purchases are not dead. But courts are now examining the exact provider rules, the record behind those rules, and the particular claims at issue before deciding whether consumers can be forced out of public court.
