# Pou’s HOLDBACKS Act Targets Deceptive Hidden Ticket Inventory, Manufactured Scarcity

## Summary

Rep. Nellie Pou (D-NJ) introduced the HOLDBACKS Act (H.R. 9762) on July 16, which would require primary ticket sellers to disclose general-public inventory and full advertised pricing before onsale, and would prohibit intentionally withholding tickets or misrepresenting availability to inflate prices. The bill, referred to the House Energy and Commerce Committee, is explicitly framed as targeting the primary market rather than resale, distinguishing it from the TICKET Act and other resale-focused proposals. Pou cites FIFA's opaque, phased World Cup ticket releases — including nearly 1,200 Category 2 seats at $7,380 each dropped shortly before the July 19 final — as the immediate example, alongside older findings that Ticketmaster and other primary sellers routinely hold back large shares of inventory.

### Key Points
- H.R. 9762 ("Halting Organizers and Live-Event Distributors from Boosting Artificial Capacity with Known Seating Act") was introduced July 16, 2026, with Pou as sole listed sponsor; official bill text was not yet public as of July 20.
- Would require advance disclosure of general-public ticket counts and full advertised cost before onsale, and would ban intentional withholding/misleading scarcity claims meant to drive up prices.
- Does not appear to ban all holds — artist, promoter, venue, sponsor, and hospitality allocations remain standard; enforcement agency, penalty structure, and definitions are undefined pending bill text.
- Cites a 2016 NY AG investigation finding over half of tickets for many events withheld from public sale (85%+ for some), and a 2020 Hawaii audit finding Ticketmaster exceeded contractual hold limits.
- References unsealed internal Live Nation communications from the federal antitrust case showing employees discussing large unused hold pools and moving lower-priced seats (e.g., $48.50 face value) into $117.50 dynamically-priced Platinum inventory.
- Framed as a "limited companion" to the TICKET Act, shifting scrutiny from secondary-market resale practices to primary-market inventory transparency.

### Highlights
- H.R. 9762 introduced July 16, 2026; referred to House Energy and Commerce Committee; no bill text or enforcement mechanism public yet as of July 20.
- Would mandate pre-onsale disclosure of public ticket counts and pricing, and prohibit intentional scarcity manipulation by primary sellers.
- FIFA released ~1,200 Category 2 tickets at $7,380 each just before the July 19 World Cup final despite apparent sellout signals — cited as the bill's motivating example.
- Historical precedent: 2016 NY AG probe (50%+ holdbacks industry-wide, 85%+ for some events) and 2020 Hawaii audit of Ticketmaster hold-limit violations.

### Industry Problem
The bill evidences a regulatory shift toward scrutinizing primary sellers' own inventory-management and disclosure practices — not just resale — meaning platforms like Etix could face new pre-onsale transparency and anti-manipulation obligations even though Etix does not itself withhold inventory to inflate dynamic pricing the way Ticketmaster's Platinum program does.

### Proposed Solution
The bill itself proposes mandatory disclosure of public inventory and pricing before onsale plus a ban on deceptive scarcity tactics; since this is Improve/Monitor-tagged rather than build-triggering today, Etix should watch whether the bill advances out of committee or gains bill text, and be prepared to formalize existing straightforward inventory practices into a compliance-ready disclosure feature if similar state or federal transparency mandates gain traction.

Rep. The bill was referred to the House Energy and Commerce Committee. Congressional records list Pou as its sole sponsor at introduction.

Unlike many of the ticketing proposals advanced by lawmakers and live-event lobbying groups in recent years, the HOLDBACKS Act is aimed squarely at decisions made within the primary market.

Pou’s office says ticket sellers would have to disclose before an onsale how many tickets will be offered to the general public and the full advertised cost of those tickets. Once sales begin, sellers would be prohibited from intentionally withholding seats or misleading buyers about the remaining supply in order to increase prices.

“As Americans know all too well, buying tickets has become an expensive and frustrating experience,” Pou said in announcing the measure. She described customers watching available seats disappear from an online map, feeling pressured to buy before an apparent sellout and sometimes turning to higher-priced resale listings — only for additional primary inventory to appear later.

“People wait in long online queues, and when they can finally see a seating chart and watch the blue dots disappear, they feel pressure to buy tickets they think are almost sold out, sometimes at prices higher than they wanted to spend… Then, miraculously, more seats to that show or that sporting event that everyone thought was sold out appear and change the marketplace again. Consumers deserve honesty, not marketplace manipulation or ripoff games. The HOLDBACKS Act will shine a light on ticket sales so families can compete on a level playing field instead of wondering whether the deck was stacked against them from the start.“

The proposal does not appear to prohibit all ticket holds. Artist, promoter, venue, sponsor, league, team, hospitality and production allocations are standard components of major-event ticketing, and some inventory legitimately becomes available only after staging configurations or other operational requirements are finalized.

Instead, Pou’s announcement frames the prohibition around intentional withholding or misleading scarcity used to push prices higher. Precisely where the bill draws that line will be central to whether it can meaningfully change industry behavior without interfering with legitimate event operations.

## Bill Text and Enforcement Details Remain Outstanding

The official bill text had not been posted by Congress or the major legislative tracking services as of July 20. As a result, several important provisions cannot yet be evaluated, including how the measure defines a primary ticket seller, what evidence would establish an intent to inflate prices, which inventory categories must be disclosed and whether artists, teams, promoters or venues would share responsibility with ticketing platforms.

The available materials also do not identify an enforcement agency, civil penalty structure, private right of action or process for correcting an inventory disclosure when event configurations change after an onsale begins.

Those details will determine whether the HOLDBACKS Act functions primarily as a transparency requirement or gives regulators a practical mechanism for challenging inventory-management strategies.

A disclosure limited to the number of tickets designated for the general public, for example, could provide consumers with useful information while still leaving the size of artist, sponsor, hospitality and other reserved allocations undisclosed. A broader requirement showing both available and withheld inventory would offer a more complete picture of how much of a venue is actually being placed on sale.

## FIFA’s World Cup Sales Provide the Immediate Example

Pou specifically pointed to FIFA’s handling of ticket inventory for the 2026 World Cup, saying the organization’s opaque release process had frustrated fans and drawn renewed attention to holdbacks.

FIFA sold tickets through a succession of sales phases without providing buyers with a clear accounting of how much inventory remained under its control. Matches and price categories frequently appeared unavailable before additional tickets or entire seating areas emerged later.

The pattern became especially visible before the July 19 World Cup final at MetLife Stadium. FIFA released 1,178 Category 2 tickets at $7,380 each shortly before the match, even though the event had at times appeared sold out through its last-minute sales portal. It simultaneously offered additional Category 1 and hospitality inventory carrying substantially higher prices.

Late ticket releases do not by themselves demonstrate deliberate manipulation. Sponsor returns, unused team allocations, production changes and hospitality inventory can all place seats back into public circulation near an event.

But the appearance of nearly 1,200 similarly priced tickets at once showed that the inventory previously visible to consumers did not represent the full supply still controlled by FIFA. Without advance disclosures, buyers had no reliable way to know whether they were looking at the final remaining seats or only the portion FIFA had chosen to release at that stage.

That uncertainty can affect both primary and secondary prices. A customer who believes an event is nearly sold out may pay more immediately, purchase a dynamically priced ticket or move to a resale marketplace. Sellers and speculative buyers also make pricing decisions without knowing how much competing inventory the event organizer may release later.

## Holdbacks Extend Well Beyond the World Cup

The World Cup provides a timely political opening for Pou’s bill, but hidden ticket allocations have been documented throughout concerts and professional sports.

A 2016 investigation by the New York attorney general found that more than half of the tickets for many high-profile events were withheld from the general public. Examples included concerts for which 85% or more of the venue inventory was allocated to holds rather than the initial public sale. A later New York legislative report warned that held tickets could be released through primary vendors at higher prices after scarcity had already been established.

A 2020 audit involving a Hawaii venue similarly found that ticket holds could consume the overwhelming majority of inventory for some events and that Ticketmaster had exceeded contractual limits on holds in multiple cases.

More recently, internal Live Nation communications unsealed during the federal antitrust case against the company offered a rare view of how artist, venue, sponsor, VIP and Platinum allocations can leave little standard inventory available when consumers enter an onsale.

The communications did not establish that every sparse Ticketmaster seat map was deliberately created to manufacture demand. They did, however, show employees discussing large pools of held seats, holds that frequently went unused and premium locations that could be moved into dynamically priced Platinum inventory. In one exchange, a Live Nation employee said lower-priced seats originally carrying a $48.50 price had been sold by the Platinum team for $117.50.

Those examples illustrate the distinction the HOLDBACKS Act will have to navigate. Holds can serve legitimate purposes, but they also shape the supply presented to consumers, the urgency surrounding a sale and the inventory available for premium pricing.

## A Different Focus From Resale-Centered Ticket Legislation

Pou describes the bill as a limited companion to the TICKET Act, which passed the House with provisions addressing all-in pricing, deceptive speculative ticket listings and other consumer practices. The HOLDBACKS Act would instead address information that buyers receive about the underlying primary supply.

That distinction is significant in a legislative environment where much of the live-event industry’s lobbying campaign has focused on limiting resale prices, restricting ticket transfers and regulating brokers.

Resale practices remain a major source of consumer complaints, but resale-focused legislation generally does not regulate how primary sellers set initial prices, divide inventory among internal allocations, operate dynamic-pricing programs or decide when additional seats will be released.

The HOLDBACKS Act approaches ticket affordability from the opposite direction: before blaming elevated prices exclusively on secondary sellers, it asks whether consumers received an accurate picture of the primary inventory in the first place.

Its practical effect will depend on language that has not yet become public. Lawmakers will need to decide whether disclosure means only a single aggregate number or a detailed accounting of inventory categories; whether ticket sellers must update those figures as holds are released; and how regulators would distinguish intentional scarcity from ordinary changes in event production.

Even as a narrowly framed proposal, however, H.R. 9762 directs federal attention toward one of the least transparent portions of the ticket-buying process: not merely what a ticket costs, but how many tickets consumers were ever given a fair opportunity to buy.
