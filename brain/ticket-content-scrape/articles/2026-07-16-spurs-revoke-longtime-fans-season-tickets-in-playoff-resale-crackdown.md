# Spurs Revoke Longtime Fans’ Season Tickets in Playoff Resale Crackdown

## Summary

The San Antonio Spurs revoked season-ticket memberships from several longtime holders during and after the team's run to the 2026 NBA Finals, citing violations of resale restrictions. Rodolfo "Rudy" Lopez, a 15-year season-ticket holder, was removed from Frost Bank Center during Game 2 and banned from Spurs Sports & Entertainment properties for a year after admitting he resold tickets via online raffles outside Ticketmaster, the team's authorized resale channel; other longtime members say they used the Ticketmaster-linked Spurs Account Manager yet still lost memberships without a clear explanation. The article situates the crackdown within a broader pattern — the Warriors, Patriots, Packers, and Dodgers have done similarly — arguing teams tighten resale control once a winning season makes season-ticket inventory commercially valuable, serving revenue and data-capture interests as much as fan protection.

### Key Points
- Rudy Lopez, a 15-year Spurs season-ticket holder, had his 4 seats revoked and was banned one year from SS&E properties for reselling via unauthorized online raffles.
- Rojelio Lopez and another member ("Amalia"), both 10+ year holders, say they used the Ticketmaster-linked Spurs Account Manager yet still had memberships terminated without clear cause.
- Spurs warned members ahead of the NBA Finals (vs. the New York Knicks) that resale must go through the team's official marketplace, citing home-court-advantage concerns.
- Historical parallel: the Golden State Warriors and Ticketmaster allegedly canceled tickets and threatened accounts over off-platform resale during Golden State's championship rise; the New England Patriots and Green Bay Packers (starting 2025) did similarly.
- The LA Dodgers previously canceled thousands of season-ticket accounts in one sweep, reallocating them to resale-consolidation partner Eventellect, which profited directly.
- Demand and resale prices reportedly surged once Victor Wembanyama led the Spurs to the Finals, turning season-ticket holders into "competing suppliers" in the resale market.

### Highlights
- Teams are using season-ticket-agreement enforcement to funnel resale exclusively through authorized, Ticketmaster-linked exchanges once postseason demand spikes.
- Enforcement is inconsistently explained even to members who believe they used sanctioned channels, undermining trust in the resale rules.
- Historical precedent (Warriors/Ticketmaster, Patriots, Packers, Dodgers/Eventellect) shows this is a recurring industry pattern tied to revenue capture on the secondary market, not an isolated incident.

### Industry Problem
Patrons face opaque, inconsistently enforced resale rules that shift from "loyalty perk" to "revocable privilege" exactly when their inventory becomes valuable, and even authorized-channel use doesn't reliably protect them — a trust and transparency gap between team/venue resale policy and season-ticket holders.

### Proposed Solution
Teams' own answer is contractual: force all resale through an authorized, team-controlled marketplace (largely Ticketmaster) to capture visibility, buyer data, and transaction revenue. An independent platform like Etix could differentiate by offering clients transparent, rules-as-code resale enforcement — clear audit trails and member-facing violation explanations alongside configurable authorized-resale tooling — that avoids the ambiguity and backlash seen here while still giving venues legitimate inventory control.

The San Antonio Spurs [revoked season-ticket memberships held by several longtime fans](https://www.expressnews.com/news/article/rudy-lopez-spurs-season-tickets-revoked-fan-ban-22319664.php) during and immediately after the team’s run to the 2026 NBA Finals, accusing the account holders of violating restrictions on ticket resale.

The crackdown included Rodolfo “Rudy” Lopez, who had held Spurs season tickets for 15 years. Security personnel removed Lopez and his 12-year-old son from Frost Bank Center during Game 2 of the NBA Finals, and the organization subsequently revoked his four seats and banned him from Spurs Sports & Entertainment properties for one year.

Lopez acknowledged offering tickets through online raffles outside Ticketmaster, the Spurs’ authorized resale marketplace. The team’s ticket terms prohibit raffles, auctions and sales through unauthorized secondary platforms.

Other affected members, however, say they still do not know what they did wrong.

Rojelio Lopez, another 15-year season-ticket holder who is not related to Rudy Lopez, said he transferred or resold his tickets through the Spurs Account Manager connected to Ticketmaster. A third member, identified by the San Antonio Express-News only as Amalia, similarly said she used the authorized marketplace before the organization terminated a membership she had held for more than a decade.

The Spurs reportedly provided those members with little detail beyond references to the organization’s ticket terms. Rojelio Lopez said his ticket representative would no longer speak with him, while Amalia said the team rejected her challenge without explaining which transactions it considered improper.

The organization has pointed to terms allowing it to monitor resale activity and cancel memberships when tickets are raffled, auctioned or sold through unauthorized marketplaces. The Spurs also warned members before the NBA Finals that tickets had to be resold through the team’s official marketplace, citing its interest in preserving a home-court advantage against the New York Knicks.

Those explanations are not unique to San Antonio. Teams commonly argue that controlling ticket transfers helps prevent fraud, identify the people entering their buildings, enforce bans against problem patrons and keep opposing fans from overwhelming the home crowd.

But the timing of such enforcement also exposes a less frequently acknowledged commercial interest: controlling the supply of tickets entering the resale market.

That interest becomes substantially more valuable when a team is winning.

Season-ticket holders commit to full or partial packages before knowing how the team will perform or what individual games will be worth. During losing seasons, they bear the risk that unwanted tickets will sell below their allocated cost—or will not sell at all. Rudy Lopez said that during the Spurs’ six-year run of losing seasons, he sometimes struggled even to give away his seats.

The economics changed when Victor Wembanyama and the Spurs reached the NBA Finals. Demand surged, lower-level seats traded for multiples of their original prices and season-ticket holders suddenly controlled valuable inventory.

At that point, those members were no longer merely dependable customers who had financed seats through lean seasons. They were also competing suppliers in a marketplace where the team had its own financial and strategic interests.

Requiring every resale to flow through an authorized exchange gives a team greater visibility into the market, access to buyer information and potential participation in transaction revenue. It also allows the organization to dictate when, where and under what conditions season-ticket inventory may compete with tickets being sold or distributed by the team itself.

The Spurs are far from the first successful franchise to tighten its grip on season-ticket inventory when demand surges. During the Golden State Warriors’ rise a decade ago, StubHub alleged that the club and Ticketmaster had canceled some regular-season and playoff tickets – and threatened season-ticket holders with losing their accounts – if they sold through competing marketplaces. The New England Patriots regularly revoked season tickets for unauthorized resale during the franchise’s championship era, while the Green Bay Packers began canceling accounts in 2025 over resale behavior.

In at least some instances, tickets cancelled in these sweeps were redirected to marketplace partners, rather than to other interested season ticket holders. The Los Angeles Dodgers [famously cancelled thousands of season ticket accounts in one swoop](https://www.ticketnews.com/2018/02/la-dodgers-eventellect-record-breaking-deal/), allocating them to a resale consolidation company to sell at market rates with the organization directly profiting.

That does not invalidate the Spurs’ contractual case against members who clearly used prohibited raffles or unauthorized marketplaces. But it complicates the broader suggestion that these restrictions exist primarily to protect fans or preserve atmosphere.

The recurring friction is that teams expect season-ticket holders to absorb the downside of a struggling product while reserving extensive control over the upside when success creates a valuable resale market. Fans are encouraged to think of season tickets as a long-term investment in loyalty—until the tickets become valuable enough that the organization begins treating their use as a revocable privilege.
