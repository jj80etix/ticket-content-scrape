# UK Watchdog Reportedly Gearing Up Major Inquiry Into Live Nation’s Market Power

## Summary

The UK's Competition and Markets Authority (CMA) is reportedly reaching out to music-industry figures, including Live Nation critics, as it considers a formal inquiry into Live Nation's integrated position across promotion, ticketing (Ticketmaster), venues, festivals, and data in the UK. The move follows a May report from Parliament's Business and Trade Committee calling for a full market investigation before end of 2026, which found a "climate of fear" deterring public criticism of dominant firms and cited allegations that Live Nation controlled 58% (66% including affiliates) of the UK's 23.1 million primary tickets sold in 2025. The CMA has not confirmed a formal probe but says it is "giving active and careful consideration to undertaking markets work in this area"; Live Nation disputes the claims and says artists set prices and take the majority of concert revenue.

- CMA has begun contacting music-industry figures, including critics, ahead of a possible formal inquiry into Live Nation.
- Parliament's Business and Trade Committee report (May 2026) called for a full market investigation into the live music sector before end of 2026.

- Allegations cited: Live Nation controlled 58% of the UK's 23.1 million primary tickets sold in 2025 (66% including affiliated companies).
- CMA previously said it likely needs statutory powers (formal market study or investigation) to obtain internal documents and commercial-agreement data.
- Live Nation disputes the findings, says artists determine ticket prices and receive the majority of concert revenue, and declined further comment.
- No formal investigation has been announced; CMA gave no timetable for its promised response to Parliament.

### Highlights
- CMA is directly contacting industry figures (including critics) as a precursor to a possible formal inquiry — a step beyond its earlier public statements.
- Parliament's committee recommended a full market investigation be opened before the end of 2026, examining promotion, ticketing, venues, festivals, artist development, and data together.

## The Competition and Markets Authority has begun contacting music-industry figures as it considers a broad examination of Live Nation’s reach across promotion, ticketing, venues and festivals, according to The Times.

The United Kingdom’s competition regulator is reportedly laying the groundwork for a major inquiry into Live Nation Entertainment and the company’s power across the country’s live music industry.

The Competition and Markets Authority has begun contacting music-industry figures, including critics of Live Nation, to arrange meetings as it considers a broader examination of the market, [according to The Times](https://www.thetimes.com/business/companies-markets/article/ticketing-live-nation-investigation-g26kwpjlm).

The reported outreach represents the clearest indication yet that the CMA may be moving toward formal scrutiny of Live Nation’s integrated position across concert promotion, Ticketmaster, venues, festivals and other parts of the live entertainment business.

## Reported Inquiry Would Examine Live Nation’s Reach

The Times frames the emerging inquiry around concerns that Live Nation may hold a dominant and controlling position across the UK live music market.

Live Nation is the world’s largest concert promoter and the owner of Ticketmaster. Its UK operations extend beyond promotion and primary ticketing to include interests in venues, festivals, artist management, sponsorship, merchandising and other live-event services.

That reach has prompted concerns that Live Nation’s position in one part of the business can reinforce its power elsewhere.

A promoter that controls access to major tours may be able to direct events toward affiliated venues. Venue ownership can strengthen the position of an affiliated ticketing platform. Ticketing operations can produce customer data that may benefit promotion, marketing and other businesses within the same corporate group.

The UK Parliament’s Business and Trade Committee has described those relationships as central to determining whether competition is functioning properly.

In [a report issued in May](https://committees.parliament.uk/publications/53229/documents/297817/default/?utm_source=chatgpt.com), the committee called for the CMA to conduct a full market investigation into the live music sector before the end of 2026. It said the regulator should pay particular attention to Live Nation’s position and conduct across promotion, ticketing, venues, festivals, artist development and data.

The committee did not conclude that Live Nation had violated competition law. Instead, it found that the company’s scale, the limited availability of reliable market data and the interconnected nature of the industry warranted a formal investigation with stronger information-gathering powers.

“Anything less than a market investigation would be insufficient to fully understand the extent of alleged competition harms,” the committee said.

## Parliament Found a ‘Climate of Fear’

The committee’s most striking finding concerned the reluctance of artists, managers, promoters and other industry participants to criticize dominant companies publicly.

Of the 45 written submissions received during the inquiry, a significant number were submitted anonymously or confidentially because contributors feared possible retaliation.

Committee Chair Liam Byrne [described that reluctance as evidence of a “climate of fear”](https://committees.parliament.uk/committee/365/business-and-trade-committee/news/213684/committee-alarmed-by-climate-of-fear-around-dominant-firms-in-uk-music-industry/) surrounding the largest companies in the UK music industry.

Evidence presented to Parliament included allegations that Live Nation’s scale makes it difficult for artists and managers to operate independently of its businesses; that restrictive agreements can link access to particular venues and festivals; and that Live Nation-controlled venues may favor affiliated promotion and ticketing operations.

The committee also cited evidence alleging that Live Nation directly controlled 58% of the 23.1 million primary tickets offered in the UK during 2025. That share increased to 66% when tickets controlled by affiliated companies were included.

Additional concerns involved the allocation of valuable ticket inventory, restrictions directing resale toward Ticketmaster’s own platform and the collection of customer data through Ticketmaster systems.

The committee argued that conventional market-share figures may not fully capture the power created by operating across several connected parts of the live music business.

That is one reason the reported CMA meetings could be consequential. Direct conversations with industry participants may give the regulator access to evidence that witnesses were unwilling to present publicly to Parliament.

## CMA Previously Signaled Formal Powers May Be Necessary

The [CMA’s written submission to the committee](https://committees.parliament.uk/writtenevidence/161882/html/) earlier this year outlined why any meaningful examination of the industry would likely need to become a formal process.

The regulator acknowledged that its previous ticketing cases had focused largely on consumer-facing practices and did not directly address concerns about market structure, market power or integration across the live events value chain.

It also said that accurate public data about the structure of the industry is limited and that commercial relationships between promotion, ticketing, venues and other businesses are difficult to assess from public information alone.

As a result, the CMA said it would likely need statutory powers to obtain internal documents, market data and information about commercial agreements.

Using those powers would generally require the regulator to open a formal market study or a more extensive market investigation approved by the CMA Board.

A market study would allow the CMA to examine whether competition may not be functioning properly and determine whether further action is needed. A full market investigation could lead to recommendations, negotiated undertakings or remedies imposed directly on businesses.

Those remedies can range from improved transparency and changes to contract terms to measures designed to alter the competitive structure of a market.

In the most aggressive circumstances, structural remedies such as divestitures can be considered. There is no indication that the CMA has reached any conclusions about remedies involving Live Nation, and such action would follow only after a formal inquiry and findings supported by evidence.

## Board Consideration Was Expected in Spring

The CMA said in its January submission that it expected its Board to consider during the spring whether to undertake markets work in the live events sector.

That review was expected to account for the parliamentary committee’s findings, the importance of the creative industries to the UK economy and developments in the U.S. antitrust case involving Live Nation and Ticketmaster.

The CMA’s latest response does not say whether that Board consideration occurred or whether a decision was reached.

It also does not provide a timetable for the regulator’s promised response to Parliament.

The Times’ reporting of meetings with industry figures may indicate that the CMA is gathering information to help determine the scope and form of a potential inquiry. The regulator could still choose a narrower market study, a broader investigation or another form of markets work.

Any formal inquiry would likely extend beyond Live Nation alone and examine competition throughout the live music supply chain. But Live Nation’s scale and vertical integration would place the company at the center of that examination.

## Live Nation Rejects Claims of Market Harm

Live Nation has disputed the parliamentary committee’s findings and denied that its business structure is responsible for the challenges facing the UK live music industry.

The company has said that artists and their representatives determine ticket prices and typically receive the majority of concert revenue. It has also pointed to the financial risks associated with concert promotion, including events that lose money.

Live Nation has challenged descriptions of its market share in UK venues and festivals and argued that the evidence does not support the conclusion that vertical integration has harmed competition.

The company declined additional comment to The Times regarding the reported CMA activity.

The question now is whether the regulator’s reported industry outreach will result in a formal inquiry capable of testing those competing claims.

For Live Nation, such an investigation could subject the relationships between its promotion, ticketing, venue and festival businesses to the most extensive regulatory examination they have yet faced in the UK.

For the broader industry, it could finally produce the market data and internal evidence needed to determine whether Live Nation’s integrated model creates efficiencies—or gives the company power that rivals, artists and independent operators cannot realistically avoid.
