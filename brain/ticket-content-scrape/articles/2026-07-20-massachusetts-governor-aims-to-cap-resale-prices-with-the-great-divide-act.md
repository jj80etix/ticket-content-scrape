# Massachusetts Governor aims to cap resale prices with ‘The Great Divide Act’

## Summary

Massachusetts Governor Maura Healey plans to include "The Great Divide Act" — named after Noah Kahan's album — in her upcoming closeout supplemental budget. The bill would cap concert and sports ticket resale prices at 110% of face value and limit resale-platform service fees to 10% of the total ticket price, while also banning speculative ticket listings (reselling tickets the seller doesn't yet own) and prohibiting deceptive resale websites and misleading availability claims. It follows a similar resale price-cap push in Vermont, also linked to Kahan's advocacy.

### Key Points
- Resale price cap proposed at 110% of original face value (e.g., a $100 ticket capped at $110 resale).
- Resale service-fee cap proposed at 10% of the total ticket price (e.g., $30 max fee on a $300 resale ticket).
- Bill would ban speculative ticket sales (listing tickets the seller doesn't own yet).
- Bill would prohibit deceptive resale websites and misleading ticket-availability claims.
- Targets resale platforms such as StubHub and SeatGeek.
- Follows Vermont's related resale price-cap effort earlier in 2026.
- To be introduced via Healey's closeout supplemental budget, not a standalone bill vote.

### Highlights
- Proposed caps: 110% of face value on resale price, 10% of ticket price on resale service fees.
- Speculative-ticket-sale ban and anti-deceptive-listing provisions included.
- Introduced through the Governor's closeout supplemental budget vehicle (no passage date given).
- Follows Vermont's 2026 resale price-cap effort, signaling a spreading state-by-state pattern.

### Industry Problem
Evidences an accelerating state legislative push — following Vermont — to cap secondary-market prices and fees and outlaw speculative listings, a structural threat to resale-platform economics that also sets consumer-protection norms (guaranteed ticket transfer, no deceptive listings) states may extend toward primary sellers too.

### Proposed Solution
The bill itself is the described remedy (price/fee caps, speculative-sale ban, deceptive-listing prohibition); this is a monitor item for Etix — watch for the bill's passage and its exact statutory definitions (what counts as a "service fee," enforcement mechanism), since a Massachusetts precedent could accelerate similar bills in other states where Etix operates.
