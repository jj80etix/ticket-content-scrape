# Meta rolls back Muse AI tool after talent agency outcry

## Summary

Meta disabled a feature of its new Muse Image AI generator days after launch following backlash from talent agencies CAA and UTA and performers' union SAG-AFTRA, who objected to the tool's automatic opt-in of public Instagram accounts for AI image generation. The controversy echoes earlier industry pushback against OpenAI's Sora 2 video tool over unauthorized use of artists' likenesses.

### Key Points
- Muse Image launched July 7 with public adult Instagram accounts automatically opted in; private and under-18 accounts were excluded.
- CAA and UTA publicly criticized the lack of consent, credit, and compensation; SAG-AFTRA called the opt-in default "unacceptable."
- Meta disabled the feature, saying it "missed the mark," while retaining other parts of the Muse Image/Video tools (which carry invisible watermarks).
- Artists including Taylor Swift, the Backstreet Boys, and Lionel Richie have separately pursued trademark protections against AI voice/likeness replicas.

Meta has rolled back a feature within its first AI image generator tool, Muse, after organisations like CAA and UTA hit out at the unapproved usage of artists’ likenesses.

The technology giant behind Facebook, Instagram and Threads announced it had disabled part of the tool that “ [missed the mark](https://aibusiness.com/generative-ai/key-feature-meta-s-muse-image-axed) ” less than a week after launching Muse Image. It was designed as part of the [first media-generation model](https://ai.meta.com/blog/introducing-muse-image-muse-video-msl/) from Meta’s Superintelligence Labs, with the feature allowing the creation of images by utilising content from public Instagram accounts.

When Muse Image launched last week (7 July), public Instagram accounts for users aged over 18 were automatically opted-in, with private accounts or under-18s excluded.

Meta was quickly hit with widespread pushback over privacy concerns, reminiscent of the blowback OpenAI faced with its [Sora 2](https://www.iqmagazine.com/2025/10/talent-agencies-hit-out-at-openais-sora-2/) video generation tool, which ultimately led to the company’s abandonment of the product.

Following the launch of Muse, both CAA and UTA spoke out about the potential pitfalls of image generation for their artists, as artists like [Taylor Swift, the Backstreet Boys, and Lionel Richie](https://www.musicbusinessworldwide.com/backstreet-boys-file-to-trademark-the-sound-of-their-voices-against-ai-replicas-joining-taylor-swift-and-lionel-richie/) move to protect themselves against deepfake content.

“Artists deserve to decide if and how their likeness and work is used, with consent and the ability to set their own terms,” a CAA spokesperson said, per [*Deadline*](https://deadline.com/2026/07/meta-removes-muse-image-ai-feature-backlash-1236979605/). “This means letting creators impose restrictions, monitor usage, and prevent unauthorised endorsements or exploitation.

“Responsible AI requires clear disclosures and swift removal of unauthorised content. There must be easy ways to spot, track, and take down misuse, and it should be clear when something is AI-generated. CAA believes in the power of new technology, but not at the cost of individuals’ rights or livelihoods. The future of creativity depends on respecting the ownership and autonomy of those who make it possible,” they said.

> “The use of such property without opt-in consent, credit and compensation is exploitation, not innovation”

Similarly, UTA was critical of the tool being automatically applied across all public Instagram users, with the agency representative calling it “exploitation”.

“The use of such property without opt-in consent, credit and compensation is exploitation, not innovation,” UTA said, per [*The Los Angeles Times*](https://www.latimes.com/entertainment-arts/business/story/2026-07-09/meta-muse-image-ai-hollywood).

Performers’ labour union Screen Actors Guild (SAG-AFTRA) was also critical of the feature’s automatic opt-in, calling it “unacceptable” in a statement.

“Anything other than a clear and conspicuous opt-in for these types of uses of Instagram users’ images is unacceptable, and an utter miscalculation of public sentiment regarding the obvious dangers and harms inherent in such use,” the union [said](https://www.theguardian.com/technology/2026/jul/11/meta-ditches-muse-image-ai-feature-instagram-privacy).

Despite saying that the feature was built with “strong controls and safety guardrails from day one,” Meta ultimately disabled the feature days after its announcement.

“Our intent was to provide a useful creative tool and to give people control ⁠over whether their public content could be referenced in this way. We’ve heard the feedback that ⁠this feature missed the mark, so it’s no longer available,” Meta said.

But it is only one part of Meta’s Muse Image and Video tools, which are set to carry an invisible watermark to help differentiate them from real images.
