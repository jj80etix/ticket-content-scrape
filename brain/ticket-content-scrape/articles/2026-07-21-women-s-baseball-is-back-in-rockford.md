# Women’s Baseball Is Back In Rockford

## Summary

The City of Rockford and the International Women's Baseball Center celebrated the reopening of Beyer Stadium, historic home of the Rockford Peaches ("A League of Their Own" fame), on July 18 as part of a multi-phase redevelopment called "A Place of Their Own." Phase one delivered new bleachers, restrooms, a resurfaced field, and restoration of the historic ticket booth; a larger second phase will add a museum and activity center, with the full campus targeted for completion in 2030.

### Key Points
- Beyer Stadium reopened July 18, 2026, timed to launch Women's Baseball Week in Rockford.
- Phase one renovation (~$5 million): new bleachers, modernized restrooms, resurfaced field, renovated dugouts, upgraded lighting and scoreboard, and restoration of the historic ticket booth.
- Phase two ($22.8 million): a museum and activity center; full IWBC campus targeted for 2030.
- Stadium was home to the Rockford Peaches (AAGPBL) from 1943-1954, winning four league titles.
- Events tied to the reopening include the WBSC Women's Baseball World Cup Group Stage and Baseball For All Nationals.
- The site dates to circa 1913 and was renamed Beyer Stadium in 1948 after longtime coach Charles Beyer.

ROCKFORD, ILLINOIS – 1944. The Rockford Peaches of the All American Girls Baseball League pose for a team portrait at home in 1944. (Photo by Mark Rucker/Transcendental Graphics, Getty Images)

One of the most historically significant women’s baseball venues in the United States is back thanks to efforts by the City of Rockford and the International Women’s Baseball Center, which is leading a multi-phase redevelopment of the former home of the Rockford Peaches – the team made famous in the film “A League Of Their Own.”

The campaign dubbed “A Place of Their Own” includes the recent renovation of Beyer Stadium at Maybelle Blair Park and further establishes Rockford, Illinois, as the international epicenter of women’s baseball.

The ballpark celebrated its official reopening on July 18. The first phase of the $5 renovation included new bleachers, modernized restrooms, resurfaced playing field, renovated dugouts, upgraded lighting, an improved scoreboard and restoration of the historic ticket booth. The $22.8 million second phase includes a museum and activity center. The completed IWBC campus is targeted for 2030 and is intended to become the international home for the history, development and promotion of women’s and girls’ baseball.

The reopening was timed to launch Women’s Baseball Week in Rockford, leading into several major events including the WBSC Women’s Baseball World Cup Group Stage; Baseball For All Nationals, the largest girls’ baseball tournament in the United States; and the reunion of former players of the All-American Girls Professional Baseball League (AAGPBL).

Beyer Stadium is not just a renovated ballpark it’s a heritage venue similar to historic sports sites like Fenway Park – the oldest, active MLB stadium – or the ballparks of the Negro League. The six-acre campus is preserving the story of women breaking barriers in professional sports. With growing attention on women’s baseball, Beyer Stadium is positioning itself as a national landmark as well as an active ballfield.

The site began around 1913 as Kishwaukee Park and was originally used for men’s baseball. It later became known as Rockford Municipal Stadium and was used for high school athletics, football, track and community events. It was renamed Beyer Stadium in 1948 in honor of Charles Beyer, a longtime Rockford high school athletics coach.

The stadium’s place in sports history was secured when it became home to the Rockford Peaches of the AAGPBL from 1943 through 1954. The Peaches played their home games at Beyer Stadium throughout the league’s 12-year existence. They were the only team in the league to win four titles in 1945, 1948, 1949 and 1950.

The team was created during World War II when Major League Baseball feared a shortage of players because many men were serving in the military. The league became a pioneering professional opportunity for women athletes and the team became a symbol of women’s athletic achievement.

After the AAGPBL ended, Beyer Stadium continued hosting local sports. Over time, the facility deteriorated and the grandstands were removed in the early 1990s. Interest surged following the movie and the stadium was renovated and rededicated in 2010, with former Peaches players honored during the celebration.

Today, the nonprofit IWBC is responsible for the field’s use, upkeep and transformation from a vacant lot into a major destination for women’s baseball. The park is named for Maybelle Blair, a former AAGPBL player and longtime advocate for women in the sport.
