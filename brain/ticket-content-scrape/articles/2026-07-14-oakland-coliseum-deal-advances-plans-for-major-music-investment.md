# Oakland Coliseum Deal Advances Plans for Major Music Investment

## Summary

The Oakland City Council voted to sell the city's 50% stake in the Coliseum complex for $125 million to Oakland Acquisition Company, affiliated with the African American Sports and Entertainment Group. The arena parcel is expected to be resold to Oak View Group, led by former Ticketmaster CEO Irving Azoff, for $100 million, funding renovations aimed at improving the concert and fan experience. Oakland will retain a share of future ticket revenue as it seeks to reposition itself as a music destination after losing its professional sports teams.

### Key Points
- Oakland City Council approved the sale 6-1; the city receives a $110M lump sum plus $15M in closing payments, with the deal expected to close as early as September (deadline January 2027).
- Oak View Group is expected to pay $100 million for the 9-acre arena parcel and invest "tens of millions" in concessions, amenities and operations.
- Oakland will receive 6% of future ticket revenue from events at the arena and stadium while they remain operational.
- The stadium parcel's $60M payment is structured over up to seven years, with a $5M deposit already made.

### Highlights
- Oak View Group (led by former Ticketmaster CEO Irving Azoff) is set to acquire and renovate Oakland Arena as part of a broader consolidation of venue ownership/management in the market.
- The city negotiated a 6% ticket-revenue share as part of the transfer, tying municipal finances directly to future ticketing activity at the venues.
- Deal structure (deferred stadium-parcel payments over seven years, January 2027 close deadline) signals a long transition period during which venue operations and vendor relationships may change hands.

### Industry Problem
The deal reflects ongoing consolidation of venue ownership and management under major live-event operators, which can reshape technology and ticketing-vendor decisions at newly-acquired venues as incoming operators standardize on their preferred platforms.

### Proposed Solution
Monitor whether OVG's Oakland Arena takeover includes a ticketing-platform RFP or vendor change, since incoming operators frequently re-bid ticketing contracts; the trigger for Etix to act would be an open technology/vendor selection process once OVG formally assumes arena operations.

Oakland officials have approved the sale of the city’s stake in the Coliseum complex. The $125 million deal could lead to major upgrades at Oakland Arena and bring more concerts to the East Bay.

The Oakland City Council voted 6-1 Monday to approve the transaction. The city will sell its 50% share of the 112-acre property to the Oakland Acquisition Company. The company is affiliated with the African American Sports and Entertainment Group.

Under the agreement, Oakland will receive a $110 million lump-sum payment. The parties also agreed to $15 million in additional closing payments. The sale could close as early as September. It must be completed by January 2027.

City Council President Kevin Jenkins said the deal gives Oakland a chance to build a new identity after losing several professional sports teams.

“I like to say Oakland has never won a sports deal … but I truly feel like we won this deal,” Jenkins said. “We get a bad rep for losing three sports teams. So we are making a transition into a music city, where acts of all kinds want to come and perform in our city.”

The property will be divided into two parcels. A nine-acre parcel includes Oakland Arena. A larger 103-acre parcel includes the Coliseum stadium, nearby land and parking lots.

Oakland Acquisition Company is expected to sell the arena parcel to Oak View Group for $100 million. Music executive Irving Azoff founded the venue management and development company. Azoff is also a former CEO of Ticketmaster.

Azoff joined Oakland officials after the council vote. He said Oak View Group plans to invest tens of millions of dollars in the arena.

The improvements would focus on concessions, amenities and building operations. They would also aim to improve the experience for fans attending concerts and other events.

“This is not a rescue mission in itself; it’s just an opportunity to take what I consider to be a B experience and make it an A-plus experience,” Azoff said. “The fan experience at the arena is going to be second to none.”

Oak View Group has worked on similar projects in other cities. Those projects include the Kia Forum in Inglewood, California, and CFG Bank Arena in Baltimore.

Azoff said the company will preserve what already works at Oakland Arena. It will also address areas that need improvement. Local businesses could be brought in to support concessions and other venue services. “Our opportunity is to respect what works, invest in what could be better, invest in the community and bring our unique artist relationships, both as a management company and as OVG, to get to the next chapter of this great building,” Azoff said.

Oakland will also receive 6% of future ticket revenue from events at the arena and stadium. The agreement applies while the venues remain operational. City officials said the revenue could support investment in East Oakland. That could include the Hegenberger Road corridor, which connects the Coliseum area to Oakland International Airport.

Councilmember Rowena Brown said the city hopes the money will help surrounding neighborhoods. “It is our hope that we can use that to help revitalize the Hegenberger Corridor, as well as help uplift East Oakland that has been underserved for so long,” Brown said.

Payment for the stadium parcel will take longer. The acquisition group agreed to pay $60 million for the city’s interest within seven years. It has already made a $5 million deposit.

Council members raised concerns about the extended payment schedule. City finance director Bradley Johnson said Oakland would keep the deed if the buyer failed to make the required payments. The city would also avoid future operating expenses after the ownership transfer. Oakland currently spends about $6 million each year to maintain the Coliseum.

The deal follows a decade of major sports departures. The Golden State Warriors, Raiders and Athletics have all left Oakland. The Oakland Roots soccer club also recently announced plans to leave the Coliseum this fall.

Mayor Barbara Lee said the sale could bring jobs and new investment to the surrounding area. “This deal … really paves the way to create jobs and economic development, economic activity, specifically in underserved areas in East Oakland and deep East Oakland,” Lee said. “These investments are long overdue, and our community deserves this.”
