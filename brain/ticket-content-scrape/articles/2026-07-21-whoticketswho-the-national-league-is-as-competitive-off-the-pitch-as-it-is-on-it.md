# #WhoTicketsWho? The National League is as competitive off the pitch as it is on it

## Summary

An independent analysis of the 24 English National League football clubs preparing for the 2026/2027 season finds ten different ticketing systems in use, with no single vendor holding more than a quarter of the market. Fanbase and TicketCo each supply six clubs (25% each), Kaizen Ticketing supplies five (20.8%), and seven smaller suppliers split the rest. The piece traces how promotion/relegation and mid-season vendor switches reshaped supplier share, profiles the leading vendors' differentiated value propositions, and covers the League's new "Ticketing and Fan Experience Partner" deal with Fanbase.

### Key Points
- Ten ticketing systems serve the 24 National League clubs for 2026/2027; the top three (Fanbase, TicketCo, Kaizen Ticketing) cover 70.8% of clubs.
- Club movement (promotion/relegation) plus vendor switches reshaped share: Future Ticketing fell from 3 clubs to 1; TicketCo and Fanbase each grew to 6.
- 2025/2026 division-wide average attendance was 2,803/club (~1.54M total); Kaizen's clients accounted for ~31.3% of total attendances, Fanbase's for ~22.5%.
- National League named Fanbase its league-wide "Ticketing and Fan Experience Partner" in June 2026, covering fan data, communications, and matchday experience beyond ticket transactions.
- Leading vendors differentiate sharply: TicketCo (operational simplicity/support), Fanbase (fan data/engagement), Kaizen (football-specific operations), Future Ticketing (API-first integration).
- New National League leadership: Phil Alexander became permanent CEO in November 2025; Nic Coward became Chair on July 1, 2026.

### Highlights
- No single ticketing vendor holds more than 25% share among the 24 clubs; the top three combine for 70.8%.
- Vendor differentiation clusters around four distinct value props: operational simplicity/support, fan-data/engagement, sport-specific operations, and open API integration.
- League named Fanbase exclusive "Ticketing and Fan Experience Partner" (June 2026), extending beyond ticketing into fan data and communications.
- Article notes smaller clubs with limited internal teams especially prioritize ease-of-use and support over feature depth when picking a vendor.

### Industry Problem
Illustrates client (venue/organizer) pain points in choosing a ticketing vendor: resource-constrained clubs prioritize operational simplicity and support, while others want deeper fan-data/engagement tools or open integration flexibility — no single platform serves all these priorities, and vendor switching happens often even absent dissatisfaction, driven here by league promotion/relegation churn.

### Proposed Solution
The article prescribes no fix, but the fragmented, priority-driven vendor selection among smaller/mid-size venue clients is a build/improve signal: Etix should make its own positioning (ease of onboarding vs. fan-data depth vs. integration flexibility) explicit for resource-constrained venue clients, since that segment appears most likely to switch to whichever vendor best matches its top operational priority.

**Ten different ticketing systems are represented across the 24 clubs preparing for the 2026/2027 National League season, with no provider accounting for more than a quarter of the division.**

That should come as no surprise as competition has always been central to the structure of the National League.

Twenty-four clubs play each other home and away across a demanding 46 game season, yet only the champions are guaranteed promotion to the English Football League. One further place is decided through the play-offs, a structure the National League is seeking to change through its [3UP campaign](https://www.thenationalleague.org.uk/3up/).

Last season provided another demonstration of just how difficult it can be to escape. York City secured automatic promotion as champions with 108 points, while Rochdale finished just two points behind on 106 and had to come through the play-offs to join them in League Two.

Competition, however, is not just limited to the pitch.

My analysis of the ticketing systems being used by the division’s clubs during the upcoming 2026/2027 season shows an equally crowded supplier landscape.

### Ten systems across 24 clubs

Fanbase and TicketCo currently lead the market by club count, with six clubs each. Both account for 25% of the division.

Kaizen Ticketing follows with five clubs, representing 20.8%.

The remaining seven clubs each use a different platform:

● EKM Systems Ltd  
● Future Ticketing  
● Seatlab  
● Ticketmaster Sport  
● Tickets Dot Com  
● Venditan  
● Venue Management Systems

Collectively, those seven suppliers account for the remaining 29.2% of the division. Each  
supplies one club, equivalent to approximately 4.17%.

The three largest providers therefore supply 17 of the 24 clubs, representing 70.8% of the division, while the remaining seven clubs are spread across seven different systems.

That combination of concentration among the three leading providers and variety throughout the rest of the division makes the National League a particularly active and contested part of the football ticketing market.

![](https://www.theticketingbusiness.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/07/The-National-League-Ticketing-Systems-26-27-300x200.png)

### How promotion, relegation & supplier changes reshaped the market

The National League’s supplier landscape can change significantly without any club deciding to replace its ticketing platform.

Six of the 24 clubs competing in the division last season have left, meaning exactly 25% of its membership has changed for 2026/2027.

York City and Rochdale were promoted to League Two, while Brackley Town, Morecambe, Braintree Town and Truro City were relegated.

They have been replaced by Barrow and Harrogate Town from League Two, alongside AFC Fylde, Kidderminster Harriers, Worthing and Hornchurch from National League North and South.

Those movements alone altered the supplier picture within the division.

Seatlab enters the division with Barrow, while Ticketmaster Sport arrives with Harrogate Town. Total Tickets and JustTikit leave following the relegations of Morecambe and Truro City. Kaizen Ticketing gained Hornchurch and Kidderminster through promotion. Fanbase lost Rochdale but gained Worthing, while TicketCo lost Brackley and Braintree but gained AFC Fylde. Future Ticketing lost York City following the club’s promotion.

There has also been movement among the clubs remaining in the National League. Hartlepool United and Tamworth have both moved to TicketCo, while Sutton United has switched to Fanbase.

The combined effect of divisional movement and those three platform changes is significant:

- TicketCo increases from five clubs to six.
- Fanbase increases from five clubs to six.
- Kaizen Ticketing remains on five, having gained Hornchurch and Kidderminster through promotion but lost Sutton United and Tamworth through supplier changes.
- Future Ticketing falls from three clubs to one.

The supplier picture is therefore shaped by two different forces, clubs choosing new platforms and clubs moving between divisions.

### A division with history, support and commercial potential

The National League traces its origins to 1979, when the Alliance Premier League was established as the first attempt to create a unified national division immediately below the Football League. It became the Football Conference in 1986 and adopted the National League name in 2015.

It may sit outside the EFL, but the 2026/2027 line-up contains clubs with considerable Football League history.

Carlisle United spent the 1974/1975 season in the top division of English football. Southend United previously played six seasons in the second tier, while Scunthorpe United competed in the Championship as recently as 2010/2011. Yeovil Town reached the Championship for the first time in 2013.

Barrow, Forest Green Rovers, Harrogate Town and Sutton United have all competed in the EFL during the past decade. Hartlepool United, Boston United, Kidderminster Harriers and Aldershot Town add further Football League experience.

### Attendance adds another dimension

According to European Football Statistics, the National League recorded an average home attendance of 2,803 during 2025/2026. Fourteen of the division’s 24 clubs averaged more than 2,000, while six exceeded 3,000.

Southend United led the division with an average attendance of 8,103, followed by Carlisle United with 7,342. Both used Kaizen Ticketing.

York City, using Future Ticketing, averaged 6,658, while Fanbase client Scunthorpe United averaged 4,685. Hartlepool United, also using Future Ticketing, averaged 3,771, and Rochdale, using Fanbase, averaged 3,056.

Other notable examples included Morecambe, using Total Tickets, with an average attendance of 2,930, and Yeovil Town, using Tickets Dot Com, with 2,918.

Placing those attendance figures alongside the supplier data adds another perspective to the market picture.

Using the published club averages across 23 home league fixtures, the division generated an estimated reported attendance of just over 1.54 million during 2025/2026.

Kaizen Ticketing’s five National League clients accounted for approximately 483,575 attendances, representing 31.3% of the total. Fanbase’s five clubs accounted for approximately 348,381, or 22.5%, while Future Ticketing’s three clubs represented approximately 267,490, or 17.3%. TicketCo also served five clubs, accounting for approximately 134,182 attendances and 8.7% of the total.

The remaining six clubs, across five other suppliers and one club with no online matchday system, collectively represented approximately 313,467 attendances, or 20.2%.

These figures are not intended to link attendance levels to supplier performance. They simply indicate the scale of the audiences associated with each ticketing supplier’s club portfolio, while recognising that reported attendance may include season ticket holders, match buyers, hospitality guests, complimentary admissions, away supporters and customers paying at the turnstile. That scale also reinforces the importance of ease of use, support and operational practicality, particularly for clubs often working with smaller teams than those higher in the pyramid.

### A new league partnership

In June 2026, the National League announced Fanbase as its new [Ticketing and Fan Experience Partner](https://www.theticketingbusiness.com/2026/06/national-league-signs-up-fanbase-as-ticketing-and-fan-experience-partner/).

The scope of the partnership extends beyond ticket transactions, covering supporter communications, fan engagement, customer data and the wider matchday experience.

Alasdair Crawley, Co-Founder of Fanbase, described the partnership as “a statement of intent” around “fan data, matchday commerce and overall growth”, adding that clubs outside the Premier League should be able to deliver a world class supporter experience and operate “like the top 1%”.

Phil Alexander, Chief Executive Officer of the National League, said Fanbase was “at the cutting edge in its sector” and described the partnership as a way for the League to understand its supporters better, serve them well and improve the overall fan experience.

### Different suppliers, different priorities

The appointment of a league-wide ticketing partner does not create a single platform model across the division however. Ten different systems remain in use, and the leading suppliers are not all competing on the same proposition.

TicketCo places considerable emphasis on operational simplicity, onboarding and support. Its approach appears focused on making the system workable for the people using it every day, particularly at clubs where internal resources may be limited and matchday delivery cannot become unnecessarily complicated.

Fanbase’s proposition extends further into supporter engagement and first party data. Its position is that ticketing should sit at the centre of a wider relationship with supporters, connecting purchases, memberships, communications and other interactions rather than operating as a standalone transaction platform.

Kaizen Ticketing’s approach is rooted firmly in football specific operations. Its emphasis is on understanding how clubs sell, report and manage tickets in practice, then using that experience to support revenue generation and address the day to day problems faced by ticketing teams.

Future Ticketing differentiates itself through openness and integration. Its API first approach is designed to allow clubs to connect ticketing with their wider digital operation, including CRM, access control, websites, apps and other specialist systems.

Those differences matter because National League clubs are not all trying to solve the same problem.

Some want a simpler platform and greater operational support. Others are looking for better supporter data, stronger communications or the flexibility to connect several specialist systems.

The range of suppliers represented across the division is therefore not only a sign of competition. It also reflects the different priorities, resources and ambitions of the clubs themselves.

### New leadership and a new phase

The ticketing partnership announcement also comes during a wider period of change for the National League.

Phil Alexander was appointed permanent Chief Executive Officer in November 2025, having initially taken the position on an interim basis the previous May.

Alexander previously spent 26 years at Crystal Palace, becoming the longest serving Chief Executive in the Premier League. His time at the club included three promotions to the top flight.

Nic Coward took over as Chair on 1st July 2026. His previous experience includes serving as General Secretary of the Premier League and twice as interim Chief Executive of the Football Association, as well as senior roles across horse racing, athletics, cricket and golf.

The National League therefore enters 2026/2027 with a relatively new Chief Executive, a new Chair and a new Ticketing and Fan Experience Partner.

Ten systems, 24 clubs and no supplier with more than six.

The National League may be one of the most difficult divisions to escape on the pitch. In ticketing terms, it is proving just as difficult for any single supplier to establish a dominant position.

*The ticketing data was compiled by independent ticketing consultant Jonathan Buck using publicly available information and was correct as of 1st July 2026. The wider analysis also draws on discussions with senior figures from several of the ticketing suppliers represented.*
