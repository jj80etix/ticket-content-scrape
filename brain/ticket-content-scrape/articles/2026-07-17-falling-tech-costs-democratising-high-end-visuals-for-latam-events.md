# Falling tech costs democratising high-end visuals for LatAm events

## Summary

Visuals company Disguise says falling costs for high-end production technology are driving a "Latino takeover" of global festival visuals, citing its work powering Karol G's and Camila Cabello's sets at this year's Coachella. CEO Fernando Küfer says cheaper, more powerful hardware will let smaller LatAm artists access concert visuals once reserved for major pop stars, with more real-time and interactive elements expected.

### Key Points
- Disguise powered Coachella visuals for Karol G (first Latina headliner) and Camila Cabello.
- Falling hardware costs are lowering the barrier to high-end LED/laser production for smaller acts.
- CEO predicts growth in real-time, interactive concert visuals.
- Frames the trend as a two-decade continuation of Disguise's work with Rosalía, Bad Bunny, and Shakira.

## Transcript

Disguise CEO Fernando Küfer says the trend shows that imagery has "never been more important" for festivals

![](https://www.iqmagazine.com/wp-content/smush-webp/2026/07/IMG_5001-1-scaled.jpeg.webp)

© Disguise

Live visuals company Disguise has said its work on this year’s Coachella showcases how the falling cost of high-end visual technology is seeing global festivals and live events increasingly “shaped by Latin culture”.

This comes after the firm provided visuals for this year’s Coachella, powering sets by artists including Karol G and Camilla Cabello, which the company’s CEO Fernando Küfer said showed that “ imagery has never been more important” for festivals.

Küfer told *IQ* fans are increasingly seeking multimedia storytelling as part of live shows, and noted a trend of Latin artists looking to onstage screens and lighting to hold displays about “an artist’s origins and cultural experiences”.

“I don’t believe this shift has been accidental,” he said. “For the past 20 years, our team has helped create technologies that power live concert visuals for artists like Rosalia, Bad Bunny, and Shakira, and have seen how these visuals are changing concert experiences for fans,” Disguise CEO Fernando Küfer told IQ.

“As fewer high-budget music videos are greenlit, Latino artists are increasingly using LED graphics, laser shows, dancers, and even pyrotechnics to add a visual dimension to their live music, and together these immersive experiences are giving LatAm stars an even bigger global stage.”

Driving this transition is a significant evolution in the accessibility of the production hardware itself, he said.

“Technologies like ours are getting both more cost-effective and more powerful,” Küfer explained, predicting that this will lead to high-end visuals becoming more attainable in a wider range of show formats, including “smaller LatAm DJs and artists performing with concert visuals that rival those of big pop stars”.

“We’ll also likely see an increase in the use of real-time visuals at concerts, leading to more interactive audience experiences,” the CEO said.

> “These kinds of possibilities will help artists tell their stories in even more memorable ways”

According to Küfer, this growth in the attainability of high-end visual technological is already manifesting on the global festival circuit. He says this year’s Coachella was an “especially important milestone” for the company and the profile of Latin music and culture at festivals outside South and Central America, calling the April festival “a Latino takeover”.

The festival hosted acts including Becky G, Paulina Rubio, Bizarap, Camila Cabello, Nelly Furtado, and Zulan, with Karol G also becoming the first Latino woman to ever headline the festival.

For Karol G’s set, Disguise worked with media server operator Charly Camina, who used the company’s software and hardware “to power the show and output visuals that honoured both Colombian and Latino culture”, Küfer said.

This cultural storytelling was also reflected in other key performances across the weekend, with Küfer citing Junior H as “another artist who perfectly blended his music with visuals to showcase Latino culture”.

For the artist’s Coachella performance in 2025, Küfer credited Camina and his team with “successfully bringing Junior H’s underground Latin trap aesthetic” to the stage, for which they used the Unreal Engine software alongisde Disguise’s own Designer software, explaining this led to improvements in synchronised cueing and triggering for the set.

Looking ahead, Küfer expects these interactive and real-time capabilities to become foundational for how artists connect with global audiences.

“These kinds of possibilities will help artists tell their stories in even more memorable ways. This will shape not just how we experience concerts and festivals, but also serve a deeper purpose: bringing visibility to underrepresented communities and celebrating their culture,” Küfer said.

He added: “It’s been incredibly exciting to see this trend emerge firsthand, to see an artist’s origins and cultural experiences universalised at a time when imagery has never been more important.”
