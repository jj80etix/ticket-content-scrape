# Star-Studded World Cup Final Halftime Show Delights Music Fans & Alienates Soccer Purists

## Summary

FIFA staged its first-ever World Cup final halftime show on July 19 at MetLife Stadium, an 11-minute, Chris Martin-curated performance featuring Madonna, Justin Bieber, BTS, and Shakira. The show extended halftime past FIFA's own 15-minute limit to about 27 minutes and drew mixed reactions — praised as a global spectacle by some, criticized by soccer purists and musicians (including Robert Smith and Liam Gallagher) as excessive commercialization.

### Key Points
- First-ever FIFA World Cup final halftime show, produced by Global Citizen and curated by Coldplay's Chris Martin.
- Lineup included Madonna, Justin Bieber, BTS, and Shakira across an 11-minute set.
- Halftime break ran roughly 27 minutes, violating FIFA's 15-minute halftime rule.
- FIFA anticipates ~$15 billion in revenue for the 2023-2026 cycle, up from $7.56 billion the prior cycle, aided by added ad inventory and record ticket prices.
- Public criticism from musicians The Cure's Robert Smith and Oasis's Liam Gallagher, and mixed remarks from BBC analyst Wayne Rooney.

FIFA made quite the impression in its second World Cup final on U.S. soil, with Madonna, Justin Bieber, BTS and Shakira headlining a vibrant, dense 11-minute halftime show at New York-New Jersey Stadium (otherwise known as MetLife Stadium).

The soccer governing body’s first-ever halftime show, curated by Coldplay’s Chris Martin, raised the intensity following a scoreless first half with a star-studded lineup that celebrated cultures from across the globe.

Madonna opened the historic show, produced by Global Citizen, with her 2000 hit “Music,” accompanied by Brazilian soccer icons Ronaldo and Ronaldinho in a prerecorded video, and concluded the dance track by walking out of the tunnel and onto the field.

It quickly transitioned to the center circle, where Gustavo Dudamel conducted a large orchestra that featured three Muppets from The Electric Mayhem band, including Animal on the drums, performing an instrumental rendition of The White Stripes’ “Seven Nation Army,” which has become a popular soccer anthem for teams across the globe.

The orchestra then lowered its instruments to replicate Norway’s Viking row, a trend that went viral on social media, and the camera weaved between musicians before finding the members of South Korean megastars BTS, who lit it up with their hit “Dynamite.”

That high was slightly lowered with a quick cut to actors Jason Sudeikis and Brendan Hunt, dressed as the characters they portray in the Emmy-winning soccer comedy “Ted Lasso,” who called for a substitution for an artist with “heart” and “soul” who could “bring the love,” and in came an eager Justin Bieber, who walked on the field for an acoustic rendition of “Everything Hallelujah.”

Shakira brought the energy back up with help from Burna Boy for her World Cup song, “Dai Dai,” and the show closed with the PS22 Chorus of local elementary-school students singing alongside Chris Martin, Muppets and Sesame Street characters, Dudamel and BTS.

It truly was the spectacle Martin and everyone else involved had promised: a celebration of the global event that has united fans worldwide, as evidenced by viral social media videos of tourists passionately celebrating the victories and even the losses during the tournament.

**FANS, COMMENTATORS CONFLICTED ABOUT HISTORIC HALFTIME SHOW**

The Topps Halftime Show was a congested, fast-moving 11 minutes that aimed to draw younger audiences and casual fans by featuring some of the most-streamed global artists. Nearly 1.5 billion people viewed the 2022 World Cup final between Argentina and France in Qatar, and FIFA expects this year’s final in New Jersey to eclipse that.

While musical performances are common in American sports, most notably during the Super Bowl, soccer isn’t known to feature such halftime events.

The Global Citizen-produced show, though, is unlike any other halftime event because the field must be preserved and protected as much as possible for the second half of the match, and the performers had an 11-minute window. That mission was accomplished, but it also extended the World Cup final halftime break to just over 27 minutes, violating FIFA’s own rule that halftime breaks must not exceed 15 minutes.

That was enough to validate the soccer purists who were vehemently opposed to the changes FIFA made to this year’s competition, the largest in its history, with 48 teams and 104 matches.

The addition of hydration breaks at the midpoint of each allowed FOX, the English-language network that televised matches in the U.S., to incorporate additional ads for more advertising revenue. That only drew more fans to NBCUniversal Telemundo’s Spanish-language U.S. broadcast, which opted to keep the camera on the players free from commercials.

With additional advertising revenue and record-high ticket prices, FIFA President anticipates that revenues for the 2023-2026 cycle will reach $15 billion. The organization generated a record $7.56 billion in revenue during its previous cycle.

The commercialization of the sport and tournament has left some fans with a sour taste. Even The Cure frontman Robert Smith slammed the halftime show, posting on [Instagram](https://www.instagram.com/p/Da3GKWPzl69/) days before the performances and sharing details of the event with some of his own commentary, referring to the FIFA president at Gianni Infantosser and quotes around the event described as one that will “celebrate football and our shared values, ensuring a legacy that transcends the final whistle.” And he followed that with “AAAAAAAAAAAAAAAAAAGH… #Breadandcircuses #MUGWANK #pleasejustfuckoff.”

He further explained the post and [wrote](https://www.instagram.com/p/Da3x-T1Izbg/), “For those of us not quite getting it: the point is not really about who is curating or performing, it is about the #mugwank idea of a football world cup final half time show. If you know understand my ‘AAAAAAAGH!’ a bit better, but still don’t agree, please adjust your read cap, your ‘I 🖤 Janny + Donny + $$$’ tshirt and…knock yourself out?”

Fellow British rocker Liam Gallagher was more succinct with his thoughts.

“This is like a bad trip,” he first [posted on X](https://x.com/liamgallagher/status/2078941371663069537), and followed that with, “It’s a good job I’ve got my spiritual socks on or that halftime entertainment could have tipped me over the edge.”

English soccer star-turned-analyst Wayne Rooney was candid on the BBC broadcast following the halftime show when asked about his thoughts. He replied, “I like a lot of them artists, but I thought it was crap. I did, honestly.”

Regardless of how fans and broadcasters felt about the first World Cup final halftime show, the release of viewership numbers and revenue will likely justify FIFA’s moves, and it’s likely music will be a mainstay at the world’s biggest sporting event.
