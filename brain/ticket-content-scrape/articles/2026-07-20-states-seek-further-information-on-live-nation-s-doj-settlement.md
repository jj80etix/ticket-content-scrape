# States seek further information on Live Nation’s DOJ settlement

## Summary

Twenty-one state attorneys general who continued the antitrust case against Live Nation-Ticketmaster after an April jury verdict have asked Judge Arun Subramanian for permission to conduct discovery into the U.S. Department of Justice's separate settlement with the company, invoking the Tunney Act's public-interest review process. The states argue the settlement negotiation lacked transparency and that its terms — including the "open distribution and ticket authentication system" and the 13-venue divestiture — may not meaningfully restore competition. Live Nation and Ticketmaster, meanwhile, are separately asking the court to overturn April's monopoly verdict or grant a new trial, with a hearing on all motions expected at the end of the month.

### Key Points
- 21 states (including Colorado, New York, Virginia) filed a letter seeking Tunney Act discovery into the DOJ-Live Nation settlement.
- States say they weren't notified of the settlement talks until January 29, 2026 — about a year after negotiations began.
- Discovery targets include the settlement's ticket-authentication/open-distribution system and the selection of the 13 divested venues.
- A federal jury ruled in April 2026 that Live Nation/Ticketmaster illegally monopolized the industry and overcharged fans.
- Live Nation/Ticketmaster have moved to overturn that verdict or secure a new trial; a hearing on both sets of motions is set for late July 2026.

### Highlights
- 21 states invoking the Tunney Act to seek discovery into whether the DOJ-Live Nation settlement actually "meaningfully increase[s] competition."
- Alleged year-long transparency gap: negotiations reportedly began around early 2025, states notified only January 29, 2026.
- Discovery request specifically targets the settlement's ticket-authentication/open-distribution terms and the 13-venue divestiture selection — the parts most relevant to independent platform access.
- Live Nation/Ticketmaster's parallel bid to overturn the April monopoly verdict or get a new trial, with a hearing scheduled for late July 2026.

### Industry Problem
This is the clearest live test of whether antitrust remedies against Live Nation/Ticketmaster will actually open the primary-ticketing market to independents. The states' pushback signals that the DOJ settlement's structural remedies — venue divestitures and ticketing-system access terms — may be weaker or narrower than they first appeared, which directly affects how much real competitive room emerges for platforms like Etix.

### Proposed Solution
No product response applies yet; this is #monitor/#evolve territory. Watch for the court's ruling on the discovery request, the specific terms of the 13 divested venues' ticketing arrangements, and the outcome of the late-July hearing on the overturn/new-trial motions — any of which could materially reshape the competitive opening for independent primary platforms.

TheTicketingBusiness News

**State attorneys general which carried on the antitrust lawsuit against Live Nation-Ticketmaster have asked for permission to conduct discovery into the US Department of Justice’s (DOJ) antitrust settlement with the entertainment and ticketing giant.**

In a letter to Judge Arun Subramanian last week, some 21 states said they wanted to examine whether the settlement does enough to restore competition, and how it was negotiated.

As reported by [*Music Business Worldwide*](https://www.musicbusinessworldwide.com/after-rejecting-the-dojs-live-nation-settlement-states-press-for-discovery-into-white-house-talks/), the states said they had “significant concerns that the settlement is not in the public interest”.

**A landmark trial and ruling**

States [resumed](https://www.theticketingbusiness.com/2026/03/live-nation-trial-to-resume-after-states-reject-doj-settlement/) the antitrust case against Live Nation after they were left underwhelmed by the DOJ’s [settlement](https://www.theticketingbusiness.com/2026/03/live-nation-reaches-settlement-with-doj-in-antitrust-case/).

A federal jury ultimately [decided in April](https://www.theticketingbusiness.com/2026/04/jury-rules-live-nation-ticketmaster-held-monopoly-in-landmark-case/) that Live Nation and Ticketmaster illegally monopolised the industry and overcharged fans.

Now, states including Colorado, New York, Virginia and more are invoking the Tunney Act, which sees antitrust settlements proposed by the US government independently reviewed by a court to ensure they are in the public interest.

The [document](https://www.musicbusinessworldwide.com/files/2026/07/United-States-et-al.-v.-Live-Nation-Entertainment-letter.pdf) said that some of the states had concerns about “the process that produced the Settlement. These concerns are driven in part by the lack of transparency about the Settlement negotiations that took place prior to March 2, 2026”.

States said they were not notified of the US-Live Nation negotiations until January 29, 2026, “a year after they began”.

It was also felt that the “proposed terms appear to be insufficient to meaningfully increase competition in the relevant markets and could also increase barriers to entry and reduce competition”.

The states added: “Public reporting and Defendants’ recent filing further suggest to some states that the United States’s acceptance of the Settlement may have been influenced by individuals outside of the Department of Justice and concerns other than restoring or increasing competition.”

Should the court grant permission, the states said they would seek documents, information and testimony on the settlement’s “open distribution and ticket authentication system” and the “exclusive venue booking arrangements chosen for ‘Divestiture’, including the selection of the 13 venues”.

Elsewhere, Live Nation and Ticketmaster have asked that April’s [verdict be thrown out](https://www.theticketingbusiness.com/2026/07/live-nation-asks-judge-to-overturn-monopoly-verdict-or-grant-new-trial/), and should this fail, the pair have also requested that US District Judge Arun Subramanian grant a new trial.

Judge Subramanian has not yet ruled on any of the motions, which are due to be argued at a hearing at the end of the month.
