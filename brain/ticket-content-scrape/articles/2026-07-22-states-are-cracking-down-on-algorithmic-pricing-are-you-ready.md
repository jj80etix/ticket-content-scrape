# States Are Cracking Down on Algorithmic Pricing. Are You Ready?

## Summary

A Troutman Pepper State Attorneys General practice alert (published via Bloomberg Law, July 22, 2026) flags a wave of state-level scrutiny targeting algorithmic and dynamic pricing practices, urging businesses to prepare compliance now. The underlying article is paywalled at Bloomberg Law, so specifics on which states or statutes are covered aren't available from this stub alone, but the framing signals growing state AG appetite to regulate algorithm-driven price-setting — a category that includes ticketing's dynamic pricing.

### Key Points
- Bloomberg Law published "States Are Cracking Down on Algorithmic Pricing. Are You Ready?" on July 22, 2026, authored by Troutman Pepper's State AGs practice group.
- The alert signals rising state-level enforcement interest in algorithmic/dynamic pricing practices generally, not ticketing-specific.
- No state names, statutes, or enforcement actions are detailed in the available (non-paywalled) portion.
- Coincides with continued state patchwork activity on junk-fee and all-in pricing (Q9), where dynamic-pricing disclosure is a live issue for ticketing.

### Highlights
- Publication date: July 22, 2026 (Bloomberg Law legal-exchange insights).
- Authors are Troutman Pepper's State Attorneys General practice group — the same firm behind the Regulatory Oversight blog already monitored for Q9/Q11.
- No specific state or bill named in the available content; full detail sits behind Bloomberg Law's paywall.

### Industry Problem
Ticketing's use of dynamic/algorithmic pricing sits inside a regulatory category (algorithmic pricing) that state AGs are increasingly scrutinizing alongside junk-fee disclosure rules, raising compliance risk for any primary platform using demand-based pricing.

### Proposed Solution
Monitor for the underlying Bloomberg Law piece and any follow-on state actions naming ticketing or live-events pricing specifically; if algorithmic-pricing rules start naming dynamic ticket pricing, Etix should get ahead of disclosure requirements (clear "why this price" messaging) rather than react after an enforcement action.
