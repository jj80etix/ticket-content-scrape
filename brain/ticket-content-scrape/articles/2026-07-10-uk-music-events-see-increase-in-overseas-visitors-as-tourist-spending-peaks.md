# UK music events see increase in overseas visitors as tourist spending peaks

## Summary

UK Music reports a record 24.7 million music tourists attended UK concerts and festivals in 2025, driving tourist spending to an all-time high of £11.2bn (+11.3% YoY), fueled by major tours like Oasis's reunion run. UK Music's CEO used the report to renew pressure on the government to act on ticket-touting reform and post-Brexit EU touring costs for artists.

### Key Points
- Record 24.7m music tourists in 2025; overseas visitor numbers up 3.2%.
- Music tourist spending hit £11.2bn, up 11.3% from £10bn in 2024.
- Oasis reunion shows (Heaton Park, Wembley, Cardiff) were singled out as major revenue drivers.
- London spending up 27.4% YoY (£2.7bn→£3.4bn); North West up 15.6%.
- UK Music renewed calls for government action on ticket touts and EU touring red tape.

### Highlights
- £11.2bn in UK music tourism spend (2025), an 11.3% YoY increase — evidence of continued demand-side strength despite pricing concerns raised elsewhere.
- UK Music explicitly links growth to unresolved government commitments on anti-touting reform, keeping resale regulation politically live in the UK.

### Industry Problem
Sustained fan demand and destination-style touring are growing the addressable market, but industry advocates argue unresolved resale/touting regulation is capping how much of that spend reaches fans/artists rather than resellers.

### Proposed Solution
Primarily a policy ask (government anti-touting action), not a product fix; for a primary platform the takeaway is to monitor UK resale-regulation developments as a possible trigger for face-value-only or capped-resale features if legislation moves.

**A record 24.7 million music tourists came to concerts and festivals in the UK last year as music tourist spending reached an all-time high of £11.2bn ($15bn/€13.1bn).**

The figures, from UK Music, mark an 11.3% increase on the 2024 spend of £10bn while the number of overseas visitors attending music events has risen by 3.2%.

“The billions spent are a huge shot in the arm for towns and cities right across the UK and benefit hotels, restaurants, bars and transport firms and thousands of other businesses,” said UK Music chief executive Tom Kiehl.

“However, the Government must support music fans by delivering on their manifesto pledge to tackle the menace of ticket touts who charge exorbitant prices for resale tickets – squeezing the amount of cash fans have to spend on gig-going.

“We need to see long overdue Government and EU action to help UK performers and musicians touring the EU who have to deal with soaring costs and red tape post-Brexit.

“We also need to see music’s grassroots protected and nurtured. This includes the artists, venues, festivals, studios and rehearsal rooms that are embedded in our local communities and who are essential to the future prosperity of our world-leading industry.

“We must make sure that policymakers recognise their vital economic and cultural value and deliver the support they need.”

A series of Oasis gigs – including five at Manchester’s Heaton Park, seven at Wembley Stadium and two at Cardiff’s Principality Stadium – saw revenues surge across the UK.

In the North West, music tourists’ spending rose 15.6% from £1.2bn in 2024 to £1.4bn in 2025. In London, music tourism spending increased by 27.4% from £2.7bn in 2024 to £3.4bn last year.

Festivals such as Glastonbury, Download, Reading, Boomtown and Wireless were also instrumental to the improved numbers.

“These record-breaking figures are a testament to what the UK’s music industry does better than anywhere else in the world,” said Creative Industries Minister Ian Murray.

“Whether it’s Oasis playing Heaton Park, or an up-and-coming artist finding their feet on a smaller stage like the Dublin Castle or The Troubadour, this is a nation of incredible live performance, with world-renowned venues to support it.

“That’s why this Government is committed to backing the entire music ecosystem in its upcoming plan for music: protecting fans from the exploitation of ticket touts, supporting the grassroots venues and studios that are the lifeblood of our future talent, and working to improve opportunities for UK artists to tour in Europe.”
