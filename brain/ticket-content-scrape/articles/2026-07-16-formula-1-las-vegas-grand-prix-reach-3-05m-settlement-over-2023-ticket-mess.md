# Formula 1, Las Vegas Grand Prix Reach $3.05M Settlement Over 2023 Ticket Mess

## Summary

Formula 1 parent Liberty Media and Las Vegas Grand Prix Inc. have agreed to a $3.05 million settlement fund for ticket holders who were removed from the circuit before the delayed second practice session at the inaugural 2023 Las Vegas Grand Prix. U.S. District Judge Gloria Navarro granted preliminary approval in March 2026, with a final approval hearing set for November 4 in the U.S. District Court for the District of Nevada. The case survived a partial dismissal after the judge found fans had adequately alleged injury from being denied the practice session without a proportional refund. Direct and Ticketmaster purchasers will be paid automatically, while holders of transferred tickets must file a claim by August 27.

### Key Points
- Settlement fund totals $3,047,986; preliminary approval granted March 2026, final approval hearing November 4, 2026, in the U.S. District Court, District of Nevada.
- Case: *Diep et al. v. Liberty Media Corporation et al.*, No. 2:23-cv-02124-GMN-NJK; Judge Gloria Navarro dismissed much of the original complaint in February 2025 but allowed amended breach-of-contract claims to proceed.
- Class covers Thursday-only and three-day ticket holders who entered before midnight Nov. 16, 2023, including qualifying transferred tickets.
- Direct/Ticketmaster purchasers are paid automatically by email match; transferred-ticket holders must submit a claim with documentation by August 27, 2026.
- Attorneys' fees are capped at 30% ($914,396) of the fund; class representatives may receive up to $2,500 each in service awards.
- The 2023 in-the-moment response was a $200 merchandise credit for Thursday-only holders and no compensation for three-day holders; the settlement replaces that with court-supervised cash payments.

### Highlights
- A ~$3.05M cash settlement compensates patrons for a missed session after event disruption, replacing an earlier merchandise-credit-only response that drew criticism.
- The award formula weighs average ticket price, the missed session's share of ticket value, and a litigation-risk discount — a concrete model for pro-rating refunds on partial event delivery.
- Transferred-ticket holders face a manual claims process with an August 27 deadline, while direct/Ticketmaster buyers are refunded automatically — a meaningful gap in refund friction based on how the ticket was acquired.

### Industry Problem
The case shows how little standardized recourse patrons have when an event is materially curtailed after purchase — the operator's original response (a merchandise credit) was seen as inadequate, and it took over two years of litigation to produce a cash refund. It also exposes how ticket transferability complicates refund administration, since transferred-ticket holders bear extra claims friction that direct buyers don't.

### Proposed Solution
Absent litigation, the practical fix is a pre-defined, transparent partial-refund/insurance policy for service disruptions (e.g., pro-rated credit tied to the specific missed component) built into ticketing terms rather than negotiated after the fact. Etix could differentiate by offering built-in disruption-refund tooling — automatic pro-rated payouts triggered by event-change flags, extended to transferred tickets — as a trust-building alternative to the credit-then-lawsuit pattern seen here.

The fund will also cover settlement administration expenses, court-approved litigation costs and attorneys’ fees of up to 30%, or $914,396. Attorneys may additionally seek service awards of as much as $2,500 for each of the five class representatives.

## Fans Removed Before Delayed Practice

The lawsuit grew out of a chaotic opening night for Formula 1’s heavily promoted return to Las Vegas.

The first practice session was stopped after approximately nine minutes when a loose water valve cover damaged the cars driven by Carlos Sainz and Esteban Ocon. Formula 1 subsequently inspected and repaired similar covers around the Strip circuit, delaying the second practice session for several hours.

Fans waited late into the night but were eventually ordered to leave before the rescheduled session began. Organizers said the decision was driven by concerns involving security, transportation and hospitality staffing.

[TicketNews reported at the time](https://www.ticketnews.com/2023/11/formula-1-lawsuit-las-vegas-grand-prix/) that Thursday-only ticket holders were offered a $200 credit for the Las Vegas Grand Prix’s official merchandise store. Fans holding full three-day tickets were not offered compensation for the missed Thursday session.

That response drew particular criticism because the race had been marketed as one of the most expensive events in Formula 1 history. Individual tickets initially started at hundreds of dollars, while premium and hospitality packages reached several thousand dollars.

## Lawsuit Survived Partial Dismissal

The fan lawsuit was filed immediately after the disrupted race weekend and eventually proceeded in federal court as *Diep et al. v. Liberty Media Corporation et al.*, case number 2:23-cv-02124-GMN-NJK.

Plaintiffs initially pursued claims including breach of contract, negligence and deceptive trade practices, seeking compensation for tickets and other expenses connected to attending the event.

Navarro dismissed much of the case in February 2025, finding that the plaintiffs had sufficiently alleged an injury but had not adequately pleaded their legal claims. The judge dismissed Las Vegas Paving from the action while allowing the ticket holders to amend their breach-of-contract allegations against Liberty Media and the Las Vegas Grand Prix.

The amended case ultimately focused on whether organizers wrongfully revoked fans’ admission without providing the partial ticket refund they were allegedly owed.

While the proposed settlement will not provide full reimbursement for the expensive race weekend, it would replace Formula 1’s original merchandise-credit response with court-supervised cash payments for qualifying ticket holders.

No payments will be distributed unless the court grants final approval. Funds would then be sent after the approval becomes effective and any appeals are resolved.
