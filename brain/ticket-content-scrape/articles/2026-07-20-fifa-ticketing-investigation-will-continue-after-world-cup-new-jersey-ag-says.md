# FIFA Ticketing Investigation Will Continue After World Cup, New Jersey AG Says

## Summary

New Jersey Attorney General Jennifer Davenport confirmed her office's investigation into FIFA's World Cup ticketing practices will continue past the tournament's July 19 conclusion, focused on potentially misleading seat-category maps, phased inventory releases, and variable pricing. The NJ probe (joint with New York, subpoenaed May 27) sits alongside separate investigations from California and Texas AGs, a proposed class action in Washington D.C. alleging Category 1 buyers were misled about seat quality, and Rep. Nellie Pou's newly introduced HOLDBACKS Act — together shifting ticket-policy scrutiny toward primary-seller inventory and pricing conduct as FIFA prepares to report roughly $15 billion in World Cup revenue.

### Key Points
- NY and NJ jointly subpoenaed FIFA on May 27 over ticket sales for the eight MetLife Stadium matches, including the July 19 final; the investigation continues post-tournament.
- FIFA reportedly raised prices for more than 90 of 104 matches between October 2025 and April 2026, with average increases of 34% across its three main public categories.
- FIFA introduced higher-priced "Front Categories" carved from previously sold zones after millions of tickets were already purchased, and released ~1,200 Category 2 seats at $7,380 each shortly before the final.
- California AG Rob Bonta (opened May) and Texas AG Ken Paxton (opened under the Texas Deceptive Trade Practices Act) are separately investigating; a proposed D.C. class action alleges Category 1 buyers were misled about access to premium seats already reserved for VIPs/sponsors.
- FIFA maintains its category maps were "indicative," not precise, and that its ticket terms gave it broad discretion over seat assignments; no penalties or remedies have been identified yet.
- FIFA is expected to report roughly $15 billion in World Cup revenue, above its original $11 billion projection, with ticketing/hospitality driving a significant share of the increase.

### Highlights
- Active multi-state consumer-protection investigations (NJ/NY joint subpoena, CA, TX) plus a D.C. class action target FIFA's seat-map accuracy, phased releases, and 34% average price increases across 90+ matches.
- HOLDBACKS Act (H.R. 9762, introduced July 16) is explicitly linked to this same FIFA controversy, proposing pre-onsale inventory and pricing disclosure requirements for primary sellers generally.
- No penalties, enforcement mechanism, or case timeline has been set; investigations continue with no stated end date.

### Industry Problem
This is a live, expanding regulatory examination of primary-seller conduct — misleading seat-category representations, phased/staggered inventory releases, and large price swings — that mirrors exactly the kind of operational practices (seat maps, category definitions, release timing) a primary platform like Etix must get right, making it directly relevant to how "all-in"/pricing-transparency-style regulation could extend beyond fees into inventory and seat-map accuracy.

### Proposed Solution
No solution is offered by FIFA or regulators yet beyond ongoing investigation; Etix should monitor outcomes of the NJ/NY/CA/TX inquiries and the parallel HOLDBACKS Act for any resulting disclosure standard (e.g., accurate real-time seat-category maps, defined release-schedule communication) and be positioned to adopt such a standard proactively as a trust differentiator versus larger platforms under active investigation.

One major focus is FIFA’s handling of its broad seating categories.

During the tournament’s early sales phases, many buyers selected a ticket category without receiving a specific section, row or seat. FIFA presented Category 1 as its most desirable standard public tier, followed by Categories 2, 3 and 4.

After millions of tickets had already been sold, FIFA introduced additional “Front Categories” containing some of the most desirable seats within those original zones and offered them at higher prices.

Consumers who had previously purchased Category 1 tickets complained that they were assigned locations in corners, behind the goals or farther from the field than the maps available during the sales process appeared to suggest. Some buyers also said their assigned seats were in areas previously displayed as lower-priced categories.

The attorneys general are examining whether those maps and category descriptions gave consumers a misleading understanding of the inventory they had a chance to receive.

FIFA has previously said its category maps were “indicative” rather than precise seating plans and that changes were made to reflect final stadium configurations and supporter allocations. Its ticket terms also gave FIFA broad discretion over seat assignments and category boundaries.

The consumer-protection question, however, is not limited to whether FIFA contractually reserved the right to alter its maps. Regulators are examining what an ordinary buyer reasonably understood from the sales presentation when committing to tickets that sometimes cost thousands of dollars without knowing the exact seat location.

## Holdbacks and Variable Pricing Also Under Scrutiny

Davenport said investigators are separately examining FIFA’s prices and its decision to release inventory in phases throughout the tournament’s sales cycle.

Between October 2025 and April 2026, FIFA reportedly raised prices for more than 90 of the tournament’s 104 matches. Prices across the three principal public categories increased by an average of 34%, according to figures cited when New York and New Jersey announced their investigation.

The attorneys general said they want to determine whether FIFA’s release schedule, public statements and other conduct contributed to those increases.

The issue is particularly significant because FIFA controlled both the primary supply and the timing of additional releases. Matches and categories could appear unavailable before additional blocks of tickets emerged, leaving consumers unable to determine whether an apparent shortage represented the remaining supply or only the inventory FIFA had chosen to release at that moment.

For the World Cup final, FIFA continued releasing substantial blocks of tickets shortly before the match, including nearly 1,200 Category 2 seats priced at $7,380 each. FIFA also offered Front Category 1 and hospitality inventory at significantly higher prices.

Late releases can result from legitimate operational changes, including sponsor returns, unused team allocations and finalized production requirements. Without meaningful inventory disclosures, however, consumers had no reliable way to distinguish those circumstances from a deliberate staggered-release strategy.

## Lawsuits and Other State Investigations Could Outlast Tournament

The New York-New Jersey inquiry is only one part of the legal scrutiny surrounding FIFA’s ticket rollout.

California Attorney General Rob Bonta [opened a separate review in May](https://www.ticketnews.com/2026/05/california-ag-presses-fifa-over-world-cup-ticket-maps-after-fans-say-seat-categories-were-changed-after-purchase/), requesting copies of stadium-map iterations, purchasing pages shown to consumers and information about buyers who received seats that would have appeared in lower categories under earlier maps.

Texas Attorney General Ken Paxton l [ater opened an investigation under the Texas Deceptive Trade Practices Act](https://www.ticketnews.com/2026/06/texas-ag-joins-scrutiny-of-fifa-world-cup-ticket-sales-after-seat-map-pricing-complaints/) following similar complaints from buyers attending matches in Arlington and Houston.

FIFA’s U.S. ticketing subsidiary is also [facing a proposed class action in Washington, D.C](https://www.ticketnews.com/2026/07/fifa-hit-with-class-action-alleging-world-cup-category-ticket-maps-misled-buyers/)., alleging that Category 1 buyers were led to believe they had a chance of receiving premium midfield seats that had already been reserved for corporate partners, VIPs and hospitality customers.

The lawsuit argues that FIFA did not merely assign disappointing seats, but misrepresented the pool of inventory from which Category 1 assignments would be made. FIFA had not publicly responded to the complaint when it was first reported.

The dispute has also entered [Congress. Rep. Nellie Pou of New Jersey introduced the HOLDBACKS Act on July 16](https://www.ticketnews.com/2026/07/pous-holdbacks-act-targets-deceptive-hidden-ticket-inventory-manufactured-scarcity/?utm_source=chatgpt.com), proposing that primary ticket sellers disclose how much inventory will be offered to the general public and prohibiting intentional withholding or misleading availability claims intended to increase prices.

Pou specifically cited FIFA’s opaque World Cup inventory releases as an example of why consumers need greater visibility into how much ticket supply is actually being made available.

Together, the investigations, lawsuit and proposed legislation shift the ticket-policy debate toward decisions made by event organizers and primary sellers. The central questions are not simply why tickets became expensive or what they later cost on resale platforms, but what inventory FIFA controlled, what it showed buyers and whether its sales representations gave consumers an accurate picture of the product and supply available to them.

The continuing scrutiny comes as FIFA prepares to announce a reported $15 billion in World Cup revenue, far above its original $11 billion projection. Ticketing and hospitality reportedly accounted for a significant portion of the increase, including FIFA’s revenue from transactions conducted through its official resale marketplace.

Davenport has not identified what penalties or remedies could result if investigators conclude that FIFA violated state consumer-protection law. The subpoenas nevertheless ensure that the tournament organizer’s commercial success will not end the questions surrounding how those revenues were generated.

The matches are finished. The legal examination of how FIFA sold access to them is only beginning.
