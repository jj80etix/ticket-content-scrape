# Live Nation, Oak View Group Launch Saudi Venue JV Amid Antitrust Scrutiny of U.S. Partnership

## Summary

Live Nation and Oak View Group have formed "radia," a majority PIF-backed joint venture with SURJ Sports Investment to develop and operate sports and entertainment venues across Saudi Arabia, targeting a sports sector projected to exceed $22 billion by 2030. The deal deepens the Live Nation-OVG partnership even as the DOJ's proposed antitrust settlement in the Live Nation case specifically targets the two companies' lucrative U.S. venue arrangement.

### Key Points
- New JV "radia": SURJ (majority owner, PIF-backed) + Oak View Group + Live Nation.
- Former SURJ executive Xavier Campbell named CEO of the venture.
- Targets Saudi venue build-out ahead of the 2034 FIFA World Cup; sector projected at $22B+ by 2030.
- Live Nation has operated in Saudi Arabia since 2017; OVG cites 2+ years of planning for post-World Cup venue viability.
- Comes as DOJ's proposed Live Nation settlement specifically scrutinizes the Live Nation-OVG U.S. venue relationship.

### Highlights
- JV structure: SURJ majority-owned, with OVG and Live Nation as operating/programming partners.
- Explicit tension: the new international JV deepens the same Live Nation-OVG relationship the DOJ settlement is trying to constrain domestically.
- Saudi sports sector value target: $22B+ by 2030; venue build-out tied to the 2034 World Cup.

### Industry Problem
Live Nation and OVG are expanding their joint venue strategy internationally at the same time U.S. regulators are trying to limit the competitive effects of that same partnership domestically, a structural tension worth tracking for how it affects the DOJ settlement's credibility and scope.

### Proposed Solution
Monitor posture: watch whether the Saudi JV draws scrutiny or commentary in the ongoing Tunney Act discovery fight over the DOJ settlement, since it's directly relevant evidence of how entrenched the Live Nation-OVG relationship is globally.

Live Nation Entertainment and Oak View Group are deepening their international partnership through a new joint venture created to develop and operate sports and live entertainment infrastructure across Saudi Arabia.

The companies joined with SURJ Sports Investment, a subsidiary of Saudi Arabia’s Public Investment Fund, to launch radia, a fully integrated venue-services platform. The new company will work across venue design, development planning, operations, commercialization, event activation and fan experience for Saudi Arabia’s growing collection of stadiums, arenas and sports precincts.

SURJ will be the majority shareholder in radia, [according to Pollstar](https://news.pollstar.com/2026/07/16/ovg-live-nation-surj-sports-investment-announce-radia-jv-to-develop-sports-entertainment-in-saudi-arabia/), which is owned by Oak View Group. Former SURJ executive Xavier Campbell has been appointed chief executive and will oversee the venture’s entry into the Saudi market. The partners say Saudi Arabia’s sports sector is projected to exceed $22 billion by 2030.

The commercial logic behind the partnership is straightforward. Saudi Arabia is undertaking a vast venue-construction program ahead of the 2034 FIFA World Cup while attempting to establish a year-round calendar of major sports, concerts and entertainment events.

SURJ provides local access and financial backing from the sovereign wealth fund. OVG brings venue-development and management experience. Live Nation supplies concert programming, artist relationships and event-promotion capabilities.

Live Nation said it has operated and invested in Saudi Arabia since 2017. OVG CEO Chris Granger said the parties have spent more than two years considering how new venues can remain commercially viable after the World Cup and other major international events have passed.

But the pairing of Live Nation and OVG is more notable than an ordinary international venue partnership.

Their relationship was one of the [central subjects of the Justice Department’s antitrust lawsuit against Live Nation and Ticketmaster](https://www.ticketnews.com/2024/05/doj-alleges-oak-view-group-colluded-with-live-nation-in-lawsuit/). It also remains directly implicated in the [proposed settlement now undergoing public review in federal court](https://www.ticketnews.com/2026/07/doj-filing-opens-public-review-period-for-live-nation-ticketmaster-settlement/).

Under the proposed final judgment, Live Nation and Ticketmaster must terminate a 2022 Ticketing Services Incentive Agreement with OVG. The settlement documents say Ticketmaster paid OVG millions of dollars to advocate for OVG-managed venues to replace competing ticketing providers with Ticketmaster.

Affected venue clients would have to be informed about the agreement and the payments made to OVG. Certain venues would also be given the opportunity to conduct a new ticketing request-for-proposals process without contractual penalties. Live Nation would be prohibited from entering similar arrangements that compensate venue managers for converting their clients to Ticketmaster.

[TicketNews previously reported](https://www.ticketnews.com/2025/07/secret-ticketmaster-kickbacks-uncovered-in-ovg-bid-rigging-case/) that documents released through OVG’s non-prosecution agreement identified a $20 million upfront payment from Ticketmaster, followed by approximately $7 million annually. The venues OVG was advising were allegedly not told that the company had a separate financial interest in their ticketing decisions.

The proposed settlement does not require Live Nation and OVG to end every commercial relationship, and nothing in its publicly available language appears to prohibit a Saudi Arabian venue venture. Its restrictions focus on the companies’ domestic ticketing incentive agreement and comparable arrangements with venue managers.

Still, the launch of radia illustrates that the two companies remain close strategic partners outside the specific U.S. relationship the settlement seeks to unwind.

The Justice Department’s amended complaint characterized OVG as a potentially formidable Live Nation competitor that instead reached an accommodation with the entertainment giant.

OVG had the venue portfolio, executive relationships, access to artists and financial resources needed to challenge Live Nation in concert promotion and other areas, according to the government. Live Nation initially classified OVG as one of its “Biggest Competitor Threats.”

Federal prosecutors alleged that the companies eventually chose to divide business opportunities rather than compete for them.

“Live Nation and Oak View Group have colluded and established a partnership to allocate business lines \[and\] avoid competing with each other,” the complaint alleged.

The government cited internal correspondence in which OVG executives repeatedly assured Live Nation that the venue company would not independently compete for concert-promotion business. In one exchange, then-OVG CEO Tim Leiweke allegedly told Live Nation CEO Michael Rapino: “I never want to be competitors.”

The complaint alleged that OVG generally stayed out of concert promotion while Live Nation reduced or abandoned its competing arena-consulting operations. The companies sometimes partnered on venue agreements and shared profits rather than bidding against one another.

Their 2022 ticketing agreement extended that alignment into primary ticketing. According to the complaint, the deal made Ticketmaster the exclusive primary ticketer at OVG-owned venues and required OVG to advocate for Ticketmaster at more than 100 venues it managed for third-party clients.

DOJ alleged that the arrangement helped remove potential opportunities for competing ticketing companies and gave OVG a financial interest in maintaining Ticketmaster’s market position.

## Azoff and Leiweke Remain Central to the Companies’ Shared History

OVG was founded in 2015 by Leiweke and Irving Azoff, whose careers connect the company directly to the formation and leadership of Live Nation Entertainment.

Azoff was Ticketmaster’s chief executive when it merged with Live Nation in 2010. He became executive chairman of the combined company and later served as chairman of its board before resigning in 2012. He subsequently co-founded OVG while continuing to oversee major artist-management interests.

Leiweke’s tenure at OVG ended amid a separate federal antitrust case.

A grand jury [indicted him in July 2025](https://www.ticketnews.com/2025/07/oak-view-group-ceo-tim-leiweke-indicted-in-alleged-bid-rigging-scheme-for-university-of-texas-moody-center/) for allegedly arranging for a competing venue-services company to stand down during bidding for the University of Texas’ Moody Center. OVG ultimately submitted the only qualified bid. Leiweke denied wrongdoing, and the allegations were never adjudicated after President Donald Trump granted him a full pardon in December.

OVG agreed to pay a $15 million penalty connected to the alleged conduct, while Legends Hospitality agreed to pay $1.5 million. Leiweke stepped down as chief executive after the indictment, and Chris Granger now leads OVG.

Two days after receiving the pardon, Leiweke invoked his Fifth Amendment rights and declined to answer Justice Department questions during a deposition in the Live Nation antitrust litigation, [according to prior TicketNews reporting](https://www.ticketnews.com/2025/12/fresh-off-trump-pardon-ovgs-leiweke-refuses-to-testify-in-live-nation-antitrust-suit/). His potential testimony was considered important because he had direct knowledge of OVG’s concert-promotion discussions and ticketing relationship with Live Nation.

The federal government later settled its portion of the Live Nation case. A group of state attorneys general rejected that deal, continued through trial and secured a jury verdict finding Live Nation and Ticketmaster liable on remaining antitrust claims. Those states are now pursuing broader remedies while DOJ’s proposed settlement proceeds through the Tunney Act public-interest review process.

Against that backdrop, radia provides another concrete example of the breadth of the Live Nation–OVG relationship.

The proposed DOJ settlement would cut off one particularly controversial mechanism through which Ticketmaster paid OVG to influence venue ticketing decisions. It would not, however, prevent the companies from pooling their venue, promotion and programming operations in overseas markets.

In Saudi Arabia, that continuing alignment will now sit at the center of one of the world’s largest sports and entertainment infrastructure expansions.
