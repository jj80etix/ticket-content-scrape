# ‘DC has raised the bar for ticketing reform nationwide’

## Summary

The D.C. Council voted unanimously on July 14 to pass The Resale Act, which would take effect January 1, 2027 if signed by Mayor Muriel Bowser. The bill imposes a 10% price cap on resale tickets, bans speculative ticket sales, requires licensing for "mass resellers" (50+ tickets/year across platforms), and mandates all-in pricing, a refund policy, transfer procedures, and a ban on surveillance-based price discrimination against resale buyers. Industry advocates including NIVA and independent promoter/venue operator I.M.P. (9:30 Club, The Anthem) praised it as a national model, coming weeks after Live Nation/Ticketmaster paid roughly $9.9M to settle a decade-long DC AG deceptive-pricing investigation and amid a new StubHub scalping-related class action.

### Key Points
- D.C. Council passed The Resale Act unanimously on July 14, 2026; awaits Mayor Bowser's signature; effective January 1, 2027 if signed.
- Sets a 10% price cap on resale tickets and bans speculative ticket sales (selling tickets not yet in hand).
- Requires licensing for "mass resellers" defined as 50+ tickets/year, counting the same ticket listed across multiple platforms.
- Mandates all-in pricing, a defined refund policy, transfer procedures, and bars surveillance-based dynamic pricing of resale buyers.
- Follows Live Nation's ~$9.9M April settlement with the DC AG over deceptive Ticketmaster pricing and a new StubHub class action over an alleged scalping operation.
- NIVA and I.M.P. publicly called D.C. a "blueprint" they expect other states to follow.

### Highlights
- 10% resale price cap; 50-ticket/year threshold defines a "mass reseller" subject to licensing.
- Effective date January 1, 2027, pending mayoral signature.
- Mandates all-in pricing and bans surveillance/dynamic pricing targeted at resale buyers.
- Follows Live Nation's $9.9M DC deceptive-pricing settlement and a fresh StubHub class action, signaling escalating enforcement momentum.

### Industry Problem
Uncapped, unlicensed resale combined with opaque junk-fee pricing and surveillance-based price discrimination is driving fan and regulator backlash, and jurisdictions are now codifying price caps, reseller licensing, and all-in pricing requirements one city/state at a time — raising compliance complexity for any platform touching primary pricing display or resale.

### Proposed Solution
The Act is a regulatory mandate rather than a vendor product, but the actionable read for Etix is to get ahead of the jurisdiction-by-jurisdiction spread of resale price caps and all-in pricing rules NIVA is calling a "blueprint": build configurable resale price-cap enforcement, mass-reseller license verification, and all-in price display into the platform now, before more states adopt D.C.'s model.

## ‘DC has raised the bar for ticketing reform nationwide’

The US's District of Columbia council voted unanimously to pass The Resale Act, a move welcomed by local and national organisations

![Belle & Sebastian at The 9:30 Club](https://www.iqmagazine.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/07/Belle-Sebastian-at-The-930-Club-©-Andrew-WisemanWikimedia-Commons.png)

Councilmembers in the US’s District of Columbia (D.C.) voted unanimously to pass The Resale Act yesterday (14 July), greenlighting landmark legislation that could transform resale in the country’s capital district.

If signed off by Mayor Muriel Bowser, The Resale Act will strengthen consumer protections for those in the federal district by establishing a 10% price cap on resale tickets, banning speculative ticket sales, and requiring mass resellers to register for a license.

In this case, mass resale is described as 50 or more tickets per year, inclusive of the same ticket listed on multiple sites.

Additionally, the legislation lays out a refunding policy, all-in pricing, establishes transfer procedures, and cracks down on the use of surveillance on resale buyers. This is when online sellers utilise personal data to charge different prices for the same product.

“Today, the D.C. Council stood with D.C.’s most beloved and iconic music venues, with our storied theatres, and with every music and live theatre fan who’s tired of getting ripped off and priced out,” says Councilmember Charles Allen, the act’s author.

“Ticket resellers have used technology and unrestrained profits to turn our live entertainment scene into the wild west – just last week it was revealed [StubHub](https://www.iqmagazine.com/2026/07/stubhub-named-in-us-class-action-over-deceptive-practices/) has been running a major ticket scalping operation while they’ve argued they’re just a platform for fans to sell extra tickets. People are sick of big tech wringing more and more money out of them. D.C. is a leader here, and I expect many other jurisdictions to step in once they see we can save our residents money and keep more dollars in our local economy instead of propping up big tech,” he says.

With the measures, D.C. councilmembers are seeking to boost transparency and fairness in resale practices for the area’s fans, artists, venues, and festivals, a move welcomed by local players and national organisations.

> “We hope states across the country will look to Washington, D.C. as the blueprint”

The National Independent Venue Association (NIVA)’s executive director, Stephen Parker, says the district had “raised the bar for ticketing reform nationwide”.

“From the resale price cap to stronger safeguards against deceptive ticketing practices, the Resale Act puts fans first and strengthens the District’s independent stages. We commend Councilmember Allen, Councilmember Henderson, Councilmember Pinto, the entire D.C. Council, and the many local advocates whose time and leadership made this landmark achievement possible.

“We hope states across the country will look to Washington, D.C. as the blueprint for the next generation of resale ticketing reform,” Parker concludes.

Others see it as “an end to predatory scalpers” in the local market, according to Audrey Fix Schaefer, communications director for independent promoter I.M.P. and venues 9:30 Club and The Anthem.

“This will stop opportunistic profiteers from making more than the artists, and it will enable more fans to see more shows, and then cross the street to enjoy a meal in the neighbourhood restaurants, since they still have enough money in their pockets. The Resale Act will keep millions of dollars in D.C.,” Schaefer says.

If it advances, The Resale Act would take effect from 1 January 2027.

Legislative bodies in D.C. have been cracking down on ticketing practices in recent months, with Live Nation agreeing in April to pay nearly $10 million (€8.5m) over [misleading ticket pricing practices](https://www.iqmagazine.com/2026/04/live-nation-settles-deceptive-ticketing-claims-in-dc-for-9-9m/) through its subsidiary, Ticketmaster, after a decade-long investigation by the Office of the Attorney General.
