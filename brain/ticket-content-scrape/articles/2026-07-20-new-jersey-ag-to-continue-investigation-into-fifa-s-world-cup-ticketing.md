# New Jersey AG to continue investigation into FIFA’s World Cup ticketing

## Summary

New Jersey's Attorney General says the state will continue investigating FIFA's World Cup ticketing practices even though the tournament concluded July 19. The probe, launched jointly with New York in May via subpoenas into FIFA's pricing and transparency practices, centers on complaints that fans were misled about seat locations, subjected to variable/dynamic pricing and manufactured scarcity, and hit with a new, pricier ticket tier FIFA added after millions of tickets had already been sold. NJ AG Jennifer Davenport said it's too early to discuss potential penalties but framed the inquiry as consumer protection amid an "affordability crisis."

### Key Points
- Investigation launched in May 2026 by the NY and NJ Attorneys General; subpoenas sent to FIFA over pricing and transparency.
- Complaints cite misleading stadium seat-location maps, variable/dynamic pricing, and perceived ticket scarcity.
- FIFA introduced a new, more expensive ticket tier after millions of tickets had already sold.
- NJ AG Jennifer Davenport says it is "too early" to discuss possible penalties.
- Investigation continues post-tournament (World Cup concluded July 19, 2026).

### Highlights
- NY and NJ AGs jointly subpoenaed FIFA in May 2026 over ticket pricing and transparency.
- Complaints cite misleading seat maps, variable/dynamic pricing, and scarcity perception.
- FIFA added a new higher-priced ticket tier after millions of tickets were already sold.
- Investigation remains open post-event, with no timeline yet on findings or penalties.

### Industry Problem
The case evidences growing state-AG consumer-protection scrutiny of primary-market pricing practices — dynamic/variable pricing, mid-sale tier changes, scarcity messaging, and seat-location transparency — framed explicitly around ticket "affordability," the same state-patchwork pressure primary ticketing platforms face broadly.

### Proposed Solution
The article describes no remedy yet; as an open, unresolved investigation this is a monitor item — watch for any settlement or consent terms FIFA agrees to (seat-map accuracy standards, pricing-tier disclosure rules), since state AGs often reuse such terms as templates against other primary-market ticket sellers.

**The Attorney General of New Jersey has said that the state will continue its investigation into FIFA’s ticketing practices following the conclusion of the World Cup yesterday (July 19).**

In May, the Attorneys General of New York and New Jersey launched an investigation into the governing body’s ticketing practices and sent [subpoenas](https://www.theticketingbusiness.com/2026/05/fifa-subpoenaed-over-world-cup-ticketing-prices/) into the organisation’s pricing and transparency.

The AGs cited complaints from fans, including some who felt misled by stadium maps over the location of tickets, FIFA’s use of variable pricing, the perception of scarcity in available tickets, as well as FIFA’s invention of a higher-tiered and [more expensive category](https://www.theticketingbusiness.com/2026/04/fifa-adds-new-more-expensive-world-cup-ticket-tier/) after millions of tickets had already been sold.

“Our appetite is to make sure that we’re protecting consumers all the time,” said New Jersey Attorney General Jennifer Davenport, as reported by [The Athletic](https://www.nytimes.com/athletic/7447423/2026/07/17/new-jersey-world-cup-ticket-investigation/).

“The exploitation of fans and consumers is a huge piece of that. This started because of what we were seeing in reporting and when we have information like that, we want to make sure that we use our laws and our protections to make sure that we’re looking into it, especially as we’re grappling with an affordability crisis.

“We’re really taking a look at all the different ways that we can protect consumers. We are looking at a few issues. The first is whether or not fans were misled about the seating location of the tickets they bought.”

Davenport said that it is “too early” to talk about penalties that could be imposed on FIFA, but wants “to make sure that fans and consumers aren’t being exploited”.
