# The Architects: Pino Sagliocco, part two

## Summary

Part two of IQ Magazine's career retrospective on veteran Spanish promoter Pino Sagliocco, covering his rise from Queen's Spanish promoter during the Magic Tour through decades of landmark concerts (Rolling Stones, Madonna, Whitney Houston) that helped establish Spain as a major touring market.

### Key Points
- Recounts Sagliocco's high-risk early promotion of Queen's 1986 Magic Tour dates in Spain, including a near-disastrous Madrid show saved by a last-minute Freddie Mercury TV interview.
- Brokered the 1987 Mercury/Montserrat Caballé duet, later the official 1992 Barcelona Olympics anthem.
- Went on to promote Spanish dates for The Rolling Stones, Madonna, Whitney Houston, Prince, David Bowie and others.
- Reflects on running promotions with personal financial risk, without label/corporate backing (pre-consolidation era).

**The ghosts of August  
**The jump to the big leagues was not without its stresses. Handed three early-August dates on the Queen’s *Magic Tour* for shows in Barcelona, Madrid, and Marbella, Sagliocco was confronted with a stark reality of the extremely hot Spanish summers: everyone leaves the capital for the coast.

“The Barcelona show, I can defend myself; Costa Brava is fine, because all the people are there from the cities for their holiday. But in those days, Madrid at the beginning of August was a dead city. The city shut down. You didn’t even see a cat on the street. Not a bar open. Nothing.”

The Barcelona show at Miniestadi on 1 August went with a bang, but with the Sunday-night show at Madrid’s Estadio del Rayo Vallecano just two days away, Sagliocco’s fears of a flop seemed distinctly realistic.

“I think I had sold about 500 tickets. Out of 25,000,” he laughs. “I was scared shitless.”

Sagliocco’s financial solvency depended on avoiding a washout in Madrid, and the fear is still alive in his voice. “I didn’t have any back-up. I was not like Live Nation; it was my own money. There was no one giving you credit. You basically had to go into the bank, and they would want a guarantee.”

The promoter took his concerns to his drummer friend. “I said, ‘Roger, we have to do something. I don’t know what is going to happen in Madrid. Do you think Freddie \[Mercury\] can give an interview on Spanish TV?’ Freddie never gave interviews. But somehow, magic happened, and he said yes.”

Sagliocco admits he was too scared and too busy to watch the broadcast. All he could do was wait for the walk-up. “At 12 o’clock, I start to see people queueing up. By six, when the door opens, the queue is like a bloody nightmare. Oh my god, that was the best time of my life.”

In 1987, Sagliocco brokered the Barcelona duet between Mercury and Spanish soprano Montserrat Caballé as the centrepiece of his Ibiza ‘92 television broadcast from the Ku Club. The collaboration, marking a typically ambitious fusion of opera and pop, later became the official anthem for the 1992 Barcelona Olympic Games, and in the meantime, Sagliocco became a TV producer, broadcasting a succession of star-studded shows from Ibiza and elsewhere in Spain.

> “I strongly believe that music is still the only opportunity to find a place where we all belong. Because the world is fucked up”

“I decided to use music to bring Spain to the world. I did The Rolling Stones from Barcelona for the world. Madonna from Barcelona for the world. Whitney Houston from A Coruña, George Michael from Madrid, Leonard Cohen and Paul Simon from San Sebastián.

“So basically, I used music to put Spain on the map, big time. And I became the biggest promoter at that time. I was doing all the big stars: Prince, David Bowie, Michael Jackson, Madonna – I basically worked with everybody.”

**I’m not here to give a speech  
**Long-time ILMC-goers may recall that Sagliocco wasn’t always 100% delighted with life at the top of the mountain. Riled by back-biting and tough times in the business, he used 1994’s conference as a platform for a blistering denunciation of industry short-termism and promoters’ disproportionate share of the everyday risk in the international concert business.

It ended with a call for the music industry to pledge excess profit to organisations for children, homeless people, and the environment, etc. “I’m sure they will be happier physically and spiritually,” he said at the time, “instead of spending their time worrying about offshore companies, tax advisors, fiscal paradises, and all the legal tricks to hide the money.”

“I had a really big crisis in the business,” he says. “It was a really emotional speech, and by the time I finished, I got a standing ovation. But everything I said in that speech is still as alive today as it was then. I was basically saying, ‘You know what? Too much bullshit in this business.’ I was like, ‘I need a new inspiration.’”

Disillusioned, Sagliocco resolved only to work with people he loved. “I was doing Prince. I did the last three tours for Michael Jackson. We became very good friends. I went to Never- land a few times with him. But then he was accused. It was the biggest injustice I ever saw in my life. Nobody was defending him. I was very disappointed.”

> “A corporate company is more about volume and market share; I’m not about volume and market share. I’m a passionate man. I’m old-fashioned”

**¡Olé!  
**An avid flamenco fan, Sagliocco took up the cause of dancer Joaquín Cortés – once again with the glory of Spain in mind. “I spent basically nine years going around the world with him. I had the best time ever. He basically became a world star.

“We did the Grammys with Alicia Keys, we did the Oscars, he did the duet with Jennifer Lopez, performed with Pavarotti. He danced for the King of Spain; Armani was dressing him.

“But with Joaquín, I sort of lost interest because of ego. People ask me, what are your jobs? I say, ‘Ego administrator and fireman.’ That’s my job. That’s what I do all the time, but at the time, I just couldn’t handle it anymore.”

Live Nation promoter Iain Hill recalls the deterioration of that successful partnership. “Faced with Joaquín Cortés storming out of a venue where he was meant to dance in a gala show, Pino changed into the dancer’s stage costume and was having hair and makeup done, and willing to go on stage, when Cortés returned to the dressing room. The look on his face was priceless; he knew Pino was capable of going on stage and even stealing the show. Cortes then reacted and did his best performance ever.”

Sagliocco went back into the promoting business in 2002, merging his Troubleshooter company with Gay Mercader’s Gamerco, which in turn was sold to Live Nation in 2006. That the deal has been a successful one goes without saying: Spain has emerged as the European landing strip for a generation of Latin American superstars, even as the country has developed a serious pipeline of its own talent, and Live Nation Spain has a major stake in both. The fact that Spain is also an essential stop for broader international tours is almost a side detail.

“80% of the stadiums we are doing are Spanish artists, Latino artists, not international artists. We have never seen anything like that before, because that’s what changed. And also, we are really getting the voices now in Latin music. And unfortunately, we need more voices. Look what Bad Bunny did at the Super Bowl. Look what he did at the Grammys. We need more of those voices.”

One more secret ingredient of the booming Spanish business, he believes, nodding in the direction of his outlandish Shakira project, is “because we’re doing this kind of thing. Because we still believe creativity is driving the world, and we still believe that you have to have passion for what you do. And I believe we cannot see everything as \[being about\] money. Money is a consequence if you do things right.”

**I sell my dreams  
**Has it been easy for a maverick to adjust to life inside a global corporate?

“It’s been the hardest part, probably, in my life, because when you are in a corporate company, you have to adapt to a different concept of business. A corporate company is more about volume and market share. I’m not about volume and market share. I’m a passionate man. I’m old-fashioned. I fight for every penny. I fight for every dime. And I want to take care of every corner.

“So, it was not easy,” he reflects. “In the company, it’s been a lot of ups and downs. But I must say, times have changed. I have had the opportunity to \[get to know\] Michael Rapino better these past few years, and since then, I think it has been an honour and a pleasure to work with someone like him, because I think he has respect for people like us, and he understands who we are and what our nature is.

“I believe the music business really has been built from people like me and Leon Ramakers, Bill Graham, Barrie Marshall, Thomas Johansson – you know, real promoters. Music lovers, passionate about the job.”

Does that job still exist? Can a young promoter follow a picaresque, Sagliocco-style path in the modern world? Sagliocco, characteristically, very much hopes they can, but he does not dodge the possibility that it may be trickier than it was.

“That is one of the biggest questions of my life, because a lot of new people who come into this business are not promoters, they’re more like bookers, and they just deliver services. And as long as I am alive and kicking, I will still try to inspire people to be promoters. You have to be a music lover. You have to be always there, willing to take a risk, and willing to think outside the box if you want to make something really incredible.

“If you take a risk, and you do it yourself, then the big ones will buy you. And I hope that when they buy those creatives or those promoters, they don’t put them back in the box again. Buy them, keep them \[thinking outside\] of the box, then you can really create something different. And I believe Michael \[Rapino\] has that mentality. I really believe so. I think as long as he stays at the company, I believe the company will have that kind of understanding.”
