# National Consumers League Pushes for “Gold Standard” Ticketing Reform at House of Representatives Hearing

## Summary

The National Consumers League used a July 22 House Energy and Commerce consumer-protection hearing to renew its push for the BOSS and SWIFT Act, calling it the "gold standard" for federal ticketing reform — broader than the narrower TICKET Act already pending in the Senate. NCL VP John Breyault argued that antitrust enforcement alone (citing the jury verdict against Live Nation/Ticketmaster) won't fix consumer protections, and that federal law must also govern primary-market inventory disclosure, holdbacks, and transfer rights, not just resale. NCL also separately endorsed the MAIN Event Ticketing Act to strengthen the 2016 BOTS Act's anti-bot enforcement.

### Key Points
- BOSS and SWIFT Act (2026 version) would require primary sellers to disclose ticket counts and onsale timing before sales begin, addressing holdbacks and staged inventory releases.
- It would codify all-in pricing across both primary and secondary sales, require displayed prices to include mandatory non-government fees, and mandate refunds for canceled/postponed events.
- It would bar primary sellers from restricting resale to affiliated platforms only, require secondary sites to clearly identify themselves, and add disclosure requirements for speculative listings.
- The narrower TICKET Act already passed the House 409-15 and sits on the Senate calendar; it requires all-in pricing, regulates speculative listings, mandates refunds, and gives the FTC enforcement authority.
- The Irving Azoff-aligned Fix the Tix coalition has historically opposed BOSS and SWIFT, especially its ticket-transferability and anti-restriction provisions.
- NCL also endorsed the MAIN Event Ticketing Act (H.R. 2713), which would update the BOTS Act with stronger access controls, mandatory attempt-reporting, and a new FTC consumer complaint portal.
- Breyault cited the FTC's 2025 lawsuit alleging Ticketmaster tolerated brokers who circumvented purchase limits while collecting fees on both the initial sale and resale.

### Highlights
- BOSS and SWIFT would mandate all-in pricing and pre-sale disclosure of ticket counts/holdbacks on the primary market — a materially higher disclosure bar than current practice.
- The MAIN Event Ticketing Act (H.R. 2713) would require ticket issuers to actively report bot-circumvention attempts and maintain documented safeguards, not just rely on after-the-fact FTC/broker enforcement.
- TICKET Act (409-15 House passage) remains the more immediately viable federal vehicle and already mandates all-in pricing and FTC enforcement authority.
- No committee vote occurred and BOSS and SWIFT still lacks an assigned House bill number, so nothing here is imminent law — but the direction of travel (disclosure + all-in pricing + anti-bot) is consistent across both bills.

### Industry Problem
Patrons and regulators remain unconvinced that pricing transparency and inventory disclosure exist today — hidden holdbacks, non-all-in pricing, and bot circumvention are treated as unresolved, ongoing harms even after antitrust action against Ticketmaster.

### Proposed Solution
The bills themselves would mandate all-in pricing, pre-sale inventory/holdback disclosure, and stronger anti-bot access controls and reporting. For Etix, this is a posture-monitoring item: since Etix already supports transparent pricing for independent venues, getting ahead of holdback-disclosure and anti-bot reporting requirements (audit trails, purchase-limit enforcement documentation) before they become mandatory would be a low-risk improvement and a differentiator versus platforms built around scarcity/holdback tactics.

**The consumer group urged lawmakers to pursue the BOSS and Swift Act, which includes holdback disclosures, ticket-transfer protections and stronger anti-bot enforcement alongside the narrower TICKET Act already before the Senate.**

The National Consumers League used a House consumer-protection hearing Wednesday to revive support for one of Congress’ longest-running and most comprehensive ticketing-reform proposals, [calling the latest version of the BOSS and SWIFT Act the “gold standard for live event ticketing reform.”](https://nclnet.org/national-consumers-league-urges-congress-stronger-protections-for-consumers-ticketing-reform-and-anti-fraud-collaboration/)

John Breyault, NCL’s vice president of public policy, telecommunications and fraud, testified before the House Energy and Commerce Subcommittee on Commerce, Manufacturing and Trade during a July 22 hearing examining a wide range of consumer-protection legislation. Along with BOSS and SWIFT, NCL endorsed the bipartisan MAIN Event Ticketing Act, which would strengthen the federal prohibition against automated ticket-buying bots.

The hearing did not constitute a committee vote or formally advance either proposal. Committee materials listed the BOSS and SWIFT Act of 2026 without an assigned House bill number, suggesting the latest version remains draft legislation. The measure is now being championed by Rep. Frank Pallone, D-N.J., following the death of its longtime architect, former Rep. Bill Pascrell.

NCL argued that federal ticketing legislation must address conduct throughout the ticketing ecosystem rather than concentrating almost exclusively on brokers and the secondary market.

Breyault pointed to consumer complaints surrounding the recently concluded World Cup and the jury verdict finding Live Nation and Ticketmaster liable on monopoly claims as evidence that antitrust enforcement alone will not establish clear consumer protections for buying, using and reselling tickets. The group said the marketplace also needs federal rules covering primary sales, inventory disclosures, transfers and resale transactions.

Under the version outlined in Breyault’s testimony, primary ticket sellers would be required to disclose how many tickets will be offered to the public before an onsale begins, along with the date and time of the general sale. NCL said the requirement would give consumers more visibility into ticket holdbacks and staged inventory releases that can create the appearance of scarcity.

The bill would also codify all-in pricing across primary and secondary ticket sales, requiring the displayed price to include mandatory nongovernment fees from the beginning of the purchasing process.

Other provisions would prevent primary sellers from restricting ticket resale exclusively to affiliated platforms, require resale websites to clearly identify themselves as secondary marketplaces and provide greater disclosure around speculative listings involving tickets a seller does not yet possess. Consumers would also receive refund protections when events are canceled or postponed.

Those provisions make BOSS and SWIFT substantially broader than the TICKET Act, currently the most advanced federal ticketing bill.

The TICKET Act passed the House by a 409-15 vote and has been placed on the Senate calendar. It would require upfront all-in pricing, regulate speculative ticket listings, mandate refunds for canceled or significantly postponed events and give the Federal Trade Commission enforcement authority. NCL urged Senate leaders in May to bring the measure to a vote, calling it the best immediately available opportunity to establish national ticketing protections.

BOSS and SWIFT would go further by addressing the amount of inventory made available during public onsales and protecting consumers’ ability to transfer or resell tickets through competing platforms.

NCL’s position therefore draws a distinction between the bill most likely to move and the legislation it considers the more complete solution. The group continues to support passage of the TICKET Act while arguing that BOSS and SWIFT better addresses the primary-market practices and restrictions that shape ticket availability and consumer choice.

## A Reform Proposal Years in the Making

The BOSS legislation has been introduced in different forms since Pascrell first proposed ticketing reforms in 2009. Pascrell and Pallone introduced an expanded BOSS and SWIFT Act in the House in 2023, with the name referencing the ticketing controversies surrounding Bruce Springsteen and Taylor Swift.

Sens. Richard Blumenthal, Mazie Hirono and Edward Markey subsequently introduced a Senate version. Blumenthal renewed his call for the legislation in 2025, arguing that reforms should cover hidden charges, ticket distribution and other practices beyond the narrower provisions being considered through the TICKET Act.

Previous versions have also faced resistance from music-industry groups.

The [Irving Azoff-aligned Fix the Tix coalition opposed the 2023 bill](https://www.google.com/url?sa=t&source=web&rct=j&opi=89978449&url=https://www.ticketnews.com/2023/05/irving-azoff-led-coalition-fights-boss-and-swift-act-reforms/&ved=2ahUKEwixmP-GhumVAxWFz_ACHdIjA0IQFnoECBgQAQ&usg=AOvVaw1hN-AVYR53P97jmySq9aBL), focusing much of its criticism on provisions protecting ticket transferability and preventing event operators from forcing resale transactions onto approved platforms. The coalition instead supports legislative measures that deal exclusively with ticket resale platforms, rather than any price surging or consumer issues happening in the primary market.

Consumer-choice advocates have warned that such restrictions, when imposed without comparable limits on primary-market pricing, holdbacks and transfer controls, could reduce competition and steer transactions back toward Ticketmaster or other incumbent primary sellers.

That disagreement remains one of the central divisions in federal ticketing policy: whether lawmakers should give artists and event operators more control over tickets after purchase or protect the purchaser’s ability to transfer and resell them through the marketplace of their choice.

## NCL Also Backs Stronger Anti-Bot Requirements

NCL separately endorsed the MAIN Event Ticketing Act, H.R. 2713, introduced by Reps. Diana Harshbarger, R-Tenn., and Troy Carter, D-La.

The proposal would update the 2016 BOTS Act by requiring ticket issuers to use stronger access controls, report known attempts to circumvent ticket limits and maintain safeguards governing ticketing systems and outside vendors. It would also direct the FTC to create a consumer complaint portal and improve information sharing with state attorneys general and law enforcement.

Breyault tied the need for stronger enforcement to allegations made in the FTC’s 2025 lawsuit against Live Nation and Ticketmaster. According to NCL’s testimony, the agency alleged that Ticketmaster tolerated sophisticated brokers who circumvented purchase limits while the company collected fees from the initial sale and subsequent resale transactions.

Live Nation and Ticketmaster have disputed government allegations that their practices violate antitrust or consumer-protection law.

NCL argued that updating the BOTS Act would place greater responsibility on ticketing platforms to prevent automated circumvention rather than relying almost entirely on after-the-fact enforcement against individual brokers.

The TICKET Act remains Congress’ most immediate opportunity to enact ticketing legislation. Wednesday’s hearing nevertheless showed that the broader debate has not disappeared.

The question before lawmakers is no longer limited to whether prices should include fees or whether bots should be prohibited. It is also whether federal reform will address the primary market’s control over inventory, scarcity signals and ticket transfers — the issues BOSS and SWIFT has attempted to bring into federal law for more than a decade.
