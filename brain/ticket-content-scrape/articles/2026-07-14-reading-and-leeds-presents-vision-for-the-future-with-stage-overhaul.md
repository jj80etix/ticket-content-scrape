# Reading and Leeds presents ‘vision for the future’ with stage overhaul

## Summary

Festival Republic is overhauling Reading and Leeds Festival's staging for 2026, introducing six new named stages and broadening its booking/programming leadership team. Managing director Melvin Benn frames the changes as necessary to differentiate the 37-year-old festival from competing stadium shows and one-day events by investing in production quality and camper experience.

### Key Points
- Six new stages (The Grid, The Gallery, The Warehouse, The Canopy, The Ballroom, The Yard) debut at the 27-30 August event.
- Promoters James Ponnusamy and Zach Desmond take expanded leadership roles alongside talent buyers Max Kafka and Jojo Mathiszig-Lee.
- The Grid (main stage) headliners include Charli xcx, Fontaines D.C., Dave, Raye, Florence + The Machine, and Chase & Status.
- Benn cites competition from stadium shows and audience demand for enhanced campsite/production experiences as the rationale.


## Transcript

## Reading and Leeds presents ‘vision for the future’ with stage overhaul

Festival Republic managing director Melvin Benn tells IQ about the plans for new stages and a broadened leadership team as part of the festival's next phase

![](https://www.iqmagazine.com/wp-content/smush-webp/2026/07/Reading-and-Leeds-scaled.jpeg.webp)

Reading and Leeds Festival has announced what it calls “the biggest staging and arena overhaul” in its 37-year history, with Festival Republic managing director Melvin Benn saying six new stages are set lead the festival into its “next phase”.

Organisers said the changes will “take the festival firmly into its next evolution”, explaining the aim is to “change the way Reading and Leeds Festival is seen”. With it, the festival is broadening the leadership of its artist booking and programming team, with promoters James Ponnusamy and Zach Desmond to take on expanded roles, supported by talent buyers Max Kafka and Jojo Mathiszig-Lee.

Starting with its event on 27-30 August, stages titled The Grid, The Gallery (presented by Budweiser), The Warehouse, The Canopy, The Ballroom, and The Yard (Reading only) will be introduced to the festival.

Managing director Benn said part of the festival entering next phase is empowering other team members to take the lead on aspects of festival operation and planning.

“He has been part of the team for a long time, but James Ponnusamy is now leading that vision and that talent with Max and Jojo. What we’re beginning to present now is their vision of the future of the festivals, their vision for the next phase of Reading and Leeds.”

“I don’t know why now is the right time,” Benn said when asked what sparked the decision for 2026. “When’s the right time for parents to have a baby? When’s the right time for parents to have a second baby? When’s the right time for me as the parent of Reading and Leeds to give the reins to somebody else? Now is the right time,” he continued.

And asked if the changes represent Festival Republic aiming to get ahead of trends across the market, Benn said the core driver was ensuring festivals stand out to artists against stadium and one-day open-air offerings.

“I think when you look at the popularity of live music, the competition that we face with stadiums and acts wanting to play their own shows in stadiums or in Finsbury Park, for instance, the question that I had to ask myself was: what takes Reading and Leeds and separates it from every other festival environment, and makes it the best festival environment for live music that we can possibly create?”

> “The music is getting better and better and better. But the audience wants more.”

He continued: “And that was a statement that was: ‘let’s do it at this point in time because James and Max and JoJo have got great ideas about what they want the festival to be in the future’. I’m embracing that and helping them with it and developing it with them, ensuring that that next phase has got a long future.”

“It’s wonderful, it’s a breath of fresh air. It’s new blood, and it gives Reading and Leeds that vision for the future,” Benn added.

The managing director opined that the health of the UK festival sector is largely in line with the wider live ecosystem. However, he argued that camping festivals, as providers not only of music but of wider accommodation and lifestyle experiences, are well-placed to offer the type of “enhanced” fan experience that fans are increasingly demanding.

“The music is getting better and better,” he says of recent years in the festival space, “but the audience wants more”.

“That’s the change: audiences for years and years were less and less demanding. They were wanting to see the acts, they were camping in environments that could have been better but weren’t better. We’re now at that point where we want those stage presentations to be a statement,” Benn told *IQ.*

“We want the artist to be playing in an environment that they remember as being Reading Festival and Leeds Festival. We want those campsite facilities to stand out as opposed to just being regular. We want those toilets to be better, the showers to be better. It’s that enhanced audience experience,” he said.

> “It’s wonderful, it’s a breath of fresh air. It’s new blood, and it gives Reading and Leeds that vision for the future”

“Historically, presenting a stage at a festival other than the main stage is about putting a stage in a big top with some lights and sound and a bit of a screen,” Benn told *IQ* of the thinking behind the overhaul. “With the Gallery, with the Canopy, with the Ballroom, and obviously the Warehouse, it’s about creating an environment that’s a total environment within this space. And that, I think, is our vision for the future.”

The new stages are designed to cultivate their own identities, organisers said. They include The Grid as the main stage, on which this year’s headliners will perform. Kasabian are also confirmed as the first ever Thursday night headliner of Leeds Festival on the stage after the festival extended its licensing hours for the day.

The Grid will be the festival’s main stage, where headliners including Charli xcx, Fontaines D.C., Dave, Raye, Florence + The Machine, and Chase & Status will play this year.

Speaking of the Warehouse stage, which will be devoted to dance music, Benn told *IQ:* “One of, if not the most important, addition would be the Warehouse and bringing back a dance music stage. I had previously tried to combine what was a dance music stage and a second stage with the Chevron.

“That never quite worked,” Benn reflected. “It looked great, it felt great, it sounded great, but that mix of acts between bands and DJs didn’t quite sit properly, and the bands were really clear about the need for a new dance stage to come back.

“Adding the Warehouse for me was really the key to the changes. That means that the Gallery, the second stage, becomes much more of a band and loud music focus.”

He continued that the creation of Canopy aims to strengthen the festival’s abilities to showcase new artists.

“Historically we’ve had the BBC Introducing stages for the emerging acts, and it was a really inexpensively presented stage. We want to create something that’s significantly better and stronger and has a great feel about it,” Benn said.

But it is the Ballroom Stage that Benn said is “the best demonstration of change that we see as a vision for the future”. The Ballroom will be complete with unique chandeliers and draping that plays host to artists who are “defining and dominating 2026”, festival organisers said, adding that the aim is for the stage to mix “genre, styles, and moments”.

These new stages are joined in Leeds by the return of the “fan favourite” LS23 and Piccadilly Circus stages, with full programming to be revealed in the coming weeks for the latter, organisers said.

Ultimately, festivals have some inherent advantage in this competitive landscape, the managing director said, citing the “sense of community that is garnered at a festival”, and the knowledge “that you are of like mind with the people that are there, that you wake up in a tent and the people in the tent next door to you are of like mind and you can chat with them and socialise with them and become friends with them.”

Speaking of the condition of the wider space, the festival stalwart told *IQ* he is sanguine about the future, in part because of recent developments in what events are offering, but also because of the fundamentals of the festival experience.

“At a festival, you really get to know your neighbours. You become part of a community. That’s what sets them apart,” Benn summarised. “But actually, to keep them alive, to keep them strong, I think the way we’re leading the way with Reading and Leeds, creating better environments in the campsites as well as in the music performance areas, I think that will be the future of festivals.”
