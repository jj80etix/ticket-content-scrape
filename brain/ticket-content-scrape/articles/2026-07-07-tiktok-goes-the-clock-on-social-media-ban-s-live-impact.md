# TikTok goes the clock on social media ban’s live impact

## Summary

As more countries move to restrict social media access for minors (following Australia's under-16 ban), live-music industry figures are weighing the long-term impact on artist discovery and marketing, since platforms like TikTok currently drive the bulk of viral breakthroughs and campaign spend. Agents and promoters interviewed say the near-term ticket-buying impact is limited since minors aren't the primary purchasers, but expect discovery to shift toward touring, traditional media, and multi-channel strategies for developing artists.

### Key Points
- Over a dozen countries (Canada, France, UK, Spain, etc.) are considering child social-media bans following Australia's precedent.
- 84% of songs that hit Billboard's Global 200 in 2024 went viral on TikTok first, per a TikTok/Luminate report.
- Agents report ~99% of marketing budgets currently flow through social platforms.
- Frontier Touring says on-sale/announce engagement from under-16s has softened slightly in Australia post-ban, though overall ticket sales are largely unaffected since minors aren't primary buyers.
- Industry figures expect a resurgence of touring, TV talent shows, radio, and out-of-home marketing as discovery channels for younger artists if the bans spread.

## Transcript

## TikTok goes the clock on social media ban’s live impact

![A fan records a Coldplay show](https://www.iqmagazine.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/07/A-fan-records-a-Coldplay-show.png)

A fan records a Coldplay show

With several global governments weighing up banning social media access for children, questions have begun circulating about the widespread impacts on music discovery, touring campaigns, and the value of live experiences for the next generation, signalling that the international live music industry could soon be at a crossroads.

Last December, Australia became the world’s first country to ban social media access for children under 16 years old, blocking the likes of Instagram, TikTok, YouTube, and Reddit, but excluding messaging service WhatsApp.

Since then, over a [dozen](https://techcrunch.com/2026/06/15/social-media-ban-children-countries-list/) countries have weighed up plans to roll out similar legislation, including Canada, France, Greece, Indonesia, Poland, Spain, Türkiye, the UAE, and the UK, with the momentum to curb children’s access to digital platforms becoming an increasing reality.

Virality has become a core component of artists breaking through in today’s competitive music marketplace. In 2024, 84% of the songs that entered *Billboard* ‘s Global 200 charts went viral on TikTok first, according to a report from [TikTok and Luminate](https://newsroom.tiktok.com/tiktok-and-luminate-release-latest-music-impact-report?lang=en), underlining the power of social media for the music industry today, especially for those of a younger demographic.

“Social media absolutely drives music discovery right now,” The Team agent Suzie Melki tells *IQ*. “These social media platforms definitely sit at the heart of most music campaigns these days. It can make or break an artist’s career.”

Questions have sprung up about the long-term consequences for the industry if the rising cohort of musicians and fans is disconnected from digital forums.

“At the moment, I would say that 99% of our marketing budgets are spent across social media platforms,” says UK-based agent Melki. “All of that is going to have to change again, depending on the artist. I don’t think it’s going to be such an issue with more established artists, as their audiences are used to more traditional methods like billboards and radio. But with the younger artists, we’re going to have to shift our attitude completely.”

> “Social platforms are still a major driver of awareness and momentum for tours”

In Australia, the live music business has already started to feel the effects of the ban that went into place less than a year ago, says Frontier Touring’s Reegan Stark.

“From a day-to-day promoter perspective, we haven’t seen a dramatic immediate shift – largely because under-16s aren’t the primary ticket buyers,” the promoter’s chief marketing & communications officer tells *IQ.* “The bulk of transactions still sit with older audiences or parents. Where we’ve felt it is more subtly, in engagement patterns.

“Social platforms are still a major driver of awareness and momentum for tours – particularly during announce and on-sale windows – and we’re seeing slightly less direct interaction from younger audiences. That hasn’t changed outcomes in the short term, but it has reinforced the importance of not relying on a single channel,” she says.

A question now is how to reach the next generation of ticket buyers if their access point has been restricted. During the pandemic, similar questions arose, though the touring industry’s continued resurgence signals the interest in live events won’t dissipate if digital access is curtailed.

“Since COVID, we’ve seen younger audiences putting more value in shared experiences than material goods,” Stark says. “The risk is more around discovery and connection. Social media has been a key entry point for younger audiences – it’s where they discover artists, build identity around music, and feel part of a community. If that access is limited, it could slow how quickly that connection forms. But it doesn’t remove the desire to attend live shows – it just means that discovery may happen later, or through different channels, often mediated by parents or broader cultural touchpoints.”

> “Her whole career would be totally different if she wasn’t able to post anything”

A loss of social media communities could lead to the resurgence of methodologies of days gone by – with potential avenues including artist websites, TV shows like the UK’s *Top of the Pops* or competition series, school-based advertising, radio activations, and physical activities, or more concentrated fan groups on still-accessible WhatsApp services.

Talent from television programmes and competition series is not a new phenomenon, and in some markets across [Asia](https://www.iqmagazine.com/2026/06/uob-live-success-underpins-aegs-thailand-expansion-plans/), it is still a primary driver for rapidly building a hyperinvolved fan community.

In the case of Courtney Hadwin, who went viral at 13 in 2018 for her soulful rendition of Otis Redding’s *Hard to Handle* for her *America’s Got Talent* audition, her career may have never come to fruition without the aid of online fandom, says her manager, Oliver Moheda of Total Artist Management.

“Her whole career would be totally different if she wasn’t able to post anything,” he tells *IQ*. “The biggest time of her career was when she was 13 years old, so maybe she would never have been an artist if social media were banned today.”

Some more established artists, like Phoebe Bridgers, Jack White, and Eddie Vedder, have already restricted access to phones at their shows, demonstrating a shifting attitude toward recording live experiences for artists of a certain calibre. But without the aid of online connections, breaking through will be “way harder than it was in the past,” he says, adding that acts are returning to touring as their main method of building up fanbases around the world.

“Artists are already going back to that – touring is now the way to discover it,” he says.

> “It reinforces the importance of long-term artist development rather than relying on short-term viral traction”

Without the rapid, global interconnectivity that digital platforms provide, the timeline from artists’ emergence to prominence may slow, meaning fewer artists will break through the pack, potentially leading to a more concentrated pack of acts rising.

“Ultimately, I think that the only impact this is going to have is on those emerging and developing artists who completely 100% rely on algorithm-driven discovery – but I don’t necessarily think it’s a terrible thing if you suddenly don’t have 100 different artists suddenly blowing up overnight on TikTok,” Melki says.

“You get a lot of artists these days who do blow up very quickly overnight on TikTok, and the labels swarm around them, but then you may not hear of the artist ever again. It’s a crying shame,” she adds.

It’s difficult to gauge the long-term implications of a widespread young person social media ban on the live music industry. But if artists and their teams have to pivot from reliance on viral quick-hits to a multi-segmented, tangible approach, the next generation of artists could enjoy a longevity and staying power that seems almost fleeting in today’s market.

“From a promoter perspective, it reinforces the importance of long-term artist development rather than relying on short-term viral traction,” Stark says. “It reiterates our long-term position that the most effective campaigns will deploy a multi-channel approach. Social media will remain important, but we’ll continue to place a lot of emphasis around our artist databases, media partnerships and out-of-home marketing.”

> “There will always be new artists; it might just take a little longer to discover them”

Plus, the emphasis on offline experiences – concerts, shows, sports, touring entertainment, and everything in between – is only likely to benefit the live industry as a whole, despite a shifting set of challenges.

“There’s an argument, isn’t there, that this could make what we do more appealing?” Melki says. “Because they \[kids\] are having less screen-based entertainment, the use of live concerts as a social event for kids could be of more importance.

“At the end of the day, what we’re all wanting to do is try to get people to all come into one space and meet up in real life and have a real experience rather than something on social media,” she says.

It could result in a new set of stakeholders in the live industry, from local organisations looking to bring children together, schools bringing in artists to perform at events, or fandoms creating in-person meet-ups to bridge the gap between the online community and in-person connection.

“Although it’s not ideal, because obviously it forms such a core of what we do as an industry now, it’s happening,” Melki says. “We just have to figure it out and move forward. We’re still here. We still have artists. There will always be new artists; it might just take a little longer to discover them.”
