# The Great Adventure: DJ Bobo on the changing dynamics of DACH touring

## Summary

Swiss Eurodance artist DJ Bobo discusses how English-language touring acts in the DACH region (Germany, Austria, Switzerland) increasingly need Broadway/Cirque-style production values to compete with international headliners, as he tours his "The Great Adventure" arena show into 2027.

### Key Points
- "The Great Adventure" tour features 33 artists and acrobats, high-wire choreography, and video-mapped staging transported across 13 equipment trucks, running into 2027.
- DJ Bobo says English-language DACH acts must match the spectacle of international (US/UK) tours since they lack access to the largest home markets (US, UK, France, Spain), leaning instead on Germany, Switzerland, Austria, Eastern Europe, and South America.
- Switzerland's German-speaking audience is roughly 5m of the country's ~9m population, capping him at two or three shows there per tour cycle.
- Promotion has shifted entirely to social media, dropping posters and TV appearances in favor of geographic and age-based targeting.
- Touring strategy increasingly favors extended multi-night stops in major DACH cities (Berlin, Hamburg, Munich) over one-off stops in medium-sized cities, which are harder to promote profitably.
- Non-DACH single-market stops (Slovakia, Hungary, Czechia, Poland) use leaner two-truck productions with local lights/sound/staging, routed consecutively to minimize costs.

## Transcript

The stalwart touring artist tells IQ about the evolving strategies of English language tours in the German-speaking world

![](https://www.iqmagazine.com/wp-content/smush-webp/2026/07/JulianHukePhotography-A1_02709-2-scaled.jpg.webp)

The Great Adventure © Julian Huke

With a touring career of over 30 years, DJ Bobo finds himself in a specific position known well to domestic English language acts touring the DACH region.

Now, the veteran Swiss DJ and producer says, German-speaking markets are increasingly demanding spectacle and staging from domestic artists, as they increasingly compete with international headline acts rather than German-speaking domestic artists.

The Eurodance stalwart is underway on his *The Great Adventure* arena tour, which has among its selling points what promoters call a “highly complex technical specification and scenery that takes the public along on an adventure”, alongside high-wire choreography and costume elements which aim the show at “the whole family”.

As he embarks on the tour that will take him into 2027, the artist tells IQ that the laundry list of staging spectacles, which includes 33 artists and acrobats, is key to the success in the niche of English language performance in the DACH region.

“I think it’s very important to give the best possible show for the best possible price for the audience. Since we play every two to three years a tour in the same market, we have to compare with international tours, worldwide tours, since we don’t have the American market, the British market, the French market, or the Spanish,” he explains.

“But we have Eastern Europe, South America, and mostly Germany, Switzerland, and Austria. So we have to deliver, comparing to international artists,” the artist adds.

> “Medium venues are more difficult to push. It’s easier to push Berlin, Hamburg, Munich”

He says this unique position for his touring business is partly a product of the largely English-language 1990s Eurodance craze, which has cemented a business model in German-speaking touring markets that is diverging from domestic German language acts.

“If you’re a German‑speaking artist who sings in German, all these things don’t count for you. You don’t have to bring a big show. You have to be yourself because they’re focused on the lyrics. In our case, since we come from the 90s, from Eurodance, which was a wave, the wave disappeared and we’re still here touring with big shows. So we have to compare with the Americans and the British, and that makes it more difficult,” DJ Bobo tells *IQ.*

With its ambitious staging, *The Great Adventure* tour must maximise efficiency in its operations, with DJ Bobo explaining: “We have 13 trucks of equipment, which is not a lot, but we make everything with video mapping. This allows us to make it look very big, to change it from venue to venue — bigger or smaller depending on the venue — and it’s always spectacular.”

But this model of touring is also backed by a pricing strategy that reflects these dynamics across the competitive landscape of DACH touring.

“We are also limited in ticket prices. People choose us because maybe we’re closer to Cirque du Soleil than we are to Bryan Adams or Bruce Springsteen. It’s more of an event,” the DJ explains. “People always say: ‘you have to go and see this because it’s a spectacular show’. It’s more than ‘you have to go and see DJ BoBo’; it’s ‘watch the show, it’s spectacular’, together with the hits”.

The promotional strategies that are emerging as part of this model also reflect that the three German speaking markets in Europe must not all be treated the same. Despite finding a reliable Swiss audience in his home country and an English language setlist, the country’s cultural and linguistic diversity ironically largely limit Bobo’s audience to Switzerland’s German-native fans.

“We don’t have nine million people \[Switzerland’s full population\]; we have five million German‑speaking people. We can play two shows in Switzerland or three max, but then that’s it because of the size of the country,” DJ Bobo says.

Promoting the tour in a multilingual country also raises questions for how and where to advertise, the artist explains. “It is very focused,” he says of his Swiss promotional approach. “We stepped away from traditional promotion a few years ago — no posters, no TV shows at local stations. We go only on social media and target the audience geographically as well as possible, and also age‑wise.

“TV is definitely not our main target audience; the young don’t watch TV anymore. So we changed our concept to social media mostly. It’s cheaper and better.”

He explains, however, that routing in Austria is “easier” because it is “mostly focused on Vienna”.

> “we have to compare with international tours, worldwide tours, since we don’t have the American market, the British market”

Vienna also opens up the option of Bratislava, typically one of the more profitable stops on a central European route due to lower production costs compared to nearby German-speaking markets. But this itself bring its own challenges, given single show stops are typically the most viable in non-DACH countries, most commonly Slovakia, Hungary, Czechia, and Poland.

“It changes dramatically,” DJ Bobo says of the strategy around these shorter stops. “When we get the trucks and everything together, the production, we have to play everything in a row to minimise and optimise costs as soon as the trucks are unloaded.

“They’re mostly one‑off shows where we go with a two‑truck production, and we have local lights, local sound, and local stages built. But we bring the decorations, the inflatables, the special effects, the costumes — everything. So the production changes when we play single shows,” he continues.

As for the big prize, the larger German cities, cultivating a family audience is central to the touring strategy, and the same pressures felt by tours across the world are also incentivising extended city stops where possible.

“Medium venues are more difficult to push. It’s easier to push Berlin, Hamburg, Munich — big cities with good arenas. Medium cities are more difficult to promote. Day‑by‑day concepts are more difficult than in the past,” DJ Bobo says.

He adds that the emerging strategy therefore “focuses more and more on the bigger cities and bigger venues. Long‑term, we have to stay in the big arenas in the big cities for at least two shows”, explaining: “That helps us optimise costs and promotion.”
