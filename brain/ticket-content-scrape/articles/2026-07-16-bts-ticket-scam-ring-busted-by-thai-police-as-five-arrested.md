# BTS ticket scam ring busted by Thai police as five arrested

## Summary

Thai police arrested five suspects, aged 18-27, in Bangkok and Surat Thani Province for running a counterfeit-ticket scam ring tied to BTS's Arirang World Tour, accused of defrauding over 100 fans of more than 1.8 million baht (€47,000). Victims bought fake tickets through social-media "booking service" agents for the tour's December stop at Bangkok's Rajamangala National Stadium; the sellers deleted their accounts once genuine tickets went on sale after collecting payment. The arrests followed complaints to Thailand's Consumer Protection Police Division, and the BBC separately reported Southeast Asian fans losing over $100,000 to Arirang Tour scams amid demand exceeding supply by up to 15x.

### Key Points
- Five suspects (ages 18-27) arrested in Bangkok and Surat Thani Province following warrants issued the week of July 6.
- Over 100 victims filed complaints with Thailand's Consumer Protection Police Division (CPPD); average individual loss was 17,700 baht (€460).
- Total reported damages exceed 1.8 million baht (€47,000), per Khaosod English reporting.
- Scammers offered paid "ticket-booking services" via social media, then deleted their accounts once real tickets went on sale.
- Charges include public fraud, false computer data entry, and unauthorized use of bank/payment accounts.
- Arirang World Tour spans 80+ dates across 34 regions; BTS have already shifted 2.4 million tickets on the tour per earlier IQ reporting.

### Highlights
- 100+ fans lost an average of €460 each to fraudulent "ticket-booking" agents operating through social media for a high-demand tour.
- Regional demand outstripped official ticket supply by up to 15x, per BBC reporting, driving fans to unverified sellers.
- Scam operators collected payment then vanished once genuine tickets went on sale — a pattern enabled by the absence of verified official channels for oversubscribed fans.

### Industry Problem
Severe demand-supply imbalance on high-profile onsales pushes patrons toward unofficial, unverifiable ticket sources, and current recourse is purely reactive — law enforcement action after fans have already lost money — rather than a platform-level safeguard.

### Proposed Solution
The item describes only post-hoc criminal enforcement; an independent primary platform like Etix could counter this pattern with verified-buyer waitlists/lotteries for oversubscribed onsales, in-app authentication of ticket status, and consumer messaging that steers fans away from unofficial social-media "booking services" before money changes hands.

Thai police have arrested five people allegedly involved in a BTS concert ticket scam ring, accused of defrauding over 100 fans of over 1.8 million baht (€47,000) in fake sales.

All five suspects, aged from 18 to 27, were arrested in Bangkok and the Surat Thani Province after authorities issued a series of warrants late last week (10 July).

The pursuit began after dozens of fans fell victim to counterfeit tickets listed on social media accounts for the K-pop powerhouse band’s *Arirang World Tour*, coming to Bangkok’s Rajamangala National Stadium for three nights in December.

With strong demand for tickets, many turned to third-party agents, many sourced through social media pages, to secure tickets. The alleged perpetrators offered ticket-booking services for an additional fee, and later deleted their accounts when tickets went on sale after receiving payment from victims.

As a result, over 100 victims filed complaints with Thailand’s Consumer Protection Police Division (CPPD), with each losing an average of 17,700 baht (€460). Total damages exceed 1.8 million baht (€47,000), according to reports from the *[Khaosod English](https://www.khaosodenglish.com/featured/2026/06/11/thai-parliament-hears-bts-ticket-scam-complaints-damages-exceed-1-23-million-baht/).*

The investigation led authorities to the scam ring, resulting in the arrest of the five suspects.

Those arrested are facing charges including public fraud, jointly entering false information into a computer system in a manner likely to cause damage to the public, and opening or allowing others to use their bank accounts or electronic payment cards for criminal activities. They are awaiting further legal proceedings.

BTS’s comeback *Arirang Tour* spans over 80 dates across 34 regions, and is one of the most [in-demand tours](https://www.iqmagazine.com/2026/01/bts-shift-2-4-million-tickets-add-extra-dates-to-world-tour/) out this year, resulting in a rush for fans to snag tickets.

In Southeast Asia, which accounts for less than 20% of the tour dates, *[BBC](https://www.bbc.co.uk/news/articles/cdr45nrr2jyo)* reports that fans have lost over $100,000 (€87,200) in ticket scams, with demand for tickets exceeding availability by 15x across the continent.

Initially launching in South Korea and Japan, the tour will run through North America, the UK/Europe, and South America before returning to Asia in November.

The leg kicks off with three nights at Taiwan’s Kaohsiung National Stadium, followed by multi-night runs in Bangkok, Kuala Lumpur, Singapore, and Jakarta. Next year, they will pick up in Australia with shows in Melbourne and Sydney, before hitting Hong Kong and Bulacan to finish the *Arirang World Tour* next March.
