# Madison Square Garden Sues WIRED Over Celebrity ‘Risk’ Database Report

## Summary

Madison Square Garden has sued WIRED, its parent Advance Publications, and named journalists over a report claiming MSG's internal "talent" database — 39,539 entries covering celebrities, politicians, and other prominent guests — was used to track and discriminate against LGBTQIA individuals. MSG says the database is a legitimate CRM/security tool and that WIRED built a false narrative from data stolen in a breach by hacking group ShinyHunters; WIRED calls the suit "baseless and ridiculous" and stands by its reporting.

### Key Points
- Suit filed in New York County Supreme Court against WIRED, Advance Publications, and reporters Shachtman, Varner, and editorial director Drummond.
- Claims: defamation by implication, tortious interference with contractual/confidentiality obligations.
- WIRED's underlying article was based on MSG data leaked by hacking group ShinyHunters.
- MSG seeks correction/retraction plus compensatory, presumed, special, and punitive damages.
- Database reportedly held ~39,539 entries; roughly 400 were flagged in the disputed "risk" categorization WIRED highlighted.

***MSG disputes allegations that its collection of LGBTQIA information was discriminatory, but the lawsuit adds new detail about the extensive personal data retained across its entertainment and sports operations.***

Madison Square Garden has sued WIRED and several of its journalists over a report detailing an internal database that categorized celebrities, politicians and other prominent guests by characteristics including sexual orientation, race and perceived risk to the organization.

The lawsuit, filed Thursday in New York County Supreme Court, accuses WIRED of using information stolen during a recent breach of MSG’s systems to manufacture the implication that the company was tracking LGBTQIA people for discriminatory or exclusionary purposes. WIRED has called the lawsuit “baseless and ridiculous” and said it stands behind its reporting.

MSG Entertainment Corp., MSG Entertainment Holdings LLC and MSG Sports Corp. brought the case against WIRED publisher Advance Magazine Publishers, parent company Advance Publications, reporters Noah Shachtman and Maddy Varner, and WIRED Global Editorial Director Katie Drummond.

The complaint asserts claims for defamation by implication and tortious interference with contractual and confidentiality obligations. MSG is seeking a correction or retraction along with compensatory, presumed, special and punitive damages.

At the center of the dispute is a [July 9 WIRED article headlined “Madison Square Garden Kept a List of Gay Celebrities.”](https://www.wired.com/story/madison-square-garden-celebrity-database-surveillance/) The report was based in part on MSG data published by the ShinyHunters hacking group following an apparent intrusion into the company’s systems.

WIRED reported that MSG’s internal “talent” database contained 39,539 entries covering celebrities, athletes, politicians, business figures and other prominent people. About 400 were assigned classifications ranging from “flag” and “low risk” to “medium risk” and “high risk.” Other entries reportedly carried labels including “DO NOT HOST,” references to social-media concerns or notes that an individual had been banned from MSG.

The same database included 93 entries marked “LGBTQIA,” according to WIRED. The outlet questioned why MSG was recording that information and placed it within the broader context of its previous reporting on the company’s facial-recognition systems, social-media monitoring and treatment of a transgender attendee.

MSG does not deny that its systems contained the LGBTQIA designations, risk classifications or approximately 40,000 talent records described by WIRED. Instead, its lawsuit argues that the publication falsely connected separate fields within a large customer relationship management system to imply that sexual orientation influenced risk scoring, security treatment or admission decisions.

According to MSG, the sexual-orientation field was added to its Salesforce system in 2022 and was used for Pride-related invitations, charitable outreach, hospitality, sponsorship and marketing. The company says only five VIP Services employees could edit the field, nine Fan Engagement employees could view it and most security personnel had no access to it.

MSG also says that risk notes and physical-security decisions were maintained through separate systems and personnel. Its complaint characterizes the disputed database as a conventional relationship-management platform containing more than 10 million customer accounts and approximately 40,000 talent records, with fields ranging from social-media handles and nonprofit affiliations to dietary restrictions and favorite teams.

The company argues that WIRED created what it called a list of gay celebrities by filtering the larger data export for the 93 records carrying the LGBTQIA designation. It contends that the article’s headline and the placement of “LGBTQIA,” “DO NOT HOST” and risk terminology together led readers to conclude that the classifications were connected even though they appeared in different database fields.

WIRED’s reporting did not state that every LGBTQIA entry had received a risk score. It did, however, question the purpose of collecting sexual orientation and published comments from a digital-rights advocate who said MSG appeared “overly interested in queer and trans people.” The story also reported that a source familiar with the system linked at least some risk scores and hosting decisions to social-media criticism of MSG or Executive Chairman James Dolan.

“Earlier today, WIRED learned that Madison Square Garden was suing us for our accurate reporting,” the publication said after the lawsuit was filed. “We stand by this reporting, and plan to vigorously defend it against this baseless and ridiculous lawsuit.”

WIRED added that it intends to continue investigating Dolan’s use of technology throughout his entertainment businesses.

The lawsuit also goes beyond contesting the meaning of the published article. MSG’s tortious-interference claim alleges that WIRED knowingly encouraged at least one employee or internal source to provide confidential information in violation of company policies and contractual obligations.

The complaint says MSG plans to seek discovery involving source communications, article drafts, headline deliberations and internal editorial discussions. It specifically anticipates seeking such material should the defendants invoke New York’s anti-SLAPP protections, which are intended to facilitate early dismissal of lawsuits targeting speech about matters of public interest.

That portion of the case turns MSG’s action into a challenge not only to WIRED’s conclusions, but also to the reporting process used to investigate one of the country’s most powerful venue operators.

## WIRED Dispute Follows Reported MSG Data Exposure

The defamation case follows earlier litigation over the apparent data breach that produced information examined in WIRED’s reporting, although that lawsuit is no longer pending.

A proposed class action filed by concert attendee Carlos Avalos had alleged that the ShinyHunters hacking group released more than 42 gigabytes of compressed data and over 26 million records after MSG declined to pay a ransom. The complaint claimed the exposed material may have included customer contact information, threat assessments, celebrity profiles and records associated with MSG’s facial-recognition operations.

The lawsuit was voluntarily dismissed on July 1, before the allegations were adjudicated. MSG disputed the claims underlying the case.

“These lawsuits were based on unsupported speculation and false assumptions and therefore were voluntarily dropped,” an MSG spokesperson said in a statement provided to TicketNews.

WIRED separately reported that one Salesforce-derived database contained more than 10.5 million entries, including nearly 9.8 million unique email addresses, more than 2.8 million unique phone numbers and thousands of birth dates. The different figures appear to describe distinct measures or portions of the material allegedly taken from MSG rather than necessarily conflicting estimates of affected individuals.

The reported data exposure places the current dispute in a context broader than whether WIRED fairly characterized one particular database field. Even under MSG’s description of the system, its companies retained extensive information about millions of customers and tens of thousands of public figures, including sexual orientation, addresses, social-media accounts, political relationships, community affiliations and internal assessments.

That creates potential harm independent of whether those records were originally collected for inclusion, security, hospitality or marketing.

Sensitive information gathered for one business function can be accessed for another, interpreted differently by future employees or exposed to criminals in a breach. Sexual orientation, political activity and internal risk classifications can be especially damaging when combined with names, contact information, biometric identifiers or detailed attendance histories.

MSG’s previous use of facial recognition also makes concerns about function creep — information collected for one stated purpose later being deployed for another — particularly relevant.

The company used facial-recognition technology to identify and exclude attorneys employed by firms involved in litigation against MSG, including patrons holding valid tickets to concerts and sporting events. One attorney accompanying her daughter’s Girl Scout troop to the Radio City Christmas Spectacular was removed after the system identified her connection to a firm involved in litigation against the company.

MSG defended that policy as the lawful decision of a private business, while lawmakers, privacy advocates and New York Attorney General Letitia James questioned whether the practice violated civil-rights, consumer-access or ticketing laws. Critics pointed to the episode as a tangible example of biometric security infrastructure being used against corporate adversaries rather than individuals presenting a physical danger.

The outcome of MSG’s case against WIRED will depend on whether the publication’s words and presentation conveyed a demonstrably false implication with the level of fault required under New York defamation law.

For consumers and the live-event industry, however, the filings have already established something more fundamental: major venue operators can maintain extraordinarily detailed information about the people attending, performing at and interacting with their properties.

MSG insists the LGBTQIA information at issue was gathered to facilitate inclusion. WIRED argues that the leaked records and internal sources support a broader story about surveillance, reputational screening and control under Dolan.

The breach means consumers no longer need to consider only what an event operator intends to do with their information. They must also consider what may happen when that information is retained for years, combined with other records and eventually removed from the operator’s control.
