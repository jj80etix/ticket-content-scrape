# Gen Z’s price sensitivity declining despite concert costs rising

## Summary

A new Luminate Intelligence "US Music 360" survey finds Gen Z has overtaken millennials as the highest-spending live-music demographic ($101/month vs. $94), and Gen Z's price sensitivity is easing even as ticket prices rise. Fewer respondents overall cite cost as a barrier to attendance (53%, down from 59% two years ago), with Gen Z's cost-barrier response falling sharply from 75% to 57% and travel-cost barrier from 61% to 31%. Analysts tie this to a broader shift toward larger, destination-style tours (e.g., Bad Bunny's Puerto Rico residency, Harry Styles' stadium tour).

### Key Points
- Gen Z now outspends millennials on live music: $101 vs. $94/month average (Q1 2026).
- Share citing ticket price as a barrier: down to 53% overall; Gen Z's dropped from 75% to 57%.
- Gen Z travel-cost barrier fell from 61% (2024) to 31% (2026), reflecting rising willingness to travel for shows.
- Gen Z attending 2+ concerts/year rose from 29% (2024) to 37% (2026).
- Millennials/Gen X/boomers saw ticket-cost-barrier concerns rise in the past six months — a possible ceiling on price tolerance for older cohorts.

### Highlights
- Gen Z average monthly live-music spend: $101 vs. millennials' $94 (Q1 2026), a generational spending-power shift.
- Cost-as-barrier for Gen Z fell 18 points (75%→57%) in two years even as prices rose — evidence that perceived value, not price alone, drives attendance decisions.
- Non-Gen Z cohorts show rising price sensitivity over just the past six months — a divergence worth monitoring.

### Industry Problem
Ticket price remains the single biggest attendance barrier across all age groups, but it is a "value" problem more than a pure "price" problem for younger patrons — pricing/communication strategies tuned only to sticker price risk missing what actually drives Gen Z conversion (perceived experience value, destination appeal).

### Proposed Solution
The article proposes no product fix itself, but the data argues for demand-side segmentation: primary platforms could build presale/marketing tooling and dynamic messaging that foregrounds "experience value" (bundles, travel packages, destination framing) for price-tolerant younger segments while preserving affordability signals (payment plans, tiered pricing) for more price-sensitive older cohorts.

## Gen Z’s price sensitivity declining despite concert costs rising

A new report from Luminate Intelligence reveals the growing value for live experiences for younger audiences, as Gen Z outspend millennials

![Crowd](https://www.iqmagazine.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/07/Crowd-©-Movistar-Arena.png)

While high ticket prices remain the biggest barrier to attending live music events, a new Luminate survey suggests Gen Z – the highest-spending demographic for live music – is becoming less price-sensitive, highlighting the growing value younger audiences are placing on live experiences.

The findings come as part of Luminate Intelligence’s annual consumer insight study, the [US Music 360 survey](https://luminatedata.com/reports/live-music-2026), which analyses music spending habits and industry trends among approximately 4,000 US residents.

According to the survey, Gen Z (born between 1997 and 2012) has overtaken millennials (1981-1996) as the biggest monthly spenders on live music. In Q1 2026, Gen Z spent an average of $101 (€88), compared to $94 (€82) for millennials, who are historically the highest-spending demographic for live music.

“ [Blue dot fever](https://www.iqmagazine.com/2026/05/live-nation-dont-get-the-blues-over-blue-dot-fever/) certainly shouldn’t be thought of as a terminal prognosis for the industry,” says Robert Steiner, Luminate Intelligence media analyst. “Overall, the live music business — both in terms of money in the box office and butts in seats — has seen several consecutive record-breaking years so far this decade.

“But the phenomenon is nonetheless a sign of the times: Post-pandemic demand for live music remains sky high, and artists and organisers have responded in kind. At the same time, operation costs have also ballooned, which translates to higher ticket prices,” he says.

Although ticket prices remain the leading deterrent to attendance, the number of consumers citing cost as a barrier is declining. In Q1 2026, 53% of respondents said ticket prices discouraged them, down from 59% two years earlier.

The shift was even more pronounced among the younger generations. The Gen Z share citing ticket prices as a barrier fell from 75% in Q1 2026 to 57% in Q1 2026, while the millennial group’s results declined from 66% to 56% in the same period.

> The generation’s willingness to spend more and travel further reinforces the industry’s shift toward larger, destination-style live offerings

At the same time, the perceived value of concerts and festivals increased across all age groups, indicating that audiences are increasingly willing to pay a premium for experiences they deem worthwhile.

Across the generational board, the desire to attend live events is growing, driven primarily by Gen Z respondents. Earlier this year, another survey from Live Nation found that [music fandom is central](https://www.iqmagazine.com/2026/04/music-fandom-is-central-to-gen-zs-identity/) to the digital generation’s identity, with live experiences and tangible touchstones of great importance.

Within Gen Z’s survey pool, those attending two concerts per year increased from 29% in Q1 2024 to 37% in Q1 2026, while the share of attending just one concert annually declined 10% in the same period. The results indicate that younger fans are prioritising more live experiences, despite the rising cost to attend.

Additionally, Gen Z is also showing an increasing willingness to travel for live events, reflecting the continued rise of music tourism.

The percentage of Gen Z attendees who identified travel cost as a barrier to live shows dropped from 61% in Q1 2024 to just 31% two years later, highlighting a growing acceptance of travelling to see an artist in a different market.

The generation’s willingness to spend more and travel further reinforces the industry’s shift toward larger, destination-style live offerings, like that of [Harry Styles](https://www.iqmagazine.com/2026/02/global-stadium-boom-accelerates-shift-to-residency-style-tours/) ‘  *Together, Together* tour or [Bad Bunny](https://www.iqmagazine.com/2025/09/the-inside-story-of-bad-bunnys-puerto-rico-residency/) ‘s Puerto Rico residency.

However, the findings also suggest there may be limits to consumers’ willingness to spend. Among millennials, Gen X, and baby boomers, the share of respondents citing ticket costs as a barrier rose in the past six months, signalling that price sensitivity may rise if ticket prices continue to climb.
