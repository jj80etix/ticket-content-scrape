# North Carolina Pushes Ticket Resale Regulations Through in Budget Bill Sprint

## Summary

North Carolina enacted new ticket resale regulations by folding them into its 2026 budget bill (SB 257) rather than passing them through the standard standalone-bill process, after a companion bill (SB 849) stalled without a public hearing. Effective October 1, the law requires all-in pricing, resale-marketplace disclosures, and anti-bot/anti-speculative-ticket provisions, backed by civil penalties up to $15,000/day. Live Nation publicly supported the bill, and critics argue it entrenches Ticketmaster's position by omitting transferability and anti-retaliation protections that a separate, stalled 2025 bill had included.

### Key Points
- NC's SB 257 (2026 Appropriations Act) creates new ticket resale/anti-bot rules, effective Oct. 1, 2026.
- Requires all-in pricing disclosure, resale-marketplace transparency (link to original box office), and bans "speculative" ticket listings.
- New anti-bot provisions targeting purchase-cap evasion, multi-account/IP abuse, and queue bypass.
- Penalties up to $15,000/day plus per-ticket damages; willful violations add $10,000/ticket.
- Live Nation publicly backed the bill as "common-sense" reform even as it fights antitrust litigation.
- Critics, including a former state senator, say the law increases Ticketmaster's leverage over resale since it omits transferability/anti-retaliation protections from an earlier stalled bill (HB 598).
- Bill bypassed normal committee review by riding through the must-pass budget conference process.

## The new law targets “speculative” tickets, resale website transparency and bots, but it reached the governor through the state budget after a standalone bill stalled without a public hearing

North Carolina has enacted a new ticket resale regulations that will impose fresh restrictions on resale platforms, brokers, ticketing websites, speculative listings, presale inventory and bot activity beginning Oct. 1.

Gov. Josh Stein [signed the state’s 2026 Appropriations Act](https://www.carolinajournal.com/stein-signs-34b-nc-budget-ahead-of-deadline/), SB 257, on Wednesday afternoon. Tucked inside the more than 600-page budget is Section 5.8, titled “Consumer Protections for Entertainment Event Ticket Sales and Resales,” which creates a new Article 9 under Chapter 75 of the North Carolina General Statutes.

Live Nation Entertainment – which has sought to rewrite state-specific ticket laws across the country while simultaneously fighting antitrust legal peril in court – [issued a statement in support of the bill](https://newsroom.livenation.com/statements/north-carolina-legislature-passes-common-sense-ticketing-reforms/) as it neared passage last week, framing the measures as “common-sense” reforms”

The law introduces a new disclosure-and-enforcement model for ticket resale in the state, requiring all-in pricing, resale-website disclosures, links to primary sellers, and stronger restrictions on practices lawmakers and venue advocates described as deceptive or harmful to fans.

The ticketing language closely tracks Senate Bill 849, the “Real Tickets, Real Fans Act,” filed in late April by Sens. Timothy Moffitt, Vickie Sawyer and Jay Chaudhuri with vocal support from the industry lobby. Promoted publicly by the promoter/venue lobby, the bill was marketed as a way to target fake tickets, deceptive resale sites, speculative ticket listings and bots.

SB 849 itself never advanced as a standalone bill. After it was filed on April 27, it was quickly diverted to the Senate Appropriations/Base Budget, committee, but saw no action taken on the standalone bill. Instead, the ticketing provisions resurfaced two months later inside the budget conference report, just days before it was passed. The conference committee substitute was reported June 30, passed the House and Senate on July 1 and July 2, was ratified July 2 and was signed by Stein on July 7.

That means a policy affecting ticket sellers, resale marketplaces, brokers, venues, artists, teams and consumers became law not through the ordinary path of a standalone consumer-protection bill, but through a must-pass budget package negotiated in conference and approved in the closing stretch of the session.

Supporters had been pressing the issue publicly. At a May press event at The Pour House Music Hall in Raleigh, musicians and venue owners described fans arriving with invalid tickets and consumers being misled by sites that appeared to be official box offices. Heather LaGarde, owner of the Haw River Ballroom, said bot activity and invalid resale tickets can leave fans outside, seats empty inside and venues and performers losing out on bar and merchandise sales.

The enacted law now sets several new requirements.

Ticket issuers, resellers and secondary ticket exchanges must clearly and conspicuously disclose the total price of a ticket whenever the price is displayed. That total must include all mandatory fees and the maximum order-processing fee, if any. Once the total price is shown at the start of a ticketing session, it cannot be increased during that session except for limited charges such as non-electronic delivery costs, government taxes or fees and a reasonable processing fee disclosed before purchase.

Resale marketplaces must disclose if ticket is being resold, and if a marketplace is not also the initial seller, it must must disclose that it is not the original seller and provide a link to that box office vendor – often Ticketmaster.

The law also puts notable new restrictions on when and how tickets can be listed for resale. It bans the sale of so-called “speculative” tickets – tickets not under actual possession of the person listing it for resale. It also regulates the listing for resale of tickets acquired through “fan club” or other presale allocations.

New anti-bot regulations prohibit using or creating bots to buy tickets, exceed ticket limits, use multiple IP addresses or accounts to get around purchase caps, bypass queues or presale codes, or defeat security and access-control systems.

Violations can trigger impose civil penalties of up to $15,000 per day, plus the greater of $1,000 or five times the total ticket price per ticket listed, advertised, sold or resold in violation. Willful violations may carry an additional $10,000-per-ticket penalty. The Attorney General must also provide website and telephone reporting channels for consumers and report annually on enforcement actions.

The industry lobby argues such provisions make it easier to distinguish official sellers from resale sites, reduce fake or speculative listings and give regulators stronger tools to pursue deceptive ticketing practices. Opponents point out that such significant new regulation imposed only on resale marketplaces risk handing even more power to the entrenched industry powers like Live Nation/Ticketmaster.

In an [Op-Ed published in May looking at the potential impact of SB 849](https://www.newsobserver.com/opinion/article315936371.html), former North Carolina State Senator Thomas Cowart Goolsby was critical of the potential unintended consequences, which include empowering venues and their preferred vendors to weaponize the language of the legislation to harm competition by dictating when and how consumer ticket resale can happen.

“The bill’s major shortcoming is that it doesn’t really clamp down on Ticketmaster,” he wrote in the News & Observer. “Instead, it gives the company more power to squeeze out some of the few remaining alternative ticket providers that consumers have to choose from.”

“This bill would allow venues and promoters to tell season ticket holders when they can sell their tickets,” he continued. “And here’s the kicker: Under the terms of the legislation, restrictions on resale before the public sale can be lifted if authorized by the venue, promoter or ticketing company – all three of which are more often than not Ticketmaster.”

Goolsby was quick to point out that North Carolina’s own Attorney General Jeff Jackson has been actively involved in the massive antitrust case against Ticketmaster and Live Nation, [calling proposed Department of Justice settlement “terrible”](https://portcitydaily.com/latest-news/2026/03/09/nc-attorney-general-jeff-jackson-denounces-terrible-doj-settlement-with-live-nation/) and keeping the state engaged in the case through to the landmark quilty verdict that it had operated as an illegal monopoly.

The bill’s path is also notable because North Carolina lawmakers had already considered a different ticketing framework. In 2025, House Bill 598, the “Live Event Ticketing Protections & Reforms” bill, [advanced through multiple House committees](https://www.ticketnews.com/2025/05/north-carolina-lawmakers-consider-ticket-rights-protection-bill/), in stark contrast to the language that made it in to the budget. That bill included consumer-disclosure provisions and restrictions on misleading venue-style URLs, but it also would have protected ticket transferability by preventing ticket issuers from restricting resale, setting minimum or maximum resale prices, discriminating against purchasers because a ticket was resold, or delaying delivery of electronic tickets beyond 72 hours after purchase.

Those transferability and anti-retaliation provisions – which Ticketmaster and other promoter/venue aligned lobbying groups do not support – were not included in the budget.

That contrast gives the new law a sharper political context. A bill that would have imposed restrictions on ticket issuers and protected resale rights received committee consideration but stalled. A later bill backed publicly by venues and arts advocates, and focused more squarely on regulating resale marketplaces and ticketing websites, did not move through the same public process as a standalone measure. Instead, it leapfrogged into law through the budget.

That route gave supporters the result they wanted. It also left affected businesses with little opportunity to scrutinize the final language, raise implementation concerns or seek amendments before the law was signed.

The new requirements take effect Oct. 1, 2026, and apply to tickets sold or resold on or after that date.
