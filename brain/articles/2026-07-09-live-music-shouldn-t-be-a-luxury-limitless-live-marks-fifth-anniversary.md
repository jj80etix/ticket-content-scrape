# ‘Live music shouldn’t be a luxury’: Limitless Live marks fifth anniversary

## Summary

London's Limitless Live, the UK's biggest free-entry concert series, marked its fifth anniversary at Camden Roundhouse; founder Tolu Farinto discussed plans to add a second annual show in 2027 and continue using brand partnerships (including Viagogo and DHL) to fund free access to live music while connecting underrepresented young people to employment opportunities.

### Key Points
- Limitless Live celebrates 5th anniversary; considering a second 2027 show to ease talent-booking constraints.
- Funded via brand partnerships (Viagogo, DHL among newest) rather than ticket sales; free-entry model.
- Combines free concerts with employability/internship pathways for underrepresented youth, including a past Live Nation internship partnership.

## ‘Live music shouldn’t be a luxury’: Limitless Live marks fifth anniversary

Limitless Live founder Tolu Farinto tells IQ about plans to continue scaling the largest free-entry music event in the UK

![](https://www.iqmagazine.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/07/Limitless-Live.webp)

© Limitless Live

London’s Limitless Live concert series has marked its fifth anniversary, as the UK’s biggest free music event tells *IQ* an expanded roster of corporate partners has seen the series “really build out” its music accessibility initiative.

As the series aims to expand the reach of its free entry concert series, organisers are considering adding a second show to its 2027 edition, as well as considering additions such as incorporating orchestral elements into some performances.

The series, which recently marked its fifth annual edition at the Camden Roundhouse, aims to broaden accessibility to live music and connect young people from underrepresented backgrounds with work experience and employment opportunities with the concerts’ brand partners, which has made strides this year with partnerships with Viagogo and DHL.

Limitless founder Tolu Farinto tells *IQ:* “Going into the future, we’re looking to build that out more, all with the purpose of giving young kids access to live music and being a sustainable platform they can look forward to. As music gets more expensive and the variables — some beyond our control — change, live music shouldn’t be a luxury.”

Of the founding mission of Limitless Live, he says: “I always saw that music was an interesting gateway to get young people excited about things. Of course, they enjoy live music, but what if we created a social‑purpose music platform that helped inspire them, connect them to opportunities that can transform their lives and employment opportunities?”

“Essentially it was the intersection between brands, entertainment, and culture change — how we use brands to fund a free live music experience, which then enables us to have a greater impact on young people through personal development, employability, access to experiences, and so on,” he continues.

The concerts typically fit into artists’ existing tours, but pitching the series to managers can have its challenges. Farinto explains: “The biggest headache for me is booking talent — it’s the biggest headache because it’s a standalone London show. All of them can do standalone London shows, so it becomes a conversation about money rather than impact.”

But he says the addition of a second concert to the series for 2027 and beyond could “make this conversation a lot easier” and “helps us increase the level of talent we can go after”.

Explaining how Limitless Live can fit into a touring schedule for in-demand artists, Farinto says: “For example, they may have been touring from October through into April, October through into February.

“They’ve gone around the world, finished their tour, their band’s not cold because they’ve been touring for four months, so they can just do an additional date on the end. Or they’re about to start a tour and we don’t fall foul of any exclusivities in relation to festival season because of the time of year we do it,” he says.

> “it’s about how we use brands to fund a free live music experience, which then enables us to have a greater impact on young people”

Farinto explains that through his work as a consultant for culture change at large corporates, he saw the usual route of graduate programmes and the same university pipelines sometimes failed to grab the attention of young people from a broader range of backgrounds.

He recalls thinking: “If I create Limitless, which is centred on live music and will get the attention of kids from all walks of life, but predominantly underrepresented backgrounds, and then as a consequence of getting that attention, I signpost them into internships, work experience, or anything like that, it means that there is a pathway for them.”

“Limitless Live was born in 2022. We launched at the Roundhouse, which has similar values to us around impacting young people,” Farinto recalls.

The 2022 event launched with Raye, Ms Banks, Unknown T, and Miss LaFamilia, which Farinto recalls was “huge for our first year”. “Having that lineup coming out of the gate was amazing,” he tells *IQ.*

That was “the proof of the concept”, Farinto says, adding that “how we got young people excited about a free music experience” led to a series deeper partnerships in the live music industry from the second year of the series.

“That was the first year of the 12-month internship we did with Live Nation. Kids were paid £25,000 a year and I went into Live Nation and trained all of their line managers on how to ‘manage for difference’. So you had the employability side of things, but you also had the live music moment, which was super exciting,” he says.

That took Limitless Live into 2023, where the concert featured Krept and Konan, K‑Trap, Pa Salieu, and Crystal Millz.

“We then grew from there and we had Stefflon Don as headliner in 2024. That was huge because Stefflon Don is an icon in her own right in relation to the dancehall, reggaeton, hip‑hop, rap space globally and here in the UK,” the founder says, adding: “The response to that was incredible. We continued the internships and work experience. We added Universal Music to that with internships and work experience that we were offering.”

Farinto explains bringing brands on board to support the free-entry event can sometimes be “a hard job”, but his message to brands looking to live music for their social value objectives is that while a wide breadth of projects can be easy, “getting depth right in the right way can be very difficult”.

“The message, the process, what we’re doing in relation to live music, coupling that with access to culture and sports, coupling that with employability — if they wrap their hands around that as a brand, then there’s something really meaningful in that for them,” he says.
