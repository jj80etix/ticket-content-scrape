# THE ARCHITECTS: Pino Sagliocco, part one

## Summary

Part one of an IQ Magazine profile on veteran Spanish promoter Pino Sagliocco, now president of Live Nation Spain, tracing his rise from a working-class Italian childhood to shaping Spain's live-music scene, including his current Shakira Madrid residency at the newly built Macondo Park and plans for a music district near Atlético de Madrid's stadium.

### Key Points
- Profile of Pino Sagliocco, president of Live Nation Spain, covering his career arc and artist-centric promoting philosophy.
- Currently producing a 12-night Shakira residency at Macondo Park, Madrid.
- Developing plans for a "music district" (venues, studios, hospitality) near Atlético de Madrid's stadium.

## Transcript

In part one of IQ's profile of a true maverick, Pino Sagliocco rose from life on the streets of Italy to become the promoter who put Spain on the global touring map

![](https://www.iqmagazine.com/wp-content/smush-webp/2026/07/HERO-SHOT-PINO-S_3688-copia-scaled.jpg.webp)

Pino Sagliocco

The story of one of the most creative promoters of our time begins with a confident nine-year-old attempting to blag a cigarette from soldiers at the NATO base near a small village near Naples in Italy.

“We were a little bit rebellious; we wanted to smoke,” says Pino Sagliocco – then an Italian scamp pestering American servicemen for ciggies, now an elder statesman of the Spanish live business, in sunhat and chunky jewellery, speaking to IQ from a shady terrace in the south of France.

The moment he is recalling took place in 1968, so you might be able to imagine the mysterious and enticing countercultural tunes wafting out of the radio of the sentry post.

“I was over there asking for a cigarette, and the guy on the gate was listening to Jimi Hendrix, Bob Dylan, The Stones – all this kind of music, which for me was completely new. I remember I fell very much in love with Jimi Hendrix.”

Equally enthralling, to an energetic child who was already feeling like he didn’t particularly belong in his rural Italian setting, was a photo of The Stones shown to him by the friendly GI.

“I saw Brian Jones, dressed up in this velvet jacket, the Ray- Bans, and stuff. I said, ‘I want that. What can I do to get that?’”

And so the young Pino Sagliocco did his first music-related deal. “In my village, we had this special type of prestigious little mushroom that you get at the top of a tree. And this guy loved them. He said, ‘Okay, if you get me those mushrooms, I will get you those clothes.’

“After some very hard work and negotiations, days later I was wearing the Ray-Bans, velvet and jeans like Brian Jones.” If there’s a touch of magical realism (a bit of Colombian literary icon Gabriel Garcia Márquez) in this tale, it may be relevant that the project about which 67-year-old Sagliocco is most excited on this May morning, roughly 58 years on, is a 12-night Madrid residency by returning Colombian icon Shakira.

The shows will take place in September and October in Macondo Park – a temporary stadium and immersive experience on the southern edge of Madrid, which takes its name from the village in which Márquez’s Nobel Prize-winning masterpiece One Hundred Years of Solitude was set. Sagliocco barely pauses for breath as he describes what he is building over there.

“Macondo Park is an expression of art,” he says. “You have art, music, gastronomy, dance, cinema, culture, everything. And we made the residency of Shakira, sold 12 nights, and could even have sold more if we wanted to, because the demand is massive. Why? Because it’s more than just a concert. It is Macondo Park. It is that world where we all dream of belonging, that world we all want to be part of.”

**Magical realism  
**In the story of the nine-year-old swapping mushrooms for hippy gear, and in the veteran promoter’s Márquez-inspired plan for mass musical transcendence, we find the essence of Sagliocco, then and now: restlessly creative, implausibly energetic, full of seemingly far-fetched plans, and ever eager to give them a go.

Today, Sagliocco serves as president of Live Nation Spain – a living industry legend who continues to help shape one of Europe’s most vibrant markets while keeping his own creative dream alive. In a business with plenty of characters, Sagliocco stands tall – a free-thinking rebel who loves artists so wholeheartedly that they inevitably love him back.

Speaking recently with Spanish newspaper La Provincia, he summed up the origins of his artist-centric approach as a necessary tool in a young promoter’s arsenal.

“At that time, when what was popular was glamour, my weapon was closeness, personal treatment. I took \[artists\] to lunch and dinner. I chatted with them. I always prioritised that the artist was well cared for, that they did not lack anything \[…\] because my goal was for them to trust me, and for that, I had to prove it.”

Depeche Mode’s manager, Jonathan Kessler, a friend and colleague for more than 30 years, takes exception to the suggestion that Sagliocco is just another great promoter.

“He’s the greatest!” he insists. “Not only because of his professionalism and the incredible work he’s built over the years, which speaks for itself, but also because of the way he treats artists and managers. He genuinely cares about every single artist he works with. He always goes out of his way to make us feel welcome, cared for, and heard. He has a warmth and sincerity that people immediately respond to, and of course, he is so eccentric. One of a kind.”

> “At that time, when what was popular was glamour, my weapon was closeness, personal treatment”

Today, in addition to the Shakira residency, Sagliocco wants to share his vision for the newly green-lit 21,000-cap arena near to Club Atlético de Madrid’s Riyadh Air Metropolitano stadium. “\[Atlético CEO\] Miguel Ángel Gil is a very good friend of mine, and I said, ‘Why don’t we make a music district?’” says Sagliocco, amid a torrent of details about plans for restaurants, recording studios, beach clubs, and gym compounds.

In conversation, Sagliocco is a firehose of plans and projects, memories and opinions, firmly objecting to any notion that he might be edging towards retirement.

“I am still 18! I am younger than my son!” says the famous family man. “I don’t think I have ever worked harder in my life than I do now. I think the business is bigger than it has ever been before. And I also believe that this is the time for people like me \[not to retire yet\], because I think the world needs some real Mohicans. The true ones. I strongly believe that music is still the only opportunity to find a place where we all belong. Because the world is fucked up.”

Sagliocco’s idealistic spirit has been a recurring theme throughout his almost 50 years in the business, governing many of his career’s twists and turns and watershed moments. Blame it, perhaps, on the era in which he came of age, as a young runaway in Italy in the 1970s.

Having left home before his 13th birthday – “My father said, ‘Okay, either I kill you or you go away’” – Sagliocco spent his teens on the streets, working on building sites and in markets before an intellectual girlfriend introduced him to literature and student politics.

“I was in Milan, close to the end of the 70s, and there were university strikes and a lot of revolutionary things happening at that time. And people were talking a lot, but they were not doing anything. So, I started making things happen. And suddenly people start saying, ‘Who is this Pino Sagliocco?’

“I believe we cannot see everything as \[being about\] money. Money is a consequence if you do things right”

“I built my reputation by doing stuff, organising little concerts, pop-up shows, street theatre. And I became a leader in the student movement, even though I didn’t really belong in it, because I’d never had a chance to go to university. My university was the street.”

It might sound like youthful fun, but these were heady times to be flirting with leftist Italian politics. In 1978, former prime minister Aldo Moro was kidnapped and murdered by Marxist– Leninist militant group the Red Brigades, and the mood very quickly changed.

“That was the moment when it all fell apart. And I met some people from Barcelona, and they said, ‘Pino, come to Barcelona. It is amazing.’ I said to my girlfriend, ‘You know, I think it’s time for me to change my life.’ I was 18-and-a-half, not even 19 years old. And I took the train and went to Barcelona.”

**Like a jewel in the sun  
**This is how an Italian began his journey to becoming arguably the defining figure in the Spanish live business. Immersed in Barcelona’s alternative scene, he put on his first international show, starring French Celtic harpist Alan Stivell, at the historic Basílica de Santa María del Mar.

“It was a really big concert. And on that day, I remember Gay Mercader, who was the big promoter in Spain, put on Elvis Costello in another place. But everyone came to my show.”

A spell in Edinburgh came next, learning English and clocking the emerging new wave scene. Back in Spain, he booked ornery American legends, including Chuck Berry, Ray Charles, and James Brown – “The hardest time ever, not easy guys, but they helped me build up my ‘muscles.’”

> “I don’t think I have ever worked harder in my life than I do now”

But it was when Sagliocco made his first visit to Ibiza that he found another pivot point in his life. “In Ibiza, I saw this amazing island, this beautiful island, and the Ku Club had just opened – one of the most incredible clubs on the planet. And I became the booker there, bringing shows to Ibiza.”

Sagliocco’s Ibiza connection probably justifies a retrospective all of its own, as he based himself out of Pikes Hotel, famous as the inspiration for Wham!’s Club Tropicana and for much 1980s island hedonism. But he had mainland plans, too.

“I started \[putting on\] shows at Studio 54 Barcelona, Sala Morasol in Madrid, Arena in Valencia. Those discotheques in Spain became the circuit for the new wave bands – ABC, Culture Club, Spandau Ballet, Duran Duran, Soft Cell, China Crisis, Simple Minds – who became the next big thing. The club owners gave me the opportunity to programme at their place because the bands I was getting in were bringing tremendous promotion.”

**It’s a kind of magic  
**In 1984 came another significant twist, when Sagliocco met Queen drummer Roger Taylor in Ibiza at a propitious moment. “By then, I was a really well-known promoter on the discotheque circuit. And remember, all those things were not easy. I mean, I’m still a guy who’s from nothing, but I’d built a reputation. The bands I was promoting (Depeche Mode, Spandau Ballet, Sade, Tina Turner) were becoming bigger and bigger.

“But of course, I wanted to make a jump to the next step, to the next level. And Queen was a turning point in my life, where you get an opportunity to get into a different world.”

On the strength of his friendship with Taylor, Sagliocco found himself invited to join the band at Live Aid. Queen were at a turning point of their own, dabbling in solo projects and troubled by internal tensions. When their performance stole the show, and thoughts rapidly turned to a new album and tour, Sagliocco found himself nominated as the band’s new Spanish promoter.

“I met \[Queen manager\] Jim Beach, and we became great friends. I had a great respect for him, and I think he’s also one of the most respectful managers I’ve ever known, someone who embraced me and supported me.”
