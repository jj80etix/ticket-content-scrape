# Jim King on building Roundhay into AEG’s next flagship festival

## Summary

AEG Presents' UK/Europe festivals CEO Jim King discusses the debut of Roundhay Festival in Leeds, which drew 100,000 fans over three days and exceeded year-one expectations. King outlines a deliberately cautious, artist-led growth strategy — capped at six event days — and credits close partnership with Leeds City Council for the launch's success. The festival extends AEG's UK portfolio alongside BST Hyde Park, giving touring artists a second major UK date.

### Key Points
- Roundhay Festival (3-5 July) drew ~100,000 fans headlined by Pitbull, Lewis Capaldi and Hallé Orchestra.
- Approved for up to six event days and 70,000 capacity; King plans deliberately slow, artist-driven scaling.
- Positioned as a complementary "second UK date" alongside AEG's BST Hyde Park for touring artists.
- Strategy prioritizes originating events over acquisition-led portfolio growth (vs. acquired Rock en Seine/We Love Green).
- Local-authority partnership (Leeds City Council) cited as critical to the successful launch.

### Highlights
- 100,000 attendees across a 3-day debut, exceeding organizer projections.
- Festival capped at a max of six days/70,000 cap by permit, but AEG is pacing growth deliberately rather than maximizing show count immediately.

### Industry Problem
Launching a new large-scale festival requires simultaneously selling the event, the venue, and the artist experience — a compounded go-to-market challenge for promoters/venues entering a new market, distinct from selling an established stadium/arena date.

### Proposed Solution
AEG's answer is disciplined, relationship-led growth (local authority partnership, artist-first booking, patient scaling) rather than rapid expansion; for a primary ticketing platform, this reinforces the value of deep client-success support for venues/promoters launching new events (onboarding, demand forecasting, capacity planning) rather than a pure self-serve product.

## Jim King on building Roundhay into AEG’s next flagship festival

After drawing 100,000 fans across its three-day debut, the festival boss details its long-term growth strategy, in a discussion with IQ

![Jim King](https://www.iqmagazine.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/01/Jim-King.png)

Jim King

Off the back of Roundhay Festival’s debut edition in Leeds, AEG Presents’ Jim King discusses the strategic role of the Northern England event, its long-term growth plans, and how it bolsters the company’s growing festival portfolio, in a wide-ranging discussion with *IQ*.

Approximately 100,000 fans descended on the UK’s Roundhay Park from 3-5 July to see headliners Pitbull, Lewis Capaldi, and Hallé Orchestra during the first edition of [American Express presents Roundhay Festival](https://www.iqmagazine.com/2025/09/aeg-presents-to-launch-major-new-uk-festival/), giving organisers a strong foundation for the series’ long-term development.

“That 100,000 across those three days is a fantastic achievement for us, and exceeded where we thought we would be year one,” Jim King, CEO of UK and European festivals, tells *IQ*.

“Launching a new event is always difficult. You’re selling the event, you’re selling the venue, and you’re selling the lineup. Sometimes, if you’re taking an artist into a stadium or arena, you’re selling the artist experience from that side, so it becomes more challenging when you’re outdoors and selling these elements as well. For us to achieve those results was really, really fantastic,” he says.

The multi-day event is the latest addition to AEG Presents’ festival portfolio, which includes [BST Hyde Park](https://www.iqmagazine.com/2026/06/bst-hyde-park-expansion-highlights-londons-growing-festival-debate/), All Points East, Forwards, and newer entrant LIDO in the UK, alongside Rock en Seine and We Love Green in France. Roundhay Park’s natural amphitheatre bowl has a rich musical history, spanning shows from The Rolling Stones (1982), Genesis and Madonna (1987), Michael Jackson (1988), and U2 (1997).

For AEG, bringing a festival to the famed site was part of a strategic expansion of the company’s UK portfolio, giving touring artists a major date up north in addition to BST Hyde Park in London.

“We’ve always wanted to have an event that sat alongside BST Hyde Park, as the artist coming into the marketplace usually wants to play more than one show,” he explains. “What we wanted to do was give a show in the north to be able to offer those artists – like Lewis Capaldi and Pitbull this year – the opportunity for two shows.

> “We’re quite cautious with all of our growth plans”

“Really, the underlying strategy of it is to service the artists, and I’ve always wanted to do a show there, so it was about finding the right moment for that to take place. It sits in that complementary growth plan around BST and around our UK portfolio of events.”

Its location is also designed to open up a market that is not often part of the UK’s major outdoor touring circuit.

“Leeds is not a traditional stadium market, with the greatest respect to Elland Road,” he explains. “That was appealing to us to allow artists to come in and play the market, knowing that next year or the year after, they may be coming in to do a stadium tour. It gives us a greater opportunity to bring even bigger artists around Roundhay Festival, which is ultimately what we want to achieve.”

While Roundhay has permission to stage up to six event days, with the park’s total capacity approved up to 70,000 people, King says time will tell how the event will scale.

“We see its growth going from three days to four days. There’s a maximum of six that we would do there, but we’re in no rush. It’s about sustainable growth. With all of our events, we’re not dictated by show count; we’re dictated by doing the right things for us, and that is making sure we work with the biggest artists in the world and bringing those artists to Leeds. If we have six of the biggest artists next year, we’ll do six shows. If we have four, we’ll do four,” he says.

That measured approach reflects the company’s wider philosophy around the festival business.

“We’re quite cautious with all of our growth plans,” King shares. “We like originating events. That’s my background, and that’s what the company largely prefers to do,” he says, pointing to most of the portfolio outside of the acquired Rock en Seine and [We Love Green](https://www.iqmagazine.com/2025/07/aeg-presents-groupe-combat-we-love-green/) in Paris.

![](https://www.iqmagazine.com/wp-content/smush-webp/2026/07/Pitbull-at-Roundhay-Festival-©-Josette-Crispin-430x269.png.webp)

Pitbull at Roundhay © Josette Crispin

“The timing is everything. And the question is, where do we want to be? And why do we want to be there? It’s not just a case of us accumulating a vast number of events to package them up. That’s just not our plan. Our plan is sustainable growth around the key hubs of activity we have. They’re all strategically positioned that way to support each other.”

He gives a large amount of credit for the launch edition’s success to support from Leeds City Council, which partnered with AEG on the event.

“These things are always best when they’re operating under partnership, and the local authority is one of the most important partners you’re ever going to have, alongside the community and that wider stakeholder group. They were very knowledgeable and very welcoming local authorities. It makes a huge difference,” he says.

“From the outset, we could see that we were working with a very motivated, knowledgeable, and local authority who were extremely welcoming to us for us to put down roots in Leeds, and hopefully be there for a very long time, which is what our ambition is,” King adds.

Sustainability was another major focus during Roundhay’s first year, particularly around transport. Organisers worked to provide a shuttle bus system to help alleviate the ecological impact of a large-scale event like Roundhay.

“Most event producers and sustainability consultants will all agree that sustainability starts with transportation, and the highest impact points are the fans’ travel to and from an event. We couldn’t be happier with the number of people that we moved on shuttle buses, and that’s really the first step,” he says.

> “It will be an even better, bigger, stronger, higher-value Roundhay than anyone saw this year”

Many of the initiatives deployed during Roundhay were adapted from practices established across AEG’s wider festival portfolio, like that of the green-focused [LIDO](https://www.iqmagazine.com/featured-list/green-guardians-2025/lido/).

“We use a local supply chain as much as we can to reduce production miles and food miles around everything,” he says. “We applied a lot of learnings from LIDO Festival, where our recycling rates are around 89%, which I think is probably the highest in the country for any event of that type.

“We only use HVO fuel. We’re backing that up with battery power as well, so we use a lot of battery power and a very, very low charge from HVO fuel generators going into the batteries, which again reduces emissions considerably. This is a lot of learning which we took from the [Act 1.5 show we did with Massive Attack](https://www.iqmagazine.com/2024/09/agf-claire-oneill-new-dawn-sustainable-events/), took that into LIDO, and now we’re seeing where we can apply those similar principles to the rest of our event portfolio,” he explains.

Even with this year’s edition not far in the rearview, planning has already begun for next year’s event, with plans to potentially expand from two to three stages.

“Overall, we just want to keep improving the customer experience. The way that people arrive, what they can do on the day, and the quality of what they see in front of them are always our main goals. We’ll come back, and it will be an even better, bigger, stronger, higher-value Roundhay than anyone saw this year,” he says.

King adds that the festival also quickly established itself on artists’ radar, achieving the goal of creating a pipeline between BST and Roundhay.

“The conversations that we’re having with artists now, it’s on their radar, which is one of the most important things. You want to make sure you grab attention in the artist community, and I think we’ve achieved that. There are a number of artists next year who are looking to play BST Hyde Park and are already discussing playing Roundhay,” he shares.

> “We’re certainly not resting on our laurels”

Single-day ticket prices for this year’s edition started at £70 (€82) for general admission, rising above £140 (€175) for VIP packages. As audiences’ ticket [price sensitivity shifts](https://www.iqmagazine.com/2026/07/gen-zs-price-sensitivity-declining-despite-concert-costs-rising/), King says pricing is ultimately about value for money rather than simply the cost of admission.

“However long you’ve been doing it, at what price point are we going to be successful here? I always try to flip this other way and say, at what value point are we going to be successful?” he says.

“We’ll look at what we feel are the most important elements for artists to enjoy and deliver their art in the best possible way, and then we don’t mess up the fan experience, then we’ve got everyone in a really good mood. We know there’s a ceiling on price and that value expectation needs to meet that price point. It’s more of a concentration on value and the price reflecting that,” King says.

With attention turned to next year, amidst a bumper season across the rest of the AEG festival portfolio, King says this is only the beginning for what the team can achieve with Roundhay.

“We’re certainly not resting on our laurels. Anyone who went this year and comes next will see an even better Roundhay – that’s always the plan,” he concludes.
