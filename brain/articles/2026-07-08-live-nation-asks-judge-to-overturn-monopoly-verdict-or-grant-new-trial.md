# Live Nation asks judge to overturn monopoly verdict, or grant new trial

## Summary

Live Nation and Ticketmaster have formally asked US District Judge Arun Subramanian to overturn April's jury verdict that found the companies held an illegal monopoly in live event ticketing, or, failing that, to grant a new trial. The final written arguments, filed July 2 in the Southern District of New York, come after state attorneys general urged the judge to order a breakup of the companies; Live Nation counters that plaintiffs failed to prove its conduct raised prices and that no venue was forced into an exclusive primary-ticketing contract. The motions are scheduled to be argued at a hearing later this month.

### Key Points
- Live Nation/Ticketmaster filed a reply memorandum (July 2) supporting a Rule 50(b) motion for judgment as a matter of law to overturn the verdict on every claim, plus a fallback Rule 59 motion for a new trial, both originally filed May 21.
- The April 15 verdict followed a trial (started March 2026) brought by a coalition of 33 states and DC, after the DOJ and several original states settled separately with Live Nation.
- Live Nation argues plaintiffs didn't prove its conduct raised prices, citing evidence that artists earned 18% more at large amphitheatres than arenas, and disputes claims that venues were forced into exclusive primary-ticketing contracts.
- Live Nation's new-trial argument claims the jury was swayed by "out-of-market" evidence (e.g., European fee comparisons) and "stale" evidence/rumors from the states.
- State AGs had previously told the judge the companies should be broken up.
- Judge Subramanian has not yet ruled; a hearing on the motions is expected later this month (July 2026).

### Highlights
- Post-trial motions filed May 21, 2026; final reply memorandum filed July 2, 2026, ahead of a hearing later this month.
- Original DOJ + 40-state antitrust suit filed 2024; DOJ and some states settled, but 33 states + DC proceeded to trial, resulting in the April 15, 2026 monopoly verdict.
- Live Nation seeks judgment as a matter of law (Rule 50(b)) on all claims, or a new trial (Rule 59), against a jury finding of illegal monopoly.
- State AGs are on record asking the judge to order a structural breakup of Live Nation-Ticketmaster.

### Industry Problem
This is the central antitrust/deceptive-pricing case shaping the competitive landscape for independent primary ticketing platforms (Q10): whether Live Nation-Ticketmaster's dominant, vertically integrated position (promotion, venues, primary ticketing) constitutes an illegal monopoly, and whether a breakup or structural remedy could open real market share to independents like Etix.

### Proposed Solution
There is no product solution here — this is pure litigation posture from Live Nation seeking to nullify the jury's verdict. Etix's stance should be to monitor the post-trial ruling and hearing timeline closely, since a denial of Live Nation's motions (or a breakup remedy) is the key trigger event that would materially reshape competitive opportunity for independent platforms and warrant a strategic response.

**Live Nation and subsidiary Ticketmaster have asked a federal court to throw out April’s verdict that ruled the companies held an illegal monopoly in the industry, and should this fail, the pair have also requested US District Judge Arun Subramanian grant a new trial.**

First reported by [*Music Business Worldwide*](https://www.musicbusinessworldwide.com/live-nation-and-ticketmaster-ask-court-to-throw-out-monopoly-verdict-or-grant-new-trial/), Live Nation and Ticketmaster set out their final written arguments in documents [filed last week](https://www.musicbusinessworldwide.com/files/2026/07/REPLY-MEMORANDUM-OF-LAW-IN-SUPPORT-OF-DEFENDANTS-MOTION-FOR-JUDGMENT-AS-A-MATTER-OF-LAW-PURSUANT-TO-FEDERAL-RULE-OF-CIVIL-PROCEDURE-50B-1-1.pdf) (July 2), before the judge rules on two post-trial motions, which were filed on May 21 in the US District Court for the Southern District of New York.

After US state attorneys general told Judge Subramanian that Live Nation-Ticketmaster [must be broken up](https://www.theticketingbusiness.com/2026/05/us-states-tell-judge-live-nation-ticketmaster-should-be-broken-up/), the pair argued to overturn the monopoly verdict. They argued that the evidence presented during the antitrust trial was not enough for the jury to reach the conclusion they did.

In one of Live Nation’s motions brought under Rule 50 (b) of the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure, lawyers asked the court to enter judgement as a matter of law for the companies on every claim, which would overrule the jury. Under Rule 59, lawyers also argued for a new trial if the first motion failed.

Key [arguments](https://www.hypebot.com/live-nations-demands-judge-overturn-monopoly-jury-verdict/) for overturning the verdict include Live Nation claiming plaintiffs failed to prove the company’s conduct really did result in higher prices, and the defence highlighted evidence that acknowledged artists made 18% more money playing at large amphitheatres than at arenas.

Additionally, Live Nation claimed that there was no evidence that any venue was forced into an exclusive primary ticketing contract.

Should Judge Subramanian decline to overturn the verdict, Live Nation has requested a new trial, arguing that April’s decision was “against the weight of the evidence”. Lawyers argued the jury was swayed by “out-of-market” evidence (such as comparisons to fees in Europe), and that the states relied on “stale” evidence and rumours.

Judge Subramanian has not yet ruled on any of the motions, which are due to be argued at a hearing later this month.

**A landmark case**

The [April 15 verdict](https://www.theticketingbusiness.com/2026/04/jury-rules-live-nation-ticketmaster-held-monopoly-in-landmark-case/) was made following an antitrust trial that began in early March, brought by a coalition of 33 states and the District of Columbia.

State attorneys general [opted to continue](https://www.theticketingbusiness.com/2026/03/live-nation-trial-to-resume-after-states-reject-doj-settlement/) the trial after the US Department of Justice (DOJ) and several of the original states reached a settlement with Live Nation Entertainment.

The DOJ and 40 state attorneys general had initially filed the antitrust lawsuit against Live Nation-Ticketmaster [in 2024](https://www.theticketingbusiness.com/2024/05/live-nation-ticketmaster-responds-to-doj-antitrust-lawsuit/).

In response to the jury’s verdict, Live Nation said in a statement: “The jury’s verdict is not the last word on this matter. Pending motions will determine whether the liability and damages rulings stand.”
