# World Cup Quarterfinal Ticket Prices Plummet as Host Nations Exit

## Summary

World Cup quarterfinal ticket prices have dropped sharply — a 54% median-price decline from June 30 — as host nations USA, Mexico and Canada were eliminated along with other major demand drivers, though get-in prices for every remaining quarterfinal still sit near or above $1,000. The price reset comes as StubHub faces a Texas AG investigation into "ghost ticketing" and a proposed class-action lawsuit over canceled/non-delivered World Cup resale tickets, with StubHub blaming FIFA's ticketing infrastructure for the failures.

### Highlights
- Quarterfinal median ticket price fell to $2,190 from $4,794 (June 30), a 54% drop; get-in prices remain $977-$1,923 per match.
- Texas AG Ken Paxton opened an investigation into StubHub over reported "ghost ticketing" — canceled orders and non-delivered tickets in host cities including Dallas and Houston.
- A proposed class-action was filed June 30 in SDNY alleging StubHub misled World Cup ticket buyers about fulfillment.
- StubHub attributes delivery failures to FIFA's mobile-ticketing/transfer system; FIFA disputes this and is steering fans to its own resale marketplace.

### Industry Problem
Resale-market non-delivery and "ghost ticketing" at a high-profile mega-event is generating direct regulatory and legal exposure for the marketplace involved, and highlights the patron-trust risk when secondary-market fulfillment isn't guaranteed at point of sale.

### Proposed Solution
This is a cautionary case study rather than a solution the article proposes; for a primary platform like Etix, the takeaway is to keep resale/transfer guarantees airtight and delivery-verified at time of purchase, and to monitor how state AGs (beyond Texas) treat "ghost ticketing" claims as a template for future enforcement risk.

### Key Points
- Quarterfinal pricing down 54% since June 30 as host nations were eliminated; still $1,000+ get-in across all four matches.
- Texas AG investigating StubHub over ticket-delivery failures/"ghost ticketing."
- Proposed SDNY class action accuses StubHub of failing to deliver purchased World Cup tickets.
- Dispute over responsibility: StubHub blames FIFA's ticketing system; FIFA disputes this.

But the ticket market has undergone a major reset as the bracket has clarified.

With the United States, Mexico and Canada all eliminated, and with other major demand drivers including Brazil, Portugal and Colombia also out, World Cup quarterfinal ticket prices have fallen sharply from the speculative levels seen earlier in knockout play. [According to pricing data from TicketClub](https://www.ticketclub.com/blog/world-cup-quarterfinal-ticket-prices-where-fans-can-find-value-as-the-final-eight-prepare/), the median price for quarterfinal tickets has dropped to $2,190, down from $4,794 on June 30 — a decline of 54%.

France-Morocco is currently the lowest-priced quarterfinal, with a $977 get-in price and a $1,675 median price. The match still offers a compelling storyline, with France looking like the most complete team left in the field and Morocco again pushing deep into the tournament after its 2022 semifinal run.

Spain-Belgium at SoFi Stadium has also softened substantially, with a $1,112 get-in price and $1,739 median. That game once carried the possibility of featuring the United States or Cristiano Ronaldo’s Portugal in Los Angeles. Instead, it becomes a major European quarterfinal, but one without the local-host demand or Ronaldo-driven premium that had been priced into earlier expectations.

Norway-England is now the most expensive quarterfinal by median price, with TicketClub showing a $1,923 get-in price and a $3,161 median at Miami Stadium. That market appears to be holding up better than the others, driven by England’s traveling support and Norway’s star power behind Haaland, who has become one of the tournament’s defining figures.

Argentina-Switzerland sits second among the quarterfinals by median price, with a $1,370 get-in and $2,306 median at Kansas City Stadium. Argentina’s market continues to be supported by Messi and by the defending champions’ dramatic path into the final eight, though the matchup is still significantly less expensive than earlier speculative pricing for that quarterfinal slot.

The same downward pressure is showing elsewhere in the tournament, though not as sharply. TicketClub’s current snapshot shows semifinal median prices down 34% from June 30, the Third Place match down 28%, and the Final down 16%. The Final remains the stickiest market by far, with a current median of $13,699 in TicketClub’s data.

The current picture is a reminder that late-stage World Cup ticket pricing is not driven only by the round or venue. It is driven by the specific teams, the travel profile of their fan bases, the likelihood of local demand, the star players involved, and the difference between upper-level inventory and premium seating. In several quarterfinals, upper-deck prices have softened while lower-bowl, club, suite and VIP inventory still carries a substantial premium.

The price movement also comes as World Cup ticketing remains under intense scrutiny, particularly around StubHub and ticket delivery failures that have shadowed the tournament for weeks.

[Texas Attorney General Ken Paxton announced last week](https://www.texasattorneygeneral.gov/news/releases/attorney-general-ken-paxton-investigates-stubhub-failing-provide-fifa-world-cup-tickets-fans-have) that his office had opened an investigation into StubHub over reports that fans purchased World Cup tickets through the resale marketplace only to have orders canceled or fail to arrive before matches. The Texas AG’s office said fans in host cities including Dallas and Houston had reported cancellations days or even hours before kickoff, and raised the possibility of “ghost ticketing,” in which sellers list tickets they do not actually possess and later cancel when they cannot deliver.

“My office is investigating reports that StubHub is failing to deliver tickets that Texas fans have rightfully purchased,” Paxton said in the announcement. “In many cases, attending a World Cup match is a once-in-a-lifetime experience. If StubHub is ghost ticketing Texans out of that experience, my office will use every tool available to hold them accountable and help fans who have been wronged.”

The investigation adds to StubHub’s legal and public-relations problems around the tournament. As TicketNews reported last week, StubHub is already facing a proposed class-action lawsuit filed June 30 in the U.S. District Court for the Southern District of New York. That complaint accuses the company of misleading consumers who purchased World Cup tickets on the secondary market and then “did not get what they paid for” when tickets were not delivered as promised.

StubHub has defended its broader handling of the tournament, arguing that many of the delivery issues stem from FIFA’s ticketing infrastructure, including its mobile-ticketing system, transfer restrictions and acceptance process. The company has also pointed to its FanProtect Guarantee, saying buyers are entitled to replacement tickets or a refund when problems arise. FIFA, meanwhile, has pushed fans toward its own official resale marketplace and has disputed StubHub’s suggestion that FIFA’s systems are primarily responsible for the failures.

For buyers, the result is a complicated market. Prices are clearly down from earlier knockout-stage levels, and the absence of the three host nations has removed a major source of demand. But World Cup tickets remain expensive, price movement can be sudden, and fulfillment risk has become part of the story in a way that goes beyond normal resale-market volatility.

As the quarterfinals begin, the central ticketing question is no longer whether the World Cup is expensive. It still is. The more important question is where the market has finally become more rational — and whether buyers can trust that the tickets they purchase will actually get them through the gate.
