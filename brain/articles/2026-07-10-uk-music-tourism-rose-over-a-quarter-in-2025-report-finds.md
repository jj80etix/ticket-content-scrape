# UK music tourism rose over a quarter in 2025, report finds

## Summary

A companion IQ Magazine report on the same UK Music data details a 26.8% year-over-year rise in "music tourists" (long-distance travelers) attending UK live music events in 2025, driven by Oasis, Beyoncé, Dua Lipa, Coldplay and other major tours, with overseas visitor trips up over a quarter. UK Music's CEO again pressed the government on anti-touting reform and post-Brexit EU touring costs.

### Key Points
- 26.8% YoY rise in long-distance UK music tourist trips; record 24.7m total music tourists in 2025.
- Oasis Live '25 alone lifted total tourist numbers 4.8% YoY (23.5m→24.7m).
- Direct spend by music tourists: £5.7bn; total economic impact incl. indirect spend: £11.2bn (+11.3%).
- Overseas visitor trips rose over 25% YoY (to 2.1m).
- UK Music renewed calls for anti-touting legislation and EU touring-cost relief for UK artists.

### Highlights
- Direct music-tourism spend of £5.7bn plus £5.5bn in indirect value-chain spend, underscoring the sector's economic-lobbying weight.
- Renewed explicit call to revive the UK's stalled anti-touting "manifesto pledge," keeping resale/price-cap policy live.

### Industry Problem
Same dynamic as the companion article: strong demand growth is politically leveraged by advocates to push stalled resale/touting regulation, which a primary ticketing platform should track as a possible compliance trigger.

### Proposed Solution
Monitor UK anti-touting legislative developments (previously "backpedaled" per referenced IQ reporting) for a potential resale-cap or transferability mandate that would affect any UK-facing resale integrations.

## Transcript

UK Music has found 26.8% more people travelled long distances to attend the country's live music events compared to 2024

![](https://www.iqmagazine.com/wp-content/smush-webp/2026/07/Oasis-@-Heaton-Park-SJM-Concerts-Copyright-Big-Brother-Recordings-scaled.jpg.webp)

Oasis live at Heaton Park © Big Brother Recordings

The UK saw its music tourism rise by more than a quarter in 2025 compared to the year before, as the country’s live sector attracted a record 24.7 million music tourists to concerts and festivals, a new report from UK Music has found.

UK Music cited high-profile live events from artists including Oasis, Beyoncé, Dua Lipa, Coldplay, Lana Del Rey, and Kendrick Lamar as drivers of the 26.8% rise in people travelling long distances to attend concerts and festivals, which also contributed to a jump in the sector’s overall economic impact.

The report defines a music tourist as either someone who travelled from outside the UK to attend a live music event in the UK, or someone who travelled from within the UK to see live music events, provided they travelled more than three times an average commute for that region.

Chief among the live events that drove these numbers was the highly anticipated Oasis Live ’25 Tour, whose 41-date run boosted the total number of music tourists in the UK by 4.8% from 23.5 million in 2024 to the new record of 24.7 million last year, the report found.

“A series of Oasis gigs – including five at Manchester’s Heaton Park, seven at Wembley Stadium and two at Cardiff’s Principality Stadium – saw revenues surge across the UK. In the North West, music tourists’ spending rose 15.6% from £1.2 billion in 2024 to £1.4 billion in 2025,” UK Music said.

Meanwhile, music tourism spending in London alone increased by 27.4% from £2.7 billion in 2024 to £3.4 billion last year.

The economic impact of music tourism in the UK also rose, with spending by those making trips for live music rising 11.3% compared to 2024. There was also a 3% rise in the number of jobs supported by music tourists’ trips.

> “With the right support, we can help the UK music industry continue to boost the economy”

“Music tourism spending is made up of £5.7 billion spent directly by music tourists attending concerts and festivals in the UK, including the cost of a ticket, on-site spend, travel, accommodation, and meals while travelling to events,” UK Music said.

However, the 11.3% rise in economic impact also includes a further £5.5 billion which was spent “indirectly through the value chain”, including costs such as fencing and security or a restaurant paying for ingredients.

UK Music chief executive Tom Kiehl said the money spent through music tourism is “a huge shot in the arm for towns and cities right across the UK”, naming hotels, restaurants, bars, and transport firms among the main beneficiaries.

Domestic music tourist trips also rose 3.2%, as 22.6 million people came from elsewhere in the UK to see live music events, the report found. This means 2.1 million visitors came from overseas to take part in the UK’s live sector, which represents an increase of over a quarter year-on-year.

UK Music’s Kiehl used the launch of the report to reiterate calls for greater government support for the sector, saying Westminster must “support music fans by delivering on their [manifesto pledge to tackle the menace of ticket touts](https://www.iqmagazine.com/2026/05/uk-sector-dealt-blow-with-anti-tout-law-backpedal/) “.

He also called for “long overdue” government and EU action to help UK performers and [musicians touring the EU](https://www.iqmagazine.com/2026/06/uk-live-campaigners-call-for-time-bound-eu-touring-talks/) who he said have had to “deal with soaring costs and red tape post-Brexit”.

“We also need to see music’s grassroots protected and nurtured. This includes the artists, venues, festivals, studios and rehearsal rooms that are embedded in our local communities and who are essential to the future prosperity of our world-leading industry. We must make sure that policymakers recognise their vital economic and cultural value and deliver the support they need,” Kiehl said.

“With the right support, we can help the UK music industry continue to boost the economy, create jobs and give music lovers some unforgettable experiences,” the chief executive added.
