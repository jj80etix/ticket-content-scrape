# No sign of letting go: Release Athens’ monumental 10th edition

## Summary

Fuzz Productions' Release Athens festival is marking its 10th anniversary with record attendance, on pace to surpass 300,000 tickets sold (up from 130,000 in 2025) across an 18-day, star-studded lineup at Athens' Plateia Nerou. General manager John Mirabitas discusses the festival's deliberate capacity-management philosophy, a philanthropic discounted-pricing partnership, and its planned move to a larger venue.

### Key Points
- 2026 edition (13 June-25 July) is tracking to exceed 300,000 attendees vs. 130,000 in 2025 (~130% growth), on a 25,000-capacity site.
- Lineup spans Limp Bizkit, Pantera, Megadeth, David Byrne, Gorillaz, Nick Cave, and Jean-Michel Jarre, among others.
- Festival deliberately caps ticket sales below maximum demand to protect on-site experience, prioritizing "long-term trust" over short-term revenue.
- Partnered with the Stavros Niarchos Foundation to price select shows (Byrne, Gorillaz, Jarre) up to 50% lower; two of those shows sold out.
- Mirabitas calls for a national centralized framework in Greece covering live-event safety, operations, and licensing standards.
- Festival is outgrowing Plateia Nerou and finalizing a move to a larger, yet-to-be-announced venue.

## No sign of letting go: Release Athens’ monumental 10th edition

"This anniversary isn't a finish line," Fuzz Productions' John Mirabitas tells IQ amid the Greek festival series' record-breaking year

![The Offspring at Release Athens](https://www.iqmagazine.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/07/The-Offspring-at-Release-Athens.png)

The Offspring at Release Athens

Amid Release Athens’ 10th-anniversary edition, Fuzz Productions tells *IQ* why a record-breaking year represents “a new starting point” for the annual Greek festival as it draws record crowds and eyes a move to a larger venue.

This year, the 25,000-capacity summer series spans 18 days of programming running from 13 June to 25 July, with a headliner pool that ranges from Limp Bizkit, Three Days Grace, Sabaton, Pantera, and Megadeth to David Byrne, Nick Cave, Jean-Michel Jarre, Gorillaz, and Moby.

Although the Fuzz Productions team initially set its sights on hitting the 200,000-ticket mark for the first time in the festival’s history, a blockbuster lineup and strong audience demand have put Release Athens on track to surpass 300,000 attendees – a 130% increase on 2025’s audience of 130,000.

“Ticket sales have been exceptionally strong,” Fuzz’s general manager, John Mirabitas, tells *IQ*. “The festival continues to combine an outstanding on-site experience with the strongest lineup in our history.”

Mirabitas attributes part of this year’s success to the diverse programme that balances both quantity and quality, staged at the scenic waterside Plateia Nerou (Water Square) venue.

“Each festival day has its own identity and is built around fans who genuinely love that particular style of music, rather than trying to appeal to everyone at once,” he says. “That’s why you’ll find artists as different as Nick Cave, Pantera, Pet Shop Boys, The Offspring, and Three Days Grace in the same festival. What connects them isn’t their genre, it’s the quality of the live experience they offer.”

Alongside international stars, Mirabitas adds that platforming Greek artists “has always been part of our philosophy”, nodding to headline shows from hip-hop act Imiskoubria and Pavlos Pavlidis on this year’s calendar.

> “Long-term trust is far more valuable than short-term revenue”

While demand for this year’s edition has been unprecedented, it has also presented a challenge as the festival begins to outgrow its waterfront home.

“Fans don’t compare this year’s edition with last year’s; they compare every show with the best live experience they’ve ever had,” he explains. “One of our biggest challenges this year has been optimising the venue’s capacity while making sure every visitor has the best possible experience. For any promoter, it’s never an easy decision to stop selling tickets for a show that could generate significant additional revenue.

“Once we believe the audience experience could be compromised, we stop. We’d rather leave demand unmet than overcrowd the venue. Long-term trust is far more valuable than short-term revenue, and that’s a principle that guides every decision we make,” he adds.

With attendance on a remarkable trajectory, the team is soaking in what could be its final chapter at Plateia Nerou as it finalises its relocation to a larger site, with the official venue to be announced later this year.

“It will give us much greater flexibility in how we programme the festival and allow us to showcase even more Greek artists, both as support acts and as headliners,” Mirabitas says.

![](https://www.iqmagazine.com/wp-content/smush-webp/2026/07/Release-Athens-430x269.png.webp)

With its audience as a guiding light, Release Athens’ team also partnered with philanthropic organisation the Stavros Niarchos Foundation (SNF), enabling tickets for select performances by David Byrne, Gorillaz and Jean-Michel Jarre to be priced up to 50% lower than usual.

“We are united by shared values, mutual respect and a shared desire to bring more live music to people of all ages,” Mirabitas explains. “The public response was overwhelming,” he says, celebrating two sold-out shows in the mix.

“Accessible pricing certainly played a role, but I don’t think it’s the whole story. We’ve seen plenty of shows in Greece, including our own, sell out at significantly higher ticket prices,” he says. “I don’t believe there’s a single level of price elasticity that applies to the entire market. It depends on many factors, including the artist, how often they perform in Greece, the uniqueness of the show, the target audience, and even the announcement timing.”

It’s clear that Greek audiences are hungry for more. To help meet this demand, Mirabitas says the country needs to implement a centralised framework to help live music organisers at all levels expand their offerings.

“Whether it’s a festival, an outdoor concert or a large indoor production, there should be a common set of standards covering safety, operations and licensing,” he says. “Greece already has outstanding professionals, passionate audiences, and growing demand. A predictable framework would make it easier to invest, plan for the long term and continue raising standards across the entire live music sector.”

More than anything, Release Athens has grown to become an integral part of Greece’s cultural landscape, serving as a common ground for local communities.

> “Our ambition for the next decade is to bring live music to even more people”

“As we look around, we realise that we’ve all grown alongside the festival. Colleagues have got married, started families, and so have many of the people who’ve been coming to Release Athens since the very beginning,” he says.

“That really struck me during Jean-Michel Jarre’s show, when I spotted a friend in the audience sitting with his son, his father and his 90-year-old grandfather. Four generations, all sharing the same live experience. It made me realise that, over the years, Release Athens has become part of people’s lives. Sometimes the most memorable moments aren’t only the ones happening on stage, but the ones taking place in front of it,” Mirabitas remarks.

Over its first decade, the festival has welcomed headliners including Arctic Monkeys, The Prodigy, Kylie Minogue, Massive Attack, Liam Gallagher, Pulp, and Duran Duran. As Fuzz prepares to usher Release Athens into its second decade, the milestone feels more like the start of something new.

“For me, and I think for everyone on the team, there’s an incredible sense of pride and gratitude, mixed with the feeling that those ten years have passed in the blink of an eye,” he says.

“For us, this anniversary isn’t a finish line. It’s a new starting point. With our new venue, we’ll be able to welcome more people than ever before. Our ambition for the next decade is to bring live music to even more people, regardless of age or background, and to create experiences that simply wouldn’t exist without Release Athens,” Mirabitas concludes.
